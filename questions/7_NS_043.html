Question 1Grade 7 • NS
A store sells candy for $0.75 per piece. If Emily has $9.00, how many pieces of candy can she buy?
A. 120 pieces of candy
B. 15 pieces of candy
C. 1.2 pieces of candy
D. 12 pieces of candy
E. None of the above
🔒 Backend review only — not visible to students
Step-by-step solution
1
To find out how many pieces of candy can be bought, divide the total money Emily has by the cost per piece.
The problem asks for how many times a smaller amount (cost per piece) fits into a larger amount (total money), which indicates division.
2
$9.00 ÷ $0.75
This represents the total money divided by the cost of one piece of candy.
3
Move the decimal point in the divisor (0.75) two places to the right to make it 75. Move the decimal point in the dividend (9.00) two places to the right to make it 900.
To divide by a decimal, we typically convert the divisor to a whole number by multiplying both the divisor and the dividend by the same power of 10 (in this case, 100).
4
900 ÷ 75 = 12
Divide 900 by 75 to get the number of pieces of candy.
5
Emily can buy 12 pieces of candy.
The calculation shows that Emily has enough money for 12 pieces.

Answer: 12 pieces of candy (Option D)

E. None of the above
🔒 Backend review only — not visible to students
Step-by-step solution
1
To find out how many runners are needed, divide the total track length by the distance each runner completes.
The problem asks to find how many equal parts (distance per runner) are in a whole (total track length), which implies division.
2
(3/4) ÷ (1/8)
This represents the total length of the track divided by the distance covered by one runner.
3
(3/4) × (8/1)
To divide by a fraction, you multiply by its reciprocal. The reciprocal of 1/8 is 8/1.
4
(3 × 8) / (4 × 1) = 24/4
Multiply the numerators together and the denominators together.
5
24/4 = 6
Divide the numerator by the denominator to get the simplest form.
6
6 runners are needed.
The calculation shows that 6 runners are required to complete the total track length.

D. 2/5 cups

To find the average amount of sugar per cookie, divide the total sugar by the number of cookies.
The problem asks for an average amount per unit, which requires division.

3
20 = 20/1

D. -2.1 degrees Celsius/hour

🔒 Backend review only — not visible to students
Step-by-step solution
1
To find the average hourly change, divide the total temperature change by the time taken. A decrease of 13.65 degrees is represented as -13.65.
Average rate of change is total change divided by time. 'Decreased by' implies a negative value.
2
-13.65 ÷ 6.5
This represents the total temperature change divided by the total time.
3
A negative number divided by a positive number results in a negative number.
The rules for dividing integers apply to rational numbers: opposite signs give a negative result.
4
Move the decimal point in the divisor (6.5) one place to the right to make it 65. Move the decimal point in the dividend (13.65) one place to the right to make it 136.5.
This step simplifies the division process.
5
136.5 ÷ 65 = 2.1
Divide the absolute values of the numbers using long division.
6
Since the result must be negative, the average hourly change is -2.1.
Remember to include the negative sign determined in step 3.
7
The average hourly temperature change was -2.1 degrees Celsius/hour.
The final answer reflects both the magnitude and direction of the temperature change.
