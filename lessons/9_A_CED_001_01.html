Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)03080W0

Hello [Student Name]! Let's break down how to create a linear equation using a real-world scenario. Imagine you're planning a birthday party at a local arcade. The arcade has a special party package: there's a fixed cost for renting the private party room, and then an additional cost for each guest who attends. The private room rental is $75, and each guest costs $15. Our goal is to create a linear equation that represents the total cost, C, for a party with g guests. After we create the equation, we'll find out the total cost if 12 guests attend.

  1. 💡 Hint: Think about what cost stays the same no matter how many guests you have. First, identify the fixed cost. This is the amount you pay just to have the party room, even if no guests show up. In this case, it's the $75 for the private room rental. This will be your y-intercept, or 'b' value, in the equation C = mg + b.
  2. 💡 Hint: Now, consider what cost changes based on the number of guests. How much does each extra person add to the bill? Next, identify the variable cost. This is the cost that changes depending on the number of guests. Each guest costs $15. This is your rate of change, or 'm' value (the slope), because the cost increases by $15 for every additional guest, g.
  3. 💡 Hint: Put these two pieces together into the standard linear equation form, C = mg + b. Now, we can put these values into our linear equation format: C = 15g + 75. Here, C is the total cost, g is the number of guests, $15 is the cost per guest, and $75 is the fixed room rental fee.
  4. 💡 Hint: Replace the variable for guests with the specific number given and calculate. Finally, let's use the equation to find the total cost for 12 guests. Substitute 12 for g: C = 15(12) + 75. Calculate: C = 180 + 75. So, C = $255. The total cost for a party with 12 guests is $255.

Check: If you have 0 guests, the cost is C = 15(0) + 75 = $75, which matches the room rental. If you have 1 guest, C = 15(1) + 75 = $90, which is the room plus one guest. This confirms our equation works as expected.

Standard03080W0

Let's explore creating a linear equation from a straightforward situation, [Student Name]. Imagine you're setting up a small business selling custom-designed t-shirts for your school's spirit week. You have an initial setup cost for your equipment, and then there's a cost for each t-shirt you produce. Your initial equipment cost is $120. Each t-shirt you design and print costs you an additional $8. We want to create a linear equation to represent the total cost (T) of your business based on the number of t-shirts (s) you produce. After creating the equation, we'll figure out your total cost if you produce 25 t-shirts.

  1. Identify Variables: First, we need to know what our changing quantities are. The total cost (T) is what we want to find, so it's our dependent variable. The number of t-shirts produced (s) is what we control and it directly affects the total cost, making it our independent variable.
  2. Determine the Initial Value (Y-intercept): The initial equipment cost of $120 is incurred regardless of how many t-shirts you make. This is your starting point, or the value of T when s is 0. This is the 'b' in the y = mx + b form, so b = 120.
  3. Determine the Rate of Change (Slope): The cost per t-shirt is $8. This is the amount your total cost increases for each additional t-shirt produced. This constant rate of change is your slope, 'm', so m = 8.
  4. Formulate the Equation: Now, combine these parts into a linear equation. Using the form T = ms + b, we substitute our values: T = 8s + 120. This equation accurately models your total cost.

Calculate for a Specific Number of T-shirts: To find the total cost for producing 25 t-shirts, substitute s = 25 into our equation: T = 8(25) + 120. This calculates to T = 200 + 120, so T = $320. Your total cost for producing 25 t-shirts would be $320.

Check: If you make 0 t-shirts, T = 8(0) + 120 = $120, which is just the setup cost. If you make 1 t-shirt, T = 8(1) + 120 = $128. The equation makes sense based on the problem description.

Challenge03080W0

Alright [Student Name], let's tackle a trickier scenario where some information isn't immediately obvious. A local community garden charges a one-time registration fee for new members, plus a monthly fee for garden plot maintenance. Sarah paid a total of $105 after 4 months of membership. Create a linear equation that represents the total cost (C) for a membership of m months. Then, use your equation to determine the total cost after 9 months.

  1. Identify Knowns and Unknowns: We know the total cost ($105) for 4 months. We know there's a one-time registration fee (our y-intercept) and a monthly fee (our slope), but we don't know either of these values directly yet. Let 'R' be the registration fee and 'M' be the monthly fee. The equation will be C = Mm + R.
  2. Determine the Monthly Fee: We need to find the rate of change first. Sarah's total cost was $105 after 4 months. Let's say her registration fee was $25 (a common challenge is to assume the problem only gives you one data point, but you must deduce another). If the problem states 'after 4 months it was $105', and 'after 7 months it was $180', we could use (4, 105) and (7, 180) to find the slope. However, since we only have one total and a number of months, we're likely missing a piece of information or need to make an assumption about one of the fees, unless the 'one-time registration fee' IS the y-intercept and we can use it to find the slope.

Let's re-frame this to be more solvable with the given information, or provide a fixed y-intercept. Let's assume the one-time registration fee is $25. Now we can find the monthly fee. Common Error: A common mistake would be to assume $105/4 is the monthly fee. This is incorrect because the $105 includes the one-time registration fee. To avoid this, always subtract the fixed cost before dividing by the number of months to find the monthly rate. So, the cost for 4 months of just maintenance is $105 - $25 = $80. Therefore, the monthly fee (M) is $80 / 4 months = $20 per month. 3. Formulate the Equation: Now that we have the monthly fee (M = $20) and the registration fee (R = $25), we can create the equation: C = 20m + 25. 4. Calculate for a Specific Number of Months: To find the total cost after 9 months, substitute m = 9 into the equation: C = 20(9) + 25. This simplifies to C = 180 + 25, so C = $205.

Check: If Sarah paid $105 after 4 months, using our equation: C = 20(4) + 25 = 80 + 25 = $105. This matches the given information, confirming our equation is correct.

For every step you take horizontally (across the map), you always take a fixed number of steps vertically (up or down). TRAP: Misinterpreting the Y-Intercept MISTAKE: [Student Name] might mistakenly use a given y-coordinate as the y-intercept (b) without confirming if the corresponding x-coordinate is 0, or misidentify a rate as the starting value. WHY TEMPTING: Both the y-intercept and other y-values are numbers provided in the problem, making it easy to assign the wrong role without careful analysis. SOLUTION: Always identify the initial value or starting condition as the y-intercept. If given two points or a point and a slope, calculate the slope first, then use substitution to solve for the correct y-intercept. EXAMPLE: Given points (3, 15) and (6, 27). Wrong: Assume 15 is the y-intercept, leading to y = 4x + 15. Right: Calculate slope m = (27-15)/(6-3) = 12/3 = 4. Use (3, 15) in y=4x+b: 15 = 4(3) + b => 15 = 12 + b => b = 3. Equation: y = 4x + 3.

TRAP: Switching Independent and Dependent Variables MISTAKE: [Student Name] might incorrectly assign the roles of x and y in a word problem, leading to an equation that models the inverse relationship instead of the intended one. WHY TEMPTING: The order in which information is presented in a word problem can sometimes be confusing, or [they] might not clearly define what quantity depends on the other. SOLUTION: Determine which quantity is causing the change (independent, x) and which quantity is being affected or measured (dependent, y). Ask, 'What depends on what?' EXAMPLE: A cell phone bill has a flat fee of $20 plus $0.15 per minute. Wrong: m = 0.15C + 20, where 'm' is minutes and 'C' is cost. Right: C = 0.15m + 20, where 'C' is the total cost (dependent) and 'm' is the number of minutes (independent).

TRAP: Forgetting the Initial Value (Non-Zero Y-Intercept) MISTAKE: Students often create an equation that only reflects the rate of change (slope), assuming the relationship is proportional and starts at zero, even when a starting fee or amount is stated. WHY TEMPTING: Many initial examples of linear equations are directly proportional, leading students to default to a y-intercept of zero, or they overlook key phrases indicating a fixed starting point. SOLUTION: Carefully read the problem for any fixed fees, initial amounts, or starting points that occur at x=0. These represent the y-intercept (b) and must be included in the equation. EXAMPLE: A taxi charges a $3 initial fee plus $2.50 per mile. Wrong: C = 2.50m (where C is cost, m is miles). This equation implies a $0 initial fee. Right: C = 2.50m + 3. The $3 initial fee is the y-intercept.

TRAP: Incorrectly Calculating Slope from Data MISTAKE: When given two points, [Student Name] might subtract the x-coordinates in the numerator or the y-coordinates in the denominator of the slope formula, or mix up the order of subtraction. WHY TEMPTING: The slope formula involves subtraction, and it's easy to make a simple algebraic error or mix up the variables when moving quickly through a problem. SOLUTION: Always remember slope is 'rise over run' or the change in y divided by the change in x. Consistently subtract the coordinates from the same point: m = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1). EXAMPLE: Given points (2, 8) and (5, 17). Wrong: m = (5 - 2) / (17 - 8) = 3 / 9 = 1/3. Right: m = (17 - 8) / (5 - 2) = 9 / 3 = 3.

TRAP: Sign Errors with Negative Slopes MISTAKE: [Student Name] might interpret a decreasing quantity as a positive slope or fail to include the negative sign when the rate of change indicates a reduction or loss. WHY TEMPTING: Students often associate 'change' with positive values, and may not fully internalize that 'decrease' or 'reduce' directly translates to a negative rate of change. SOLUTION: Pay close attention to keywords such as 'decreases,' 'reduces,' 'spends,' 'loses,' or 'depreciates' as these indicate a negative slope. Visually, a line going downwards from left to right has a negative slope. EXAMPLE: A car's value depreciates by $1,800 per year. It was initially worth $28,000. Wrong: V = 1800t + 28000 (implies value increases). Right: V = -1800t + 28000, where V is value and t is years.

Exam Prep
Overview

To ace questions on creating linear equations, you must know three forms cold: slope-intercept (y = mx + b), point-slope (y - y1 = m(x - x1)), and standard form (Ax + By = C). Be proficient at calculating slope (m = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1)) from two points, and identifying the y-intercept (b) as the initial value when x=0. Practice extracting these components from tables, graphs, and especially word problems, translating real-world rates into 'm' and starting amounts into 'b'.

On standardized tests like the SAT or ACT, creating linear equations often appears in multiple-choice questions where you select the equation that represents a given graph, table of values, or a detailed word problem. You might also encounter open-ended questions asking you to formulate an equation to model a scenario, then use it to make predictions or interpret its parameters. Pay attention to unit consistency and the practical meaning of slope and y-intercept in context.

Strategy
  1. Translate Keywords to Math Symbols When to use: This strategy is crucial for word problems where you need to build an equation from a written description. How to apply: Underline or circle key phrases. Words like 'per hour,' 'each,' 'rate of' almost always signal the slope (m). Phrases like 'initial fee,' 'starting amount,' 'flat rate' typically indicate the y-intercept (b). Clearly define your variables (e.g., let 'C' be total cost, 'h' be hours). Example: "A streaming service costs $8.99 per month plus a one-time activation fee of $5." You'd identify $8.99/month as the slope (m) and $5 as the y-intercept (b). Let C = total cost and t = months. Equation: C = 8.99t + 5.
  1. Verify with a Given Point When to use: After you've created an equation from a graph, table, or word problem, use this to check its accuracy. How to apply: If the problem provides a specific data point (e.g., "after 4 minutes, the temperature was 72 degrees") or a coordinate pair, substitute these values into your derived equation. If the equation results in a true statement, it's likely correct. Example: If you found the equation y = 10x + 5. The problem states that when x = 3, y should be 35.
