Constant of Proportionality: Equations

Skill ID: 7.RP.006.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can write and interpret equations in the form y = kx to represent proportional relationships and identify the constant of proportionality, k.
Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

In mathematics, the Constant of Proportionality is a fundamental concept for understanding proportional relationships, especially when represented by equations. When two quantities, 'y' and 'x', are in a proportional relationship, their ratio is always constant. This constant ratio is called the constant of proportionality, denoted by 'k', and it defines the equation: y = kx. Here, 'y' is the dependent variable, 'x' is the independent variable, and 'k' is the unchanging factor that scales 'x' to 'y'. For example, if you earn $1.75 for every bottle recycled, the money earned 'y' is proportionally related to the number of bottles 'x' by the equation y = 1.75x. In this equation, $1.75 is the constant of proportionality, 'k'. It tells us the rate at which 'y' changes for every unit change in 'x'. Imagine it like a precise conversion factor; just as you'd use a conversion factor to change feet to inches, 'k' converts 'x' into 'y' in a proportional way. [Student Name], understanding this 'k' helps us predict outcomes. If [they] recycled 10 bottles, [they] would earn y = 1.75 * 10 = $17.50. This constant rate is what makes proportional relationships so predictable and useful in real-world scenarios, [Student Name]. It’s a powerful tool for mathematical modeling.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Let's establish a clear and predictable understanding of the Constant of Proportionality in equations. First, the definition: In a proportional relationship, the Constant of Proportionality, denoted by 'k', is the unchanging ratio of two quantities, 'y' and 'x', expressed by the equation y = kx. This 'k' represents the unit rate of the relationship. Second, an example: Consider the cost of apples at a local grocery store. If a bag of 3 apples costs $4.50, and a bag of 6 apples costs $9.00, we can identify a proportional relationship. To find 'k', we divide the total cost 'y' by the number of apples 'x'. For 3 apples: k = $4.50 / 3 = $1.50 per apple. For 6 apples: k = $9.00 / 6 = $1.50 per apple. Our constant of proportionality, 'k', is $1.50. So, the equation representing this relationship is y = 1.50x. Finally, an application: Knowing this equation, [Student Name] can easily predict the cost of any number of apples. If [they] wants to buy 8 apples, [they] can calculate y = 1.50 * 8 = $12.00. This stable structure—definition, example, and application—ensures [Student Name] can consistently identify and use the constant of proportionality in various scenarios to make accurate predictions.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello [Student Name]! Let's work through finding the constant of proportionality and writing an equation for a real-world situation. Imagine your school is selling spirit wear to raise money for new soccer goals. They announce that 6 t-shirts cost $90. We want to find the constant of proportionality and write an equation that represents the total cost (y) for any number of t-shirts (x).

  1. 💡 Hint: Think about what two quantities are changing together and how to express their relationship as a ratio. First, let's identify our quantities: the number of t-shirts and the total cost. We know that the cost is dependent on the number of t-shirts. So, we'll set up a ratio of cost to t-shirts: $90 for 6 t-shirts.
  1. 💡 Hint: To find the constant of proportionality, 'k', you need to find the unit rate. How much does ONE t-shirt cost? To find 'k', we divide the total cost by the number of t-shirts: $90 / 6 t-shirts = $15 per t-shirt. So, our constant of proportionality, k, is 15.
  1. 💡 Hint: Remember the general form of a proportional equation: y = kx. What do 'y', 'k', and 'x' represent in our problem? Now we can write our equation. If 'y' is the total cost and 'x' is the number of t-shirts, and our 'k' is 15, the equation is: y = 15x.

Let's check: If you bought 6 t-shirts, y = 15 * 6 = $90. This matches the given information!

Standard

Let's explore another example to understand the constant of proportionality, [Student Name]. A popular US baseball league's official merchandise store sells miniature baseball bats. If a customer buys 4 miniature bats for a total of $26, what is the constant of proportionality, and what equation represents the total cost for any number of bats?

  1. Identify the relationship: We have two quantities: the number of miniature baseball bats and the total cost. The cost depends on the number of bats purchased. We are given 4 bats cost $26.
  1. Calculate the constant of proportionality (k): The constant of proportionality is the unit rate, which tells us the cost per single bat. To find this, we divide the total cost by the number of bats: k = Total Cost / Number of Bats = $26 / 4 bats = $6.50 per bat. So, k = 6.50.
  1. Write the equation: We use the general form for proportional relationships, y = kx, where 'y' is the total cost, 'x' is the number of bats, and 'k' is our constant. Plugging in our 'k' value, the equation is: y = 6.50x.

Check: If a customer bought 4 bats, using our equation, y = 6.50 * 4 = $26. This confirms our constant and equation are correct.

Challenge

[Student Name], let's tackle a more complex problem involving the constant of proportionality. An online gaming store offers virtual currency bundles. If a user spends $35 to get 2,800 'Gems,' and for every 500 Gems, they can buy one special 'Power-Up Potion,' we need to find the constant of proportionality between the cost (y) and the number of Gems (x). Then, write the equation and figure out how much it would cost to get enough Gems for 7 Power-Up Potions.

  1. Find the constant of proportionality for dollars per Gem. The constant 'k' relates cost to Gems. k = Total Cost / Total Gems = $35 / 2800 Gems = $0.0125 per Gem. Common Error: Dividing Gems by dollars (2800/35) or trying to find Gems per dollar instead of dollars per Gem if 'y' is cost. Always ensure 'y' (dependent variable, often cost) is in the numerator.
  1. Write the equation relating cost to Gems. Using y = kx, with y = cost and x = Gems: y = 0.0125x. Common Error: Forgetting to write the equation in 'y=kx' form or mixing up 'x' and 'y' once 'k' is found.
  1. Calculate total Gems needed for 7 Power-Up Potions. Each potion needs 500 Gems, so 7 potions * 500 Gems/potion = 3,500 Gems. Common Error: Only calculating for 1 Power-Up Potion or miscalculating the total Gems needed.
  1. Use the equation to find the total cost for 3,500 Gems. Substitute 3,500 for 'x' in our equation: y = 0.0125 * 3500 = $43.75. So, it would cost $43.75 to get enough Gems for 7 Power-Up Potions.
