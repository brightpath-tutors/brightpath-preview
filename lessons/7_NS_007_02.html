Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

When adding rational numbers, the fundamental principles of combining positive and negative values you've learned for integers remain crucial. A rational number is any number that can be written as a fraction P/Q, where P and Q are integers and Q is not zero. This broad category includes integers like 5 or -3, fractions like 1/2 or -3/4, and decimals like 0.75 or -2.5. The mechanism for addition depends on the format, but the core idea is combining quantities. For instance, to add -2.5 + 3/4, [Student Name] would first convert 3/4 into its decimal equivalent, 0.75. Now we have -2.5 + 0.75. Since the numbers have different signs, we find the difference between their absolute values: | -2.5 | = 2.5 and | 0.75 | = 0.75. Subtracting, 2.5 - 0.75 = 1.75. Because the number with the larger absolute value, -2.5, is negative, our sum is -1.75. This process is like balancing a financial ledger, [Student Name]. Each transaction, whether a deposit or a withdrawal, is a rational number, and adding them tells you your total balance. Just as combining different financial activities leads to a single net result, adding rational numbers brings various values together for one definitive sum.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello, [Student Name]! Let's tackle adding rational numbers with a real-world example. Imagine your favorite online game where you earn or lose points. Let's calculate your score change: you gained 2/3 of a point, and then you lost 1/2 of a point due to a glitch. What's your total change in score? Here’s how to solve 2/3 + (-1/2):

  1. 💡 Hint: We can't add or subtract fractions unless their bottom numbers (denominators) are the same. Find a common denominator for 3 and 2. The smallest number that both 3 and 2 divide into evenly is 6. To change 2/3, we multiply both the top and bottom by 2 (22/32 = 4/6). To change -1/2, we multiply both the top and bottom by 3 ((-1)3 / (2)3 = -3/6). Our problem is now 4/6 + (-3/6).
  1. 💡 Hint: Now that the denominators match, we can combine the top numbers. Remember the rules for adding integers: if the signs are different, subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger absolute value and keep the sign of the number with the larger absolute value. We add the numerators: 4 + (-3). This is like having 4 positive steps and 3 negative steps. You end up with 1 positive step. So, 4 + (-3) = 1. The denominator stays the same. This gives us 1/6.
  1. 💡 Hint: Always check if your fraction can be made simpler. Can both the top and bottom numbers be divided by the same number (other than 1) to make them smaller? The fraction 1/6 cannot be simplified further because 1 and 6 do not share any common factors other than 1. So, your total change in score is 1/6 of a point.

To check this: Think about a number line. If you start at 0, move 2/3 (which is 4/6) to the right. Then, from there, move 1/2 (which is 3/6) to the left. You'll land at 1/6. This confirms our answer!

Standard

Let’s work through adding rational numbers with decimals, [Student Name]. Imagine a thermometer during a cold winter day in Chicago. The temperature starts at -3.75 degrees Fahrenheit. Then, later in the day, it warms up by 1.2 degrees Fahrenheit. What is the new temperature?

Here's how to solve -3.75 + 1.2:

  1. First, we need to understand the signs. We are adding a negative number (-3.75) and a positive number (1.2). When adding numbers with different signs, the sum's sign will be the same as the number with the larger absolute value. The absolute value of -3.75 is 3.75, and the absolute value of 1.2 is 1.2. Since 3.75 is larger than 1.2, our final answer will be negative.
  1. Next, subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger absolute value. This means we'll calculate 3.75 - 1.2. It can be helpful to line up the decimal points: 3.75 - 1.20 = 2.55. (Adding the 0 to 1.2 makes subtraction easier, as 1.20 is equivalent to 1.2).
  1. Finally, apply the sign we determined in Step 1. Since the absolute value of -3.75 was larger, our answer is negative. So, -3.75 + 1.2 = -2.55. The new temperature is -2.55 degrees Fahrenheit.

To check your work, you can think of it like this: If you owed someone $3.75 and then paid them back $1.20, you would still owe them money, specifically $2.55. The negative sign represents the amount you still owe, confirming our calculation.

Challenge

Let's tackle a trickier problem, [Student Name], combining different types of rational numbers. Suppose you're tracking changes in your savings account. You start with a deposit of $2 and 1/4 (2 1/4) dollars. Then, you spend $1.50 (-1.5) on a soda, and finally, you find $3/8 (3/8) of a dollar in your jeans pocket. What's your total change in savings?

Here’s how to solve 2 1/4 + (-1.5) + 3/8:

  1. [Common Error: Not converting all numbers to the same format or making conversion mistakes.] To add these, it's usually easiest to convert everything to fractions or everything to decimals. Let's convert them all to fractions with a common denominator. 2 1/4 is 9/4. -1.5 is -15/10, which simplifies to -3/2. 3/8 is already a fraction. Now find a common denominator for 4, 2, and 8, which is 8. So, 9/4 becomes 18/8. -3/2 becomes -12/8. Our problem is now 18/8 + (-12/8) + 3/8.
  1. [Common Error: Forgetting sign rules when adding multiple numbers, especially with subtraction masquerading as adding a negative.] Now, add them from left to right. 18/8 + (-12/8) = (18 - 12)/8 = 6/8. Then, add the last fraction: 6/8 + 3/8 = (6 + 3)/8 = 9/8.
  1. [Common Error: Not simplifying the final answer or leaving an improper fraction when a mixed number is more appropriate for context.] The fraction 9/8 is an improper fraction (the numerator is larger than the denominator). As a mixed number, 9/8 is 1 and 1/8. So, your total change in savings is $1 and 1/8. In decimal form, this is $1.125.

To check: You could estimate. 2 and a quarter is about 2.25. Losing $1.50 leaves about $0.75. Finding $0.375 ($3/8) makes it about $1.125. The exact answer is $1.125, which matches our check.

Analogical Thinking — Visual

[Student Name], picture a number line stretching infinitely in both directions. When you add a positive rational number, you move to the right on this line. If you add a negative rational number, you move to the left. For example, if you start at 0 and want to add 1/2, you physically jump half a step to the right, landing exactly between 0 and 1. If you then add -3/4, you turn around from your 1/2 position and jump three-quarters of a step to the left.

The key is to visualize the direction and distance of each 'jump.' Fractions and decimals simply mean your jumps aren't always full whole steps; they can be partial steps. Your final landing spot is the sum. So, 1/2 + (-3/4) would mean landing at -1/4, a quarter-step to the left of zero.
