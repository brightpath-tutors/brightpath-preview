Complex Unit Rates

Skill ID: 7.RP.001  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward00511C0

A complex unit rate precisely defines a relationship between two quantities where at least one of them is expressed as a fraction, and the goal is to find the amount of the first quantity per one unit of the second. For instance, instead of knowing you drive 100 miles in 2 hours, you might know you drive 1/2 mile in 1/4 hour. The mechanism involves dividing fractions, often using the "keep, change, flip" method. To find out how many miles [Student Name] travels in one hour, you would set up the ratio (1/2 mile) / (1/4 hour). This translates to (1/2) ÷ (1/4), which simplifies to (1/2) × (4/1) = 4/2 = 2 miles per hour. Just like simplifying a ratio of whole numbers, such as 200 miles in 4 hours becoming 50 miles per hour, a complex unit rate simplifies a ratio of fractions to find "how much for one." [Student Name], mastering this helps you compare values accurately and rigorously.

Hello [Student Name]! Let's tackle a complex unit rate problem step-by-step. Imagine a small-batch coffee roaster who uses 3/4 pound of specialty beans to produce 1/2 a bag of roasted coffee. [they] wants to know how many pounds of beans are needed to make one full bag of roasted coffee. This means we need to find the unit rate in 'pounds of beans per bag of coffee'.

  1. 💡 Hint: Remember how to divide fractions: Keep, Change, Flip (KCF). To divide by a fraction, we multiply by its reciprocal. The reciprocal of 1/2 is 2/1. * Calculation: (3/4) × (2/1)
  1. 💡 Hint: Multiply the numerators and denominators, then simplify. Now, perform the multiplication. Multiply: (3 × 2) / (4 × 1) = 6/4 Simplify: 6/4 simplifies to 3/2.
  1. 💡 Hint: Interpret your answer in context. What does the simplified fraction mean? The result, 3/2, represents 3/2 pounds of beans per 1 bag of coffee. This can also be written as 1 1/2 pounds of beans per bag. * Answer: The roaster needs 1 1/2 pounds of beans to make one full bag of roasted coffee.

Check: If 1 1/2 pounds of beans makes 1 bag, then for 1/2 bag, [they] would need (1 1/2) × (1/2) = (3/2) × (1/2) = 3/4 pounds of beans. This matches the original problem information, so our answer is correct!

Standard00511W0

Let's work through a problem involving a complex unit rate, [Student Name]. Imagine a construction crew is pouring concrete for a new sidewalk. They manage to pour 7/8 of a cubic yard of concrete in just 1/4 of an hour. The site manager needs to know how many cubic yards of concrete they can pour in one full hour to estimate the project's completion time. This is a classic complex unit rate problem, asking for 'cubic yards per hour'.

  1. Set up the ratio: We want to find the rate in cubic yards per hour. This means we'll divide the amount of concrete (7/8 cubic yard) by the time it took (1/4 hour). * Ratio: (7/8 cubic yard) / (1/4 hour)
  1. Convert division to multiplication: To divide fractions, we multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction. The reciprocal of 1/4 is 4/1. * Calculation: (7/8) × (4/1)
  1. Perform the multiplication: Multiply the numerators together and the denominators together. Then, simplify the resulting fraction. Multiply: (7 × 4) / (8 × 1) = 28/8 Simplify: 28/8 can be simplified by dividing both numerator and denominator by 4, which gives 7/2. As a mixed number, this is 3 1/2.
  1. State the unit rate: The crew can pour 3 1/2 cubic yards of concrete in one hour.

Check: If the crew pours 3 1/2 cubic yards in one hour, then in 1/4 of an hour, they would pour (3 1/2) × (1/4). Converting 3 1/2 to an improper fraction gives 7/2. So, (7/2) × (1/4) = 7/8 cubic yards. This matches the information given in the problem, confirming our answer is correct.

Challenge00511W0

[Student Name], let's try a more challenging problem involving complex unit rates. Sarah is training for a cross-country race. She ran 4 1/2 miles in 2/3 of an hour. If she wants to maintain this pace for a 12-mile race, how long, in hours, will it take her to complete the entire race?

  1. Find the unit rate (miles per hour): First, we need to determine how many miles Sarah runs in one hour. This means dividing the distance (4 1/2 miles) by the time (2/3 hour). Convert the mixed number: 4 1/2 = 9/2. Set up division: (9/2) ÷ (2/3). Common Error: A common mistake here is to incorrectly convert the mixed number or to flip the wrong fraction when dividing. Always make sure to convert to an improper fraction before dividing, and remember to multiply by the reciprocal of the second fraction. Calculate: (9/2) × (3/2) = 27/4 miles per hour.
  1. Use the unit rate to find total time: Now that we know Sarah runs 27/4 miles in one hour, we need to find out how many hours it takes to run 12 miles. This is a division problem: total distance divided by her speed. Calculation: 12 miles ÷ (27/4 miles/hour). Common Error: Students might mistakenly multiply here instead of dividing, or confuse which quantity is the divisor. Think: if you have a total distance and a rate, dividing gives you time. The units help: miles / (miles/hour) = hours. Convert 12 to a fraction: 12/1. Calculate: (12/1) × (4/27) = 48/27.
  1. Simplify and interpret: Simplify the final fraction and state the answer in hours. Simplify: 48/27 can be simplified by dividing both numerator and denominator by 3, which gives 16/9. As a mixed number, this is 1 7/9 hours. Common Error: Forgetting to simplify the fraction or not converting it to a mixed number if appropriate for the context. Always check if your answer makes sense in the real world. * Answer: It will take Sarah 1 7/9 hours to complete the 12-mile race.

Check: If Sarah runs 16/9 hours for 12 miles, her speed would be 12 miles / (16/9 hours) = 12 9/16 = 108/16 = 27/4 miles/hour. This matches our calculated unit rate. Our original unit rate (27/4 mph) for 2/3 hour means (27/4) (2/3) = 54/12 = 9/2 = 4 1/2 miles, which matches the problem.

For instance, if a unit cools a space by 2 1/4 degrees Fahrenheit in just 3/4 of an hour, the technician needs to calculate the cooling rate per full hour to assess performance or compare it with manufacturer specifications.

To find this, they set up a complex unit rate: (2 1/4 °F) / (3/4 hour)

They would convert the mixed number to an improper fraction: 9/4 °F. Then, they divide: (9/4) ÷ (3/4) = (9/4) × (4/3) = 36/12 = 3 °F per hour.

Incorrect Reciprocal Application
Mistake:
When dividing fractions in a complex unit rate, students sometimes incorrectly take the reciprocal of the dividend (the first fraction) instead of the divisor (the second fraction) before multiplying.
Why tempting:
They remember 'reciprocal' is involved in fraction division but misapply the rule to the wrong fraction.
Correction:
Always apply the 'Keep, Change, Flip' (KCF) rule: Keep the first fraction, Change division to multiplication, and Flip (take the reciprocal of) the second fraction.
Upside-Down Rate
Mistake:
Students set up the ratio for the unit rate incorrectly, placing the quantity they want 'per unit' of in the numerator instead of the denominator.
Why tempting:
They might put the numbers in the order they appear in the problem or forget which quantity defines the 'one unit.'
Correction:
Clearly identify the two quantities. The quantity you want to express 'per one unit' of goes in the denominator of your initial ratio setup. For example, for 'miles per hour,' it's miles / hours.
Ignoring Mixed Numbers
Mistake:
Students attempt to divide or multiply mixed numbers directly without first converting them to improper fractions.
Why tempting:
They might try to operate on the whole numbers and fractions separately, or simply overlook this essential conversion step for fraction division.
Correction:
Always convert all mixed numbers to improper fractions before performing any division or multiplication when calculating complex unit rates.
Decimal Dilemma
Mistake:
Students convert fractions to decimals that are non-terminating (e.g., 1/3 or 2/3) and then round prematurely, leading to imprecise or incorrect final answers.
Why tempting:
Working with decimals can feel simpler or more familiar than fractions, especially when using a calculator.
Correction:
When dealing with fractions, particularly those that result in repeating decimals, it is best to keep them in fractional form and use fraction division rules (KCF) to maintain accuracy. Convert to decimals only at the very end if required by the problem.
The 'Per' Problem (Multiplication Misinterpretation)
Mistake:
Students misinterpret 'per' in unit rates as meaning multiplication instead of division to find the value for a single unit.
Why tempting:
In other contexts, 'per' can imply multiplication (e.g., '$5 per ticket for 10 tickets is $5 * 10'), causing confusion in unit rate scenarios.
Correction:
Always remember that 'per' in unit rates signifies division, asking for the amount corresponding to a single unit of the second quantity. Set up the ratio as 'quantity A / quantity B'.

Parent ELI5

Hello [Parent Name]! Let's demystify Complex Unit Rates. Think of it as finding out 'how much for one' of something, even when the numbers you start with are a bit messy – like fractions. [they] will master this!

  1. Understand "Unit Rate": A unit rate tells you how much of one thing there is per one unit of another. For instance, if a car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, its unit rate is 30 miles per 1 hour.
  2. What Makes it "Complex"? It becomes 'complex' when the initial quantities are fractions. Imagine a recipe calls for 1/2 cup of sugar for 3/4 of a batch of cookies. The question is: "How much sugar for one whole batch?" That's a complex unit rate.
  3. Setting up the Ratio: Always write the quantity you want to find 'per one' on top. So, for the cookie example, it's (1/2 cup sugar) / (3/4 batch).
  4. The "Keep, Change, Flip" Rule: To solve, remember the rule for dividing fractions: "Keep the first fraction, change division to multiplication, and flip (find the reciprocal of) the second fraction." * (1/2) ÷ (3/4) becomes (1/2) × (4/3).
  5. Multiply and Simplify: Multiply across: (1 × 4) / (2 × 3) = 4/6. Simplify: 4/6 = 2/3. So, you need 2/3 cup of sugar for one whole batch.
