Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward02704C0

A repeating decimal is a decimal representation of a rational number where one or more digits repeat indefinitely after the decimal point. For example, 0.333... and 0.121212... are repeating decimals. The mechanism to convert these to fractions involves algebra. Let's take 0.121212... as an example. First, we assign the decimal to a variable, say x = 0.121212.... Next, we multiply x by a power of 10 that shifts the repeating block to the left of the decimal point. Since two digits repeat, we multiply by 100: 100x = 12.121212.... Now, subtract the original equation from this new one: 100x - x = 12.121212... - 0.121212.... This simplifies to 99x = 12. Finally, solve for x: x = 12/99. This fraction can be simplified to 4/33. This process, [Student Name], is akin to solving a system of equations where you manipulate two related expressions to isolate an unknown value, much like you might solve for a variable in algebra. [they] will find this method very precise for finding exact fractional forms, rather than rounded decimals.

Correction:
First, multiply the decimal by a power of 10 to shift the non-repeating part to the left of the decimal. Then, set up a second equation by multiplying by another power of 10 to shift one full repeating block past the decimal. Then subtract the two equations where repeating parts align.

Overview

Converting repeating decimals to fractions is a fundamental skill in Grade 8, bridging the gap between rational numbers expressed in different forms. To know this cold, [Student Name] must master the algebraic method: setting the decimal equal to 'x', multiplying by appropriate powers of 10 to shift the repeating block, and subtracting the resulting equations to eliminate the repeating part. Understanding when to use 10x, 100x, or 1000x based on the repeating pattern, and how to handle non-repeating digits before the repeating block, is crucial. Simplifying the final fraction is also a non-negotiable step.

On standardized tests like state assessments or the PSAT 8/9, this topic frequently appears in the Number System domain. Questions might ask for the fractional equivalent of a given repeating decimal, or require [Student Name] to apply this conversion within a larger problem involving operations with rational numbers. Sometimes, it might be presented as a multiple-choice question where simplification is key to identifying the correct option. Being able to perform these conversions quickly and accurately demonstrates a strong grasp of rational number properties and algebraic manipulation.

Strategy
  1. The 'Shift and Subtract' Method When to Use: This is the core algebraic method for converting any repeating decimal to a fraction, especially pure repeating decimals like 0.5454.... How to Apply: First, set your decimal equal to 'x'. Multiply 'x' by a power of 10 (10, 100, 1000, etc.) to shift one full repeating block to the left of the decimal point. Subtract the original 'x' equation from this new equation to eliminate the repeating tail, then solve for x. Example: To convert 0.5454...: Let x = 0.5454.... Multiply by 100: 100x = 54.5454.... Subtract x: 99x = 54. So, x = 54/99, which simplifies to 6/11.
  1. The 'Pre-Shift for Mixed Decimals' Method When to Use: This strategy is essential for repeating decimals that have non-repeating digits immediately after the decimal point, such as 0.166.... How to Apply: Before applying the main 'Shift and Subtract' method, first multiply your initial 'x' equation by a power of 10 that moves only the non-repeating digits to the left of the decimal. This creates a new equation where only the repeating part follows the decimal. Then, proceed with the 'Shift and Subtract' method on this new equation. Example: To convert 0.166...: Let x = 0.166.... First, 10x = 1.666.... Then 100x = 16.666.... Subtract: 100x - 10x = 15, so 90x = 15, which means x = 15/90 = 1/6.
  1. The 'Simplify and Verify' Method When to Use: Always, as the final step after converting any repeating decimal to a fraction. How to Apply: Once you have your fraction, simplify it to its lowest terms by finding the greatest common divisor (GCD) of the numerator and denominator and dividing both by it. For verification, use a calculator to divide your simplified fraction's numerator by its denominator. The result should match the original repeating decimal, ensuring accuracy. Example: After converting 0.1818... to 18/99. Simplify: The GCD of 18 and 99 is 9. Dividing both by 9 gives 2/11. Verify: 2 ÷ 11 on a calculator yields 0.181818..., confirming the conversion.
