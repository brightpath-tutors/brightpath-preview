Writing Algebraic Expressions

Skill ID: 6.EE.003.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Writing algebraic expressions is the crucial skill of translating real-world scenarios or verbal descriptions into the concise language of mathematics. It involves representing unknown quantities with variables—usually letters like 'x' or 'y'—and combining them with known numbers (constants) using operation symbols for addition (+), subtraction (-), multiplication (*), and division (/). For example, if you want to express "the total cost of three items, each costing an unknown amount 'c' plus a fixed shipping fee of $4.99," you would write 3c + 4.99. The '3c' represents the cost of the items (3 multiplied by c), and '+ 4.99' adds the shipping. This process helps us model situations mathematically. Think of it like being a culinary translator, [Student Name]. If a recipe calls for "twice the amount of sugar plus one cup," you're translating that into 2s + 1, where 's' is the initial amount of sugar. This mathematical translation provides a universal, unambiguous way to represent relationships, allowing us to solve problems efficiently. [they] will find this skill incredibly useful, [Student Name].

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello [Student Name]! Let's break down how to write an algebraic expression for a real-world problem. Imagine you're at a concession stand at a high school basketball game. A large soda costs $4, and a hot dog costs $5. You want to buy some hot dogs, but you're not sure how many yet, and you definitely want one large soda. Let's write an expression for your total cost.

  1. 💡 Hint: What part of the cost will change? First, identify what quantity you don't know yet and give it a letter. In this problem, you don't know how many hot dogs you'll buy. Let's use the letter 'h' to represent the number of hot dogs.
  1. 💡 Hint: How does the cost of hot dogs relate to the number you buy? Next, think about the cost associated with the changing quantity. Each hot dog costs $5. So, if you buy 'h' hot dogs, the total cost for the hot dogs would be 5 multiplied by 'h', which we write as 5h.
  1. 💡 Hint: What's the fixed cost that doesn't change? Now, consider any costs that are fixed, meaning they don't change no matter how many hot dogs you buy. You're definitely buying one large soda, which costs $4. This is a fixed amount we need to add to the cost of the hot dogs.
  1. 💡 Hint: How do you combine the changing and fixed costs? Finally, combine the variable part (cost of hot dogs) and the fixed part (cost of soda) with an addition sign. So, your total cost expression is 5h + 4.

Check: If [they] decides to buy 3 hot dogs, you would substitute 3 for 'h': 5(3) + 4 = 15 + 4 = $19. This makes sense: 3 hot dogs at $5 each is $15, plus the $4 soda, totals $19.

Standard

Let's work through writing an algebraic expression for a situation [Student Name] might encounter at a theme park. Imagine tickets to the 'Wild Waves' water park cost $45 each. If you also rent a locker for your belongings for a flat fee of $12 for the day, how would you write an expression for the total cost for your group?

  1. Identify the variable: The cost of tickets depends on how many people are in your group. Since we don't know the exact number of people, we'll use a variable to represent it. Let's use 'p' for the number of people in your group.
  1. Express the variable cost: Each person's ticket costs $45. To find the total cost for tickets, you multiply the cost per ticket by the number of people. So, the cost for 'p' people would be 45 * p, which we write as 45p.
  1. Identify the fixed cost: In addition to the tickets, there's a one-time locker rental fee of $12. This amount doesn't change, regardless of how many people are in your group. It's a constant amount that needs to be added to the total cost.
  1. Combine the terms: To get the total cost, you add the variable cost (for the tickets) and the fixed cost (for the locker rental). The algebraic expression for the total cost is 45p + 12.

Check: If there are 4 people in the group, we substitute '4' for 'p' in the expression: 45(4) + 12 = 180 + 12 = $192. This means four tickets would be $180, plus the $12 locker rental, totaling $192.

Challenge

Let's tackle a trickier problem, [Student Name]. Your school is organizing a fundraising car wash. They charge $10 for each small car and $15 for each large truck. They also received a $50 donation before the event started. If 's' represents the number of small cars and 't' represents the number of large trucks washed, write an expression for the total money raised.

  1. Cost for Small Cars: We know each small car costs $10. So, for 's' small cars, the money earned is 10 s, or 10s. Common Error to Avoid: Don't accidentally use 's' to represent the cost of a small car. It represents the number* of small cars, and we multiply that number by the fixed cost per car.
  1. Cost for Large Trucks: Similarly, each large truck costs $15. For 't' large trucks, the money earned is 15 * t, or 15t. Common Error to Avoid: Make sure you use a different variable for different unknown quantities. Using 's' for both small cars and large trucks would mix up your earnings.
  1. The Fixed Donation: The school already received a $50 donation. This is a constant amount that doesn't depend on how many cars are washed. Common Error to Avoid: Don't forget to include fixed amounts that are part of the total. Sometimes students only focus on the variable parts.
  1. Combine All Parts: To find the total money raised, we add the earnings from small cars, the earnings from large trucks, and the initial donation. The expression is 10s + 15t + 50.

Check: If they wash 8 small cars and 5 large trucks, the total would be 10(8) + 15(5) + 50 = 80 + 75 + 50 = $205. This makes sense for the combined earnings and the donation.

Reversing Subtraction/Division Order
Mistake:
[Student Name] often translates phrases like '5 less than a number' as 5 - x instead of x - 5. Similarly, 'a number divided by 7' can be mistakenly written as 7 ÷ x.
Why tempting:
The numbers appear in the phrase in that specific order, making it seem natural to write them left-to-right as read.
Correction:
For 'less than' and 'subtracted from', think of the number being removed from the unknown quantity, so the unknown comes first. For 'divided by', the first mentioned quantity is the dividend.
Ignoring Implied Grouping
Mistake:
When a phrase states 'three times the sum of a number and eight', [Student Name] might write 3x + 8. This correctly translates 'three times a number plus eight' but not the intended 'three times the sum'.
Why tempting:
It's easy to overlook 'the sum of' as an operation that needs to be performed before multiplication, leading to a direct left-to-right translation without parentheses.
Correction:
Look for keywords like 'sum of', 'difference of', 'product of', or 'quotient of' when they are followed by 'and'. These often indicate that the operation between the two numbers or variables should be grouped in parentheses before another operation is applied.
Misinterpreting 'Times' or 'Product'
Mistake:
[Student Name] might write '4 and x' as 4x + x or 4 + x. Or 'the product of 5 and a number' as 5 + x.
Why tempting:
Students are very familiar with addition as a 'putting together' operation. When they see 'and', they instinctively think addition, even in the context of multiplication phrases.
Correction:
Remember that 'times' and 'product of' specifically refer to multiplication. When writing algebraic expressions, multiplication is often shown by placing a number next to a variable (e.g., 5x) or using a dot (e.g., 5 ⋅ x) or parentheses (e.g., 5(x)).
Including an Equal Sign in Expressions
Mistake:
When asked to write an expression for 'a number increased by seven', [Student Name] might incorrectly write x + 7 =. An expression does not contain an equal sign.
Why tempting:
Students are often accustomed to solving equations, where an equal sign is always present. They may confuse writing an expression with setting up an equation.
Correction:
An expression is a mathematical phrase that can contain numbers, variables, and operations, but it does not have an equal sign. Only equations have equal signs, showing that two expressions are equivalent.
Not Recognizing Implied Operations for Exponents
Mistake:
When asked for 'a number squared' or 'the cube of a number', [Student Name] might write 2n or 3n instead of n² or n³.
Why tempting:
The words 'squared' and 'cubed' might be less familiar as mathematical operations than addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division, and can be confused with multiplying by 2 or 3.
Correction:
Remember that 'squared' means raising to the power of 2, and 'cubed' means raising to the power of 3. These are specific mathematical terms for exponents.

Exam Prep
Overview

To ace questions on writing algebraic expressions, [Student Name] needs to know the key vocabulary for each operation cold. Understand that words like 'sum,' 'increased by,' and 'more than' mean addition; 'difference,' 'decreased by,' and 'less than' mean subtraction; 'product,' 'times,' and 'of' mean multiplication; and 'quotient,' 'divided by,' and 'per' mean division. Also, recognize phrases that imply exponents, like 'squared' or 'cubed,' and those that require grouping with parentheses, such as 'the sum of a number and 7, multiplied by 2.'

On standardized tests like the FSA or SBAC, questions about writing algebraic expressions typically appear as multiple-choice items where you pick the correct expression that matches a given word phrase, or as part of a word problem requiring you to set up the expression before solving. You might also see questions that ask you to identify keywords or explain the meaning of an expression in words. Be prepared to differentiate between expressions and equations; expressions do not contain an equals sign.

Strategy
  1. Keyword Hunter: When to use: Always, when translating any word phrase into an algebraic expression. How to apply: Read the phrase carefully and circle or highlight all the operation keywords (e.g., 'sum,' 'product,' 'less than,' 'divided by'). Also, identify the unknown quantity, usually 'a number,' and assign it a variable like 'x' or 'n'. * Example: Phrase: 'Three times a number increased by seven.' Keywords: 'Three times', 'a number' (x), 'increased by', 'seven'.
  1. Break it Down, Build it Up: When to use: For longer or more complex phrases, especially those involving multiple operations or grouping. How to apply: Translate the phrase piece by piece, starting with the core unknown and then adding operations. If you see 'the sum of' or 'the difference of' before a multiplication or division, parenthesize that sum/difference first. * Example: Phrase: 'Five more than the product of eight and a number.' Break it down: 'the product of eight and a number' is 8x. 'Five more than' means + 5. Build it up: 8x + 5.
  1. Test with a Number (If Time Allows): When to use: To verify your algebraic expression matches the original word phrase, especially for tricky 'less than' or grouping scenarios. How to apply: Pick a simple number (like 2 or 3) and substitute it into your written expression. Then, substitute that same number back into the original word phrase and see if both yield the same result. * Example: Phrase: 'Ten less than a number'. Your expression: x - 10. Test with x=12. 12 - 10 = 2. 'Ten less than 12' is 2. (If you wrote 10 - x, testing with 12 would give 10 - 12 = -2, which doesn't match 'ten less than 12'.)
Memory Hook
  1. 'FROM' and 'THAN' TURNAROUND! What it helps remember: This hook reminds [Student Name] that phrases using 'from' (like 'subtracted from') or 'than' (like 'less than') require reversing the order of the numbers/variables from how they appear in the English phrase. Example with real numbers: '5 subtracted from 12' means 12 - 5, not 5 - 12. Similarly, '3 less than 10' means 10 - 3, not 3 - 10.
  1. P.A.S.S. for Operations! What it helps remember: This acronym helps [Student Name] remember common keywords for each of the four basic operations. Example with real numbers: Product/Per (multiplication/division), Added to/And (addition), Subtracted from/Share (subtraction/division), Sum/Square (addition/exponents). For 'The sum of 6 and a number' it's 6 + x. For 'The product of 4 and 9' it's 4 × 9.

  1. O que é? Uma expressão algébrica é uma forma de escrever uma situação da vida real usando números, símbolos de operações (+, -, ×, ÷) e letras (chamadas 'variáveis') para representar coisas que não sabemos o valor exato, ou que podem mudar. Por exemplo, você vai ao supermercado e compra uma garrafa de leite por R$7 e alguns pães. Você não sabe quantos pães comprará, então cada pão custa R$2 e você compra 'P' pães. Sua conta total será: 7 + 2P. Veja, P é o mistério que podemos descobrir depois!
  1. Por que usamos? Elas nos ajudam a planejar e a descrever situações de forma matemática, mesmo sem ter todos os valores. Pense em seu orçamento: se [they] tem R$1000 e gasta R$500 em aluguel e 'X' reais em despesas variáveis por mês, o que sobra é 1000 - 500 - X. Isso é super útil para organizar suas finanças!
  1. Como 'traduzir' frases em matemática? Adição (+): 'soma', 'total', 'mais que', 'aumentado por'. Ex: 'O salário de [Parent Name] mais R$200 de bônus' -> Salário + 200. Subtração (-): 'diferença', 'menos que', 'diminuído por'. Ex: '15 reais a menos do que o preço da pizza' -> Preço da pizza - 15. Multiplicação (× ou sem sinal): 'vezes', 'produto', 'o dobro', 'o triplo'. Ex: 'Três vezes o número de clientes' -> 3 × Clientes ou 3C. Divisão (÷ ou /): 'quociente', 'dividido por', 'a metade', 'a terça parte'. Ex: 'A metade dos biscoitos' -> Biscoitos / 2 ou B/2.
