Repeating Decimal to Fraction

Skill ID: 8.NS.004.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Converting a repeating decimal to a fraction is a fundamental skill in The Number System, allowing us to express numbers that appear infinitely long in their precise rational form. A repeating decimal, like 0.666..., is a decimal representation of a rational number where one or more digits repeat indefinitely. To convert this, [Student Name], we use a clever algebraic method. Let's say we want to convert 0.666... to a fraction. We assign a variable, say 'x', to the decimal: x = 0.666.... Since one digit repeats, we multiply both sides by 10 (or 100 if two digits repeat, and so on): 10x = 6.666.... Now, here's the trick: subtract the original equation from the multiplied one. (10x - x) = (6.666... - 0.666...). This simplifies to 9x = 6. Finally, solve for x: x = 6/9, which simplifies to 2/3. This process is like finding the hidden pattern in a complex sequence; you're revealing the elegant fraction that generates the endless decimal. It's similar to how a complex musical chord can be broken down into individual notes.

The core idea is the same: finding the exact fractional representation for a number. It might seem a little more complex because of the repeating nature, but the steps are logical and straightforward. [they] already knows that 0.333... is 'about' 1/3. Today, we'll learn the precise method to prove it's exactly 1/3. This isn't about entirely new math, but rather a powerful new technique for numbers you already know. You've got this, because you're simply adding a new tool to your existing understanding of rational numbers. Stability — Clear & Structured

To calmly and systematically convert a repeating decimal into a fraction, we follow a reliable sequence of steps. First, let's clearly define what we're working with: a repeating decimal is a decimal that has a digit or a block of digits that repeats infinitely, like 0.111... or 0.121212.... The goal is to express this decimal as a simple fraction (numerator over denominator). For example, let's convert 0.111... to a fraction. Step 1: Set the repeating decimal equal to a variable, say 'x'. So, x = 0.111.... Step 2: Multiply both sides of the equation by a power of 10 that moves the repeating block to the left of the decimal point. Since one digit repeats, we multiply by 10: 10x = 1.111.... Step 3: Subtract the original equation (x = 0.111...) from the new equation (10x = 1.111...). This results in 9x = 1. Step 4: Solve for x by dividing by 9: x = 1/9. This method, [Student Name], ensures you can always convert these decimals, whether you're calculating a pizza slice percentage or a school budget allocation.

Error Trap — Trap 1

TRAP: Missing the Initial Subtraction MISTAKE: [Student Name] forgets to subtract the original decimal value (x) from the multiplied value (10x or 100x), leading to an incorrect numerator. WHY TEMPTING: The focus is often on the multiplication, and the subtraction step can be overlooked as a mere calculation. SOLUTION: Always write out both equations, e.g., '10x = 2.222...' and 'x = 0.222...', and then clearly perform the subtraction (9x = 2) to ensure accuracy. EXAMPLE: Converting 0.222... to a fraction. Wrong: 10x = 2.222..., so x = 2/10. Right: 10x = 2.222..., x = 0.222..., 9x = 2, so x = 2/9.

Error Trap — Trap 2

TRAP: Incorrect Multiplier for Repeating Block MISTAKE: [Student Name] uses 10 as the multiplier when the repeating block has two digits, or 100 when it has one, resulting in a misaligned subtraction. WHY TEMPTING: It's easy to assume 10 is always the starting point, or to miscount the digits in the repeating block. SOLUTION: Count the number of digits in the repeating block. If one digit repeats (e.g., 0.777...), multiply by 10. If two digits repeat (e.g., 0.454545...), multiply by 100. If three, multiply by 1000, and so on. EXAMPLE: Converting 0.454545... to a fraction. Wrong: 10x = 4.54545..., 9x = 4.1, x = 4.1/9. Right: 100x = 45.4545..., x = 0.454545..., 99x = 45, x = 45/99 (simplifies to 5/11).

Error Trap — Trap 3

TRAP: Mismanaging Non-Repeating Digits MISTAKE: When a decimal has non-repeating digits before the repeating part (e.g., 0.12333...), [Student Name] attempts to apply the standard method directly without isolating the repeating part first. WHY TEMPTING: The general 'multiply by 10s' rule might be over-applied without considering the initial non-repeating section. SOLUTION: First, multiply by a power of 10 to move the non-repeating digits to the left of the decimal. Then, apply the standard two-equation method to the remaining repeating decimal part. EXAMPLE: Converting 0.12333... to a fraction. Wrong: 100x = 12.333..., x = 0.12333..., 99x = 12.21, x = 12.21/99. Right: Let x = 0.12333... First, 10x = 1.2333... Now let y = 1.2333.... 10y = 12.333..., y = 1.2333..., so 9y = 11. y = 11/9. Since 10x = y, x = y/10 = (11/9)/10 = 11/90.

Error Trap — Trap 4

TRAP: Forgetting to Simplify the Fraction MISTAKE: After successfully converting the repeating decimal to a fraction, [Student Name] might forget to reduce it to its simplest form. WHY TEMPTING: The algebraic steps are complete, and the student feels the problem is solved, overlooking the final requirement for simplification. SOLUTION: Always check if the numerator and denominator share any common factors after solving for x. Divide both by their greatest common factor until no further simplification is possible. EXAMPLE: Converting 0.363636... to a fraction. Correct, but unsimplified: 36/99. Right (simplified): 4/11 (divide both by 9).

Error Trap — Trap 5

TRAP: Algebraic Error in Subtraction MISTAKE: When subtracting the two equations (e.g., 10x - x or 100x - x), [Student Name] makes a computational error, especially with carrying or borrowing. WHY TEMPTING: Even simple subtraction can be rushed, or misaligned digits can lead to errors, particularly when decimals are involved. SOLUTION: Write the subtraction vertically, aligning decimal points carefully. Double-check the arithmetic, especially for the whole number part after the repeating decimals cancel out. EXAMPLE: Converting 0.171717... to a fraction. Wrong: 100x = 17.1717..., x = 0.171717... If [Student Name] incorrectly subtracts and gets 99x = 16, then x = 16/99. Right: 99x = 17, so x = 17/99.

Parent ELI5

Hi [Parent Name]! Let's demystify converting repeating decimals to fractions. This skill might seem complex, but it’s quite logical and pops up in unexpected places, like budgeting or understanding sports statistics. Imagine a scenario where a grocery store has a sale: 3 items for $1. You pay $0.333... per item. We want to turn that 'forever' decimal into a neat fraction like 1/3.

Here’s a simple process for a repeating decimal like 0.777...:

  1. Name your decimal: Let 'x' be your repeating decimal. So, x = 0.777...
  2. Shift the repeating part: Look at how many digits repeat. In 0.777..., only '7' repeats (one digit). To move this one repeating digit past the decimal, you multiply both sides of your equation by 10. 10x = 7.777...
  3. Subtract to cancel: Now you have two equations: 10x = 7.777... and x = 0.777.... Subtract the smaller equation from the larger one. The repeating decimal parts perfectly cancel each other out! 10x - x = 7.777... - 0.777... 9x = 7
