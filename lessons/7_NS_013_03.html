Multiplying Rational Numbers

Skill ID: 7.NS.013.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not zero. Multiplying rational numbers means finding the product when you combine two such numbers. The mechanism is straightforward: for fractions, multiply the numerators together and the denominators together. For example, to find the product of (-1/2) and (3/4), we multiply -1 by 3 to get -3 for the numerator, and 2 by 4 to get 8 for the denominator, resulting in -3/8. For decimals, [Student Name] can multiply the numbers as if they were whole numbers, then count the total number of decimal places in the original numbers to place the decimal point in the product. Remember the sign rules from integers: same signs yield a positive product, different signs yield a negative product. Think of it like scaling a recipe: if you need 1/2 of an ingredient that already calls for 3/4 cup, you're multiplying (1/2) * (3/4) = 3/8 cup. This is a crucial skill, [Student Name], as [they] will use it constantly.

Stability — Clear & Structured
Let's calmly and clearly understand how to multiply rational numbers. A rational number is any number that can be written as a fraction, where both the numerator and the denominator are integers, and the denominator is not zero. This includes whole numbers (like 5, which is 5/1), integers (like -3, which is -3/1), fractions (like 2/3), and terminating or repeating decimals (like 0.75, which is 3/4). When we multiply rational numbers, we are essentially finding a 'part of a part' or scaling a quantity. For example, let's multiply (-2/5) by (1/4). First, we multiply the numerators: -2 1 = -2. Next, we multiply the denominators: 5 4 = 20. So, the product is -2/20, which simplifies to -1/10. An application for [Student Name] could be calculating discounts: if a $50 video game is on sale for '1/5 off,' you multiply $50 by (1/5) to find the $10 discount. [Student Name], this predictable process will help [they] solve many problems.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Hey there, [Student Name]! Let's tackle multiplying rational numbers, starting with a problem involving fractions. We'll multiply -3/4 by 1/2. It might look a little tricky with that negative sign, but we'll break it down step-by-step to make sure you understand each part. Remember that multiplying fractions means we'll multiply the top numbers (numerators) together and the bottom numbers (denominators) together. The trickiest part often comes down to managing the signs correctly, but if you remember the rules for multiplying positive and negative numbers, you'll be golden. Let's get started! Problem: Calculate the product of -3/4 and 1/2. 1. 💡 Hint: First, think about the signs. What is a negative number multiplied by a positive number? Also, how do you multiply fractions? Identify the numbers: We have a negative fraction, -3/4, and a positive fraction, 1/2. When multiplying a negative number by a positive number, the result will always be negative. To multiply fractions, we multiply the numerators together and the denominators together. So, (-3/4) (1/2) will give a negative result. 2. 💡 Hint: Now, let's multiply just the top numbers (numerators). Multiply the numerators: The numerators are 3 and 1. 3 1 = 3. 3. 💡 Hint: Next, multiply the bottom numbers (denominators). Multiply the denominators: The denominators are 4 and 2. 4 2 = 8. 4. 💡 Hint: Put the new numerator over the new denominator and remember the sign you determined in Step 1. Can the fraction be simplified? Combine the results and apply the sign: We have a new numerator of 3 and a new denominator of 8. Since we determined the final answer must be negative, the product is -3/8. The fraction 3/8 cannot be simplified further because the greatest common divisor of 3 and 8 is 1. Final Answer: -3/8 5. 💡 Hint: How can you check if your answer is reasonable? Think about decimals or visualizing parts of a whole. Check: We can approximate this using decimals. -3/4 is -0.75, and 1/2 is 0.5. -0.75 0.5 = -0.375. Our answer, -3/8, is also -0.375. The answer matches, confirming our calculation is correct.
Standard
Alright, [Student Name], let's take on another multiplication problem with rational numbers, this time involving a mixed number and a negative fraction. We need to find the product of 2 1/3 and -3/5. The key here is to first make sure all numbers are in a consistent format – improper fractions are usually best for multiplication. Then, we apply the same multiplication rules for fractions and pay close attention to the signs. This problem combines a few important skills you've learned, so let's work through it systematically. Problem: Calculate the product of 2 1/3 and -3/5. 1. Convert mixed numbers to improper fractions: The first number, 2 1/3, needs to be converted. To do this, multiply the whole number by the denominator (2 3 = 6), then add the numerator (6 + 1 = 7). Keep the original denominator, so 2 1/3 becomes 7/3. The second number, -3/5, is already an improper fraction. 2. Multiply the numerators: Now we have (7/3) (-3/5). Let's first consider the absolute values of the numerators, which are 7 and 3. 7 3 = 21. 3. Multiply the denominators: Next, we multiply the denominators, which are 3 and 5. 3 5 = 15. 4. Combine and simplify, applying the sign: We now have 21/15. Since we multiplied a positive number (7/3) by a negative number (-3/5), the final result must be negative. So, the product is -21/15. This fraction can be simplified by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common divisor, which is 3. -21 ÷ 3 = -7 15 ÷ 3 = 5 So, the simplified product is -7/5. This can also be written as a mixed number, -1 2/5. Final Answer: -7/5 (or -1 2/5) 5. Check the answer: Let's convert to decimals for a quick check. 2 1/3 is approximately 2.333. -3/5 is -0.6. 2.333 * -0.6 is approximately -1.3998. Our answer, -7/5, is exactly -1.4. The values are very close, indicating our calculation is likely correct. The slight difference is due to the repeating decimal approximation for 2 1/3.

Here’s how [they] can think about it: 1. Forget the Signs First: Just like when you multiply 2 by 3 to get 6, initially, ignore if the numbers are positive or negative. If you remove (negative operation) a debt (negative value), that's a positive change for your finances! 3. Aqui está como multiplicá-los: 1. Multiplicando Frações: Imagine que [they] tem 1/2 de uma torta e quer comer 1/3 dessa metade. Quanto de torta [they] comeu no total? É simples: multiplique o número de cima (numerador) pelo número de cima, e o número de baixo (denominador) pelo número de baixo. (1/2) (1/3) = (11) / (23) = 1/6. [they] comeu 1/6 da torta original. 2. Multiplicando Decimais: Suponha que [they] quer calcular um desconto de 20% (que é 0,20) em um item de 45,50 dólares. Multiplicando com Números Negativos: Imagine que [they] tem uma dívida de 5 reais por semana (-R$ 5,00) por 3 semanas. Qual é a dívida total? Multiplique os números: 5 3 = 15.
