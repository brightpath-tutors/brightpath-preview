Multiplying Rational Numbers

Skill ID: 7.NS.013.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can multiply positive and negative rational numbers, including fractions, decimals, and mixed numbers, and interpret products in real-world contexts.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Multiplying rational numbers is a fundamental operation that extends your understanding of arithmetic to a broader set of numbers. A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction, [Student Name], where the numerator and denominator are integers, and the denominator is not zero. This includes integers, fractions like 3/4, and decimals such as -0.5 (which is -1/2). To multiply rational numbers, you generally follow a straightforward rule: multiply the numerators together and the denominators together. For example, if you want to calculate (2/3) (-5/7), you multiply 2 by -5 to get -10, and 3 by 7 to get 21. So, (2/3) (-5/7) = -10/21. Remember, the rules for multiplying positive and negative integers still apply: a positive times a negative yields a negative product. Think of multiplication as scaling. If you have a recipe that calls for 3/4 cup of sugar, and you decide to make only 2/3 of the recipe, you're scaling down the ingredient. You're taking 2/3 * (3/4) of sugar. Just as a magnifying glass scales an image, multiplying by a rational number scales the value of another number. This precise method allows [Student Name] to efficiently solve complex problems involving quantities that aren't whole.
Confidence — Encouraging Tone
Remember when you first learned to multiply integers, like 5 3 or even -4 6? You mastered the concept of multiplying numbers and the rules for signs, a skill covered in 7.NS.009. You also already understand rational numbers from 6.NS.007, knowing that numbers like 1/2, -0.75, and 5 can all be written as fractions. Multiplying rational numbers is simply combining these two powerful skills! [Student Name], it's not a brand new idea, but an expansion of what you already know. Imagine you're multiplying -2 (1/4). You already know that a negative times a positive gives a negative result. So, the sign will be negative. Then, you just multiply the numbers: 2 (1/4) = 2/4 = 1/2. Therefore, -2 * (1/4) = -1/2. See? You're using your existing knowledge of multiplying integers and applying it to fractions and decimals. [Student Name], you’ve successfully worked with fractions and decimals in other operations, and multiplying them is often simpler than addition or subtraction because you don't need a common denominator. This is a natural next step in your mathematical journey, and you're well-prepared for it!

Stability — Clear & Structured
Let's establish a clear and consistent method for multiplying rational numbers, [Student Name]. A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction a/b, where 'a' is an integer and 'b' is a non-zero integer. This definition means integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals are all rational numbers. When you multiply two rational numbers, you simply multiply their absolute values and then apply the appropriate sign rule (positive positive = positive; negative negative = positive; positive negative = negative). For fractions, multiply the numerators and multiply the denominators. For decimals, multiply as you would whole numbers, then count the total decimal places in the factors to place the decimal in the product. For example, to multiply (-1/2) (3/5): 1. Multiply the numerators: 1 3 = 3. 2. Multiply the denominators: 2 5 = 10. 3. Determine the sign: A negative number multiplied by a positive number yields a negative result. So, (-1/2) (3/5) = -3/10. An application of this could be calculating changes in temperature. If the temperature is dropping by 2.5 degrees Fahrenheit every hour for 1.5 hours, the total change would be 1.5 (-2.5) degrees. [Student Name] can use this structured approach to confidently tackle any multiplication of rational numbers.

You know that 4 (-3) = -12 and that (-5) (-2) = 10, understanding that two negatives make a positive. Additionally, from 6.NS.007, you became proficient in understanding and comparing rational numbers, which are numbers that can be written as a simple fraction like 3/4 or -0.8. You understand their placement on a number line and their absolute value. Now, we're simply combining these powerful ideas! The process of multiplying rational numbers takes the sign rules you know for integers and applies them to all types of rational numbers – fractions and decimals. When you see something like (-2/3) (1/2), you apply the integer multiplication rule for the sign (negative times positive equals negative), and then you apply the fraction multiplication rule (multiply numerators, multiply denominators). So, (-2/3) (1/2) becomes -(21)/(32) which simplifies to -2/6 or -1/3. The new skill, then, is to seamlessly apply these familiar rules to perform multiplication with any rational number, whether it's -1/4, 0.75, -2, or 3 1/2. If you have a negative number, say -2.5, and you multiply it by 3, you are taking 'three steps' of -2.5. So you start at zero, go -2.5, then another -2.5 (to -5.0), then another -2.5 (to -7.5). It's like flipping the number line around zero for each negative factor.
