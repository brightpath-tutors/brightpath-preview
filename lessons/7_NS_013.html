Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Multiplying rational numbers means finding the product of any two numbers that can be expressed as a fraction p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not zero. This includes all fractions, terminating or repeating decimals (which are fractions with a power of 10 in the denominator), and integers (which can be written as n/1). The mechanism involves two steps: first, multiply the absolute values of the numbers. Second, determine the sign of the product. If both numbers have the same sign (positive × positive, or negative × negative), the product is positive. If they have different signs (positive × negative, or negative × positive), the product is negative. For example, consider (-0.5) × (3/4): First, multiply the absolute values: 0.5 × 0.75 = 0.375. Since one number is negative and one is positive, the product is negative. So, (-0.5) × (3/4) = -0.375. [Student Name], think of multiplying rational numbers like scaling a measurement. If you have 3/4 of a recipe and want to make 0.5 of that reduced recipe, you're finding 0.5 × 3/4. Just like a zoom lens scales an image, multiplication scales numbers. If the scale factor is negative, it also reverses direction. [Student Name], practicing with different types of rational numbers will solidify your understanding.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Let's calmly and clearly understand how to multiply rational numbers. A rational number is any number that can be written as a fraction p/q where p and q are integers, and q is not zero. This includes all integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals. When we multiply rational numbers, we follow two main steps: 1. Multiply their absolute values: Ignore the signs for a moment and multiply the numbers as if they were both positive. 2. Determine the sign of the product: - If both numbers have the same sign (positive × positive, or negative × negative), the product is positive. - If the numbers have different signs (positive × negative, or negative × positive), the product is negative.

Example: Consider (-2/3) × (0.6). 1. Convert 0.6 to a fraction: 6/10 = 3/5. 2. Multiply absolute values: (2/3) × (3/5) = 6/15 = 2/5. 3. Determine the sign: One number is negative (-2/3) and one is positive (0.6), so the product will be negative. 4. Therefore, (-2/3) × (0.6) = -2/5. Application: If [Student Name] needs to calculate the total change in temperature if it drops by 1.5 degrees Fahrenheit each hour for 3.5 hours, [they] would multiply (-1.5) × (3.5) = -5.25 degrees. Sign Rule Slip-up
Mistake:
When multiplying two negative rational numbers, [Student Name] might incorrectly get a negative product. Or, [they] might think a negative and a positive always produce a positive.
Why tempting:
The rules for adding and subtracting positive and negative numbers can feel similar, leading to confusion with multiplication rules.
Correction:
Remember that a negative number multiplied by a negative number always results in a positive number. A negative multiplied by a positive always results in a negative.
Ignoring Cross-Simplification
Mistake:
[Student Name] might multiply fractions straight across, numerators by numerators and denominators by denominators, without first looking for opportunities to simplify.
Why tempting:
It seems like a straightforward step to just multiply everything, but it often leads to larger numbers that are harder to reduce later.
Correction:
Before multiplying, always look for common factors between any numerator and any denominator (even diagonally). Simplify these pairs first to work with smaller, more manageable numbers.
Mixed Number Mayhem
Mistake:
[Student Name] might try to multiply a mixed number (like 2 1/4) by another number without converting it to an improper fraction first. [they] might multiply the whole number part and the fractional part separately.
Why tempting:
It's easy to forget that a mixed number is a sum (2 + 1/4), not an implicit multiplication (2 * 1/4), and treating them separately simplifies part of the calculation.
Correction:
Always convert any mixed numbers into improper fractions before performing multiplication. This ensures the entire rational number is correctly included in the product.
Decimal Place Value Doubt
Mistake:
When multiplying decimals, [Student Name] might incorrectly count the total number of decimal places for the final product, placing the decimal point in the wrong spot.
Why tempting:
Counting decimal places can sometimes be confused with the alignment required for decimal addition or subtraction, where the decimal points line up.
Correction:
Multiply the numbers as if they were whole numbers. Then, count the total number of decimal places in all the factors. The product must have that same total number of decimal places.
Whole Number to Fraction Conversion Flub
Mistake:
When multiplying a whole number by a fraction, [Student Name] might incorrectly multiply the whole number by both the numerator and the denominator, or only by the denominator.
Why tempting:
Without visualizing the whole number as a fraction, it's easy to misapply the multiplication, possibly confusing it with how denominators work in addition or subtraction.
Correction:
Always remember that any whole number can be written as a fraction with a denominator of 1 (e.g., 5 is 5/1). Then, multiply the numerators and denominators as usual.
