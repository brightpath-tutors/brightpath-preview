Rational to Decimal

Skill ID: 7.NS.015.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can convert rational numbers to their decimal form using long division, recognizing whether the decimal terminates or repeats.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction, p/q, where 'p' and 'q' are integers and 'q' is not equal to zero. Converting a rational number to its decimal form involves performing the division indicated by the fraction bar. For example, to convert the rational number 7/8 to a decimal, you divide 7 by 8. Using long division, 7 divided by 8 equals 0.875. This is a terminating decimal because the division ends with a remainder of zero. Another example is 1/6. Dividing 1 by 6 results in 0.1666... This is a repeating decimal, where the digit '6' repeats infinitely. We denote this as 0.16̅. Understanding this process, [Student Name], is like unlocking a coded message: fractions are the code, and decimals are the decoded message. Just as a GPS converts coordinates into a recognizable map location, you are converting one numerical representation into another, revealing its precise value. [they] will apply this division skill directly.
Confidence — Encouraging Tone
Remember how you mastered dividing whole numbers and even integers? That foundational skill, which is part of what you learned in 7.NS.015.01, is exactly what we'll build upon today, [Student Name]. Converting rational numbers to decimals isn't a completely new trick; it's simply applying your existing division knowledge to fractions. Think of a fraction like 3/4 as just another way of writing '3 divided by 4.' You already know how to perform that operation! We're just extending it a bit further into decimals. There's no complex new math here, just a slight adjustment to how you've always approached division. You've successfully divided 15 by 3 to get 5, now we'll learn to divide 3 by 4 to get 0.75. [they] already has the most important tools for this task right in [their] mathematical toolkit. We'll simply use long division to uncover the decimal form, which will feel very familiar.
Engagement — Real-World Hook
Did you know that not all fractions can be perfectly represented by US currency? Imagine trying to split a one-dollar bill equally among three friends. Each friend would get $0.33, but there'd always be one cent left over! This is because 1/3, when converted to a decimal, is 0.333..., a repeating decimal. It never truly ends, so you can't have exactly 1/3 of a dollar with physical coins. This surprising fact, [Student Name], highlights the unique nature of repeating decimals. In sports, like basketball, a player's free-throw percentage might be 2/3, which is 0.666..., often rounded to 66.7%. It’s a rational number, but its decimal form requires a repeating notation or rounding. Even in everyday situations, like sharing a pizza, converting 3/8 of the pizza to a decimal (0.375) helps us visualize exactly how much that is. It shows that some fractions fit neatly, while others, like 1/3, endlessly repeat, creating interesting real-world challenges!

Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Hey [Student Name]! Let's conquer converting fractions to decimals, step by step. Imagine you're sharing 5 slices of a pizza, but there are 8 friends who want an equal share. How much of the whole pizza does each person get as a decimal?<br><br>1. 💡 Hint: Remember, a fraction like 5/8 is just a fancy way of writing a division problem. The top number, the numerator, is what you're dividing, and the bottom number, the denominator, is what you're dividing by. So, 5/8 means 5 divided by 8.<br><br>2. 💡 Hint: Set up your long division. Since 8 doesn't go into 5, you'll need to add a decimal point and a zero to the 5. This lets you think of 5 as 50 tenths. Write '5' inside the division symbol and '8' outside. Place a decimal point after the 5, and a zero to make it 5.0. Put a decimal point directly above the one in 5.0 in the quotient area.<br><br>3. 💡 Hint: Now perform the long division like normal. Keep adding zeros to your number inside the division symbol until you either get a remainder of zero (a terminating decimal) or notice a repeating pattern. Divide 50 by 8. Eight goes into 50 six times (8 x 6 = 48). Subtract 48 from 50, leaving 2. Bring down another zero to make it 20. Eight goes into 20 two times (8 x 2 = 16). Subtract 16 from 20, leaving 4. Bring down another zero to make it 40. Eight goes into 40 five times (8 x 5 = 40). Subtract 40 from 40, leaving 0. You're done!<br><br>4. The decimal equivalent of 5/8 is 0.625.<br><br>Let's check our work: We can multiply 0.625 by 8: 0.625 x 8 = 5. Since we got back to our original numerator, we know our conversion is correct! Standard
[Student Name], let's convert the fraction 2/3 into its decimal form. This is a common fraction you'll see in everyday life, from splitting a candy bar to figuring out how much of a basketball game has passed.<br><br>1. To convert a rational number (a fraction) to a decimal, we simply perform the division indicated by the fraction bar. The numerator (top number) is divided by the denominator (bottom number). So, 2/3 means 2 divided by 3.<br><br>2. Set up your long division. Place 2 inside the division symbol and 3 outside. Since 3 cannot go into 2, we’ll place a 0 in the quotient, add a decimal point after the 2, and then add a zero to make it 2.0. Remember to place a decimal point in the quotient directly above the one in 2.0.<br><br>3. Now, divide 20 by 3. Three goes into 20 six times (3 x 6 = 18). Write 6 in the quotient. Subtract 18 from 20, which leaves a remainder of 2. Bring down another zero to make it 20. Again, 3 goes into 20 six times. You'll notice that this pattern of getting a remainder of 2 and bringing down a zero to make 20 will continue indefinitely. This means the digit '6' will repeat forever.<br><br>4. We represent a repeating decimal by writing the repeating digit or block of digits once with a bar over it. Therefore, 2/3 as a decimal is 0.6 with a bar over the 6, written as 0.\(\\overline{6}\).<br><br>To check this, if we multiply 0.666... (or 2/3) by 3, we get back to 2, confirming our division is correct.
Challenge
Alright, [Student Name], let's tackle converting 7/12 to a decimal. This one requires a bit more careful attention to the division process, as it's not a simple termination or immediate repetition.<br><br>1. We start by recognizing that 7/12 means 7 divided by 12. Set up your long division with 7 inside and 12 outside. Since 12 is larger than 7, place a 0 in the quotient, add a decimal point to 7 to make it 7.0, and put a decimal point in the quotient above it. Common Error: Forgetting to place the initial 0 and decimal point, which can lead to misplacing digits later. Avoid this by always setting up the decimal point in the quotient before starting the division.<br><br>2. Divide 70 by 12. Twelve goes into 70 five times (12 x 5 = 60). Write 5 after the decimal point in the quotient. Subtract 60 from 70, leaving a remainder of 10. Bring down another zero to make it 100. Common Error: Making multiplication errors with larger numbers. Take your time, or quickly jot down multiples of 12 (12, 24, 36, 48, 60, 72, 84, 96, 108...) to ensure accuracy.<br><br>3. Divide 100 by 12. Twelve goes into 100 eight times (12 x 8 = 96). Write 8 in the quotient. Subtract 96 from 100, leaving 4. Bring down another zero to make it 40. Common Error: Stopping too early if you don't immediately see a repeating pattern. Keep going until you get a remainder of 0 or a clear repeat.<br><br>4. Divide 40 by 12. Twelve goes into 40 three times (12 x 3 = 36). Write 3 in the quotient. Subtract 36 from 40, leaving 4. Bring down another zero to make it 40 again. Notice the remainder of 4 repeating? This means the '3' will repeat. So, 7/12 as a decimal is 0.58\(\\overline{3}\). Common Error: Incorrectly putting the bar over more digits than actually repeat. Only the '3' repeats here, not '583'.<br><br>Check: Multiply 0.58333... by 12. (You can approximate with enough threes: 0.58333 x 12 ≈ 6.99996, which is very close to 7, confirming our answer.)
