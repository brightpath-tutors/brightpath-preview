Scaling Comparison

Skill ID: 5.NF.015.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can compare the size of a product to a factor, without calculating, by determining if the fraction multiplier is greater than, less than, or equal to 1.
Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward01998C0

Scaling Comparison is a mathematical concept where you determine if the result of a multiplication problem will be larger than, smaller than, or equal to one of the original numbers (the factor), simply by looking at the fraction you're multiplying by. You don't even need to solve the problem! Think of it like a mathematical 'stretch or shrink' tool. If you multiply a number by a fraction greater than 1, like 3/2, you are essentially 'stretching' that number, making it larger. For example, 10 multiplied by 3/2 results in 15, which is larger than 10. Conversely, if you multiply by a fraction less than 1, such as 1/2, you are 'shrinking' the number, making it smaller. So, 10 multiplied by 1/2 equals 5, which is smaller than 10. And if you multiply by a fraction equivalent to 1, like 2/2, the number stays the same: 10 multiplied by 2/2 is 10. This powerful understanding allows [Student Name] to predict outcomes, strengthening [their] number sense. [Student Name], this helps you understand the effect multiplication has, not just the answer.

First, the definition: Scaling comparison is the process of deciding if a number gets bigger, smaller, or stays the same after you multiply it by a fraction, just by looking at the fraction itself. Second, an example: Let's take the number 20. If we multiply 20 by 4/5, since 4/5 is less than 1, we know the answer will be smaller than 20 (it's actually 16). Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)01998W0

A recipe for apple pie calls for 3/4 cup of sugar. [Student Name] wants to make a batch that is 2/3 of the original recipe size. How much sugar will [they] need? Then, [they] decides to make a batch that is 1 and 1/2 times the original recipe size. How much sugar will [they] need for that?

Step 1: Calculate for 2/3 of the original recipe. 💡 Hint: To find a fraction of a number, we multiply the number by the fraction. Remember how to multiply fractions: multiply the numerators, then multiply the denominators. The original recipe uses 3/4 cup of sugar. [Student Name] wants 2/3 of that amount. So, we multiply 3/4 by 2/3. 3/4 × 2/3 = (3 × 2) / (4 × 3) = 6/12 Check: 6/12 simplifies to 1/2. Since we multiplied by a fraction less than 1 (2/3), our answer (1/2 cup) should be less than the original amount (3/4 cup), which it is (0.5 is less than 0.75).

Step 2: Calculate for 1 and 1/2 times the original recipe. 💡 Hint: First, convert the mixed number to an improper fraction. Then, multiply it by the original amount, just like in Step 1. Now, [Student Name] wants 1 and 1/2 times the original recipe. First, convert 1 and 1/2 to an improper fraction: 1 and 1/2 = 3/2. Now multiply 3/4 cup by 3/2: 3/4 × 3/2 = (3 × 3) / (4 × 2) = 9/8 Check: 9/8 can be written as 1 and 1/8. Since we multiplied by a fraction greater than 1 (3/2), our answer (1 and 1/8 cups) should be greater than the original amount (3/4 cup), which it is (1.125 is greater than 0.75). This demonstrates how multiplying by a fraction less than 1 makes the quantity smaller, while multiplying by a fraction greater than 1 makes the quantity larger.

Standard01998W0

A marathon running track is 26 miles long. For a children's fun run, the track is scaled down to be 1/4 of the marathon distance. How long is the children's fun run track? Later, for a special charity ultra-marathon, the track is scaled up to be 1 and 1/2 times the original marathon distance. How long is the ultra-marathon track?

  1. Calculate the fun run track length. To find the length of the children's fun run track, we need to calculate 1/4 of the original marathon distance. This means we multiply the original distance by the scaling factor. Original distance = 26 miles. Scaling factor = 1/4. Calculation: 26 × 1/4 = 26/1 × 1/4 = (26 × 1) / (1 × 4) = 26/4 miles. Simplifying 26/4 gives us 6 and 2/4 miles, which is 6 and 1/2 miles. Check: Since we multiplied by a fraction less than 1 (1/4), our answer (6 and 1/2 miles) is correctly smaller than the original 26 miles.
  1. Calculate the ultra-marathon track length. Next, [Student Name] needs to find the length of the ultra-marathon track, which is 1 and 1/2 times the original marathon distance. First, convert the mixed number to an improper fraction. Scaling factor = 1 and 1/2 = 3/2. Original distance = 26 miles. Calculation: 26 × 3/2 = 26/1 × 3/2 = (26 × 3) / (1 × 2) = 78/2 miles. Simplifying 78/2 gives us 39 miles. Check: Since we multiplied by a fraction greater than 1 (3/2), our answer (39 miles) is correctly larger than the original 26 miles.
Challenge01998W0

Coach Williams has 18 soccer balls for practice. [Student Name] wants to order enough new soccer balls so that the total number of balls is 1 and 2/3 times the current amount. However, 1/6 of the new total amount ends up being defective upon arrival. How many non-defective soccer balls does Coach Williams have now?

  1. Calculate the desired total amount of soccer balls. Coach Williams starts with 18 soccer balls. The goal is to have 1 and 2/3 times that amount. First, convert the mixed number 1 and 2/3 to an improper fraction: (1 × 3 + 2)/3 = 5/3. Now, multiply the original number of balls by this scaling factor: 18 × 5/3 = 18/1 × 5/3 = (18 × 5) / (1 × 3) = 90/3 = 30 soccer balls. Common Error: Forgetting to convert the mixed number to an improper fraction before multiplying, or trying to multiply the whole number and fraction separately, which can lead to incorrect results. Always convert mixed numbers first!
  1. Calculate the number of defective balls. 1/6 of the new total amount (30 balls) ends up being defective. Multiply the new total by the fraction representing defective balls: 30 × 1/6 = 30/1 × 1/6 = (30 × 1) / (1 × 6) = 30/6 = 5 defective balls. Common Error: Calculating 1/6 of the original 18 balls instead of the new total of 30 balls. Always read carefully to see which quantity the fraction is scaling or taking a part of.
  1. Calculate the final number of non-defective soccer balls. Subtract the number of defective balls from the new total number of balls to find the non-defective amount. 30 (new total) - 5 (defective) = 25 non-defective soccer balls. Check: The number of non-defective balls (25) is greater than the original (18), which makes sense because we initially scaled up, even with some defects.

TRAP: Misidentifying fractions greater than or less than 1 MISTAKE: [Student Name] might incorrectly determine if a fraction is greater than, less than, or equal to 1 by focusing only on the numerator or denominator, rather than their relationship. If the numerator is smaller, the fraction is less than 1. If it's larger, the fraction is greater than 1. If they are equal, the fraction is equal to 1. EXAMPLE: $8 \times 5/4$. TRAP: Overlooking the specific value of the fraction when scaling MISTAKE: [Student Name] might generally know that multiplying by a fraction less than 1 makes a number smaller but not accurately predict how much smaller or larger without careful calculation or comparison to 1. Exam Prep
Overview

To ace Scaling Comparison on exams, [Student Name] needs to know cold that multiplying a number by a fraction greater than 1 results in a larger product, a fraction less than 1 results in a smaller product, and a fraction equal to 1 results in the same product. [they] should be able to quickly identify if any given fraction is greater than, less than, or equal to 1 by comparing its numerator and denominator. Understanding that the word 'of' often signifies multiplication in these contexts is also crucial.

On standardized tests, Scaling Comparison typically appears in multiple-choice questions asking [Student Name] to choose a statement describing the relationship between a product and its factors. Word problems will often require [them] to predict whether a quantity will increase, decrease, or stay the same after scaling, without necessarily calculating the exact answer. These questions assess conceptual understanding, not just computation. [they] might also see questions directly comparing expressions like 'Is $8 \times 3/4$ greater than, less than, or equal to 8?'

Strategy
  1. Fraction Detective When to use: When [Student Name] needs to compare a product to one of its factors without performing the actual multiplication. How to apply: Look closely at the fraction [they] is multiplying by. Is its numerator bigger than, smaller than, or equal to its denominator? This immediately tells [them] if the fraction is greater than, less than, or equal to 1. * Example: To determine if $15 \times 7/5$ is greater than, less than, or equal to 15, observe that $7/5$ has a numerator (7) larger than its denominator (5). This means $7/5 > 1$, so the product will be greater than 15.
  1. Number Line Visualization When to use: To build a conceptual understanding of scaling and to quickly verify predictions about product size. How to apply: Imagine the original number on a number line. If [they] is multiplying by a fraction less than 1 (like $1/4$ or $2/5$), the product will land between 0 and the original number. If by a fraction greater than 1 (like $5/2$ or $9/4$), the product will be beyond the original number. * Example: For $9 \times 1/3$, picture 9 on a number line. Since $1/3 < 1$, the product (3) will be to the left of 9, closer to 0.
  1. Real-World Scenario Check When to use: When [Student Name] faces a word problem or needs to connect the abstract concept of scaling to everyday situations. How to apply: Relate the problem to a familiar context like cooking or building. If [they] needs $1/2$ of a recipe (scaling down), the ingredients will be less. If [they] needs $1 \frac{1}{4}$ times a recipe (scaling up), ingredients will be more. * Example: If a recipe calls for 3 cups of sugar, and [Student Name] wants to make $2/3$ of the recipe, [they] will use $3 \times 2/3 = 2$ cups. This is less than 3 cups, confirming that multiplying by a fraction less than 1 makes the quantity smaller.
Memory Hook
  1. The Fraction Referee What it helps remember: How a fraction acts like a referee, deciding if the product gets bigger, smaller, or stays the same when multiplying. Example with real numbers: Think of the fraction [Student Name] is multiplying by as a referee for the number 1. If the fraction is 'less than 1,' the referee makes the product smaller. If 'greater than 1,' the referee makes the product bigger. If 'equal to 1,' the product stays the same. So, for $12 \times 1/4$, the '1/4 referee' (less than 1) shrinks 12 down to 3. For $12 \times 3/2$, the '3/2 referee' (greater than 1) makes 12 grow to 18.
  1. The Fraction's Finger What it helps remember: Which direction the product moves relative to the original number on a number line. Example with real numbers: Imagine the fraction pointing a finger at the number 1. If its finger points left (less than 1), the product moves left (gets smaller). If its finger points right (greater than 1), the product moves right (gets bigger). If its finger points straight up (equal to 1), the product stays right where it is. For $20 \times 4/5$, the '4/5 finger' points left (since $4/5 < 1$), so the product (16) is less than 20.
