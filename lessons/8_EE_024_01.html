Equations with Distribution

Skill ID: 8.EE.024.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Equations with Distribution are linear equations where one or more terms involve a factor multiplied by an expression inside parentheses, requiring the distributive property as a fundamental step towards finding the unknown variable. Precisely, it's about transforming an expression like a(b + c) into ab + ac within an equation. For example, consider the equation 5(2x - 3) + 7 = 42. To solve this, [Student Name], your first step is to distribute the 5: 10x - 15 + 7 = 42. Then, combine like terms: 10x - 8 = 42. From there, you isolate the variable using inverse operations, just like in simpler equations. This process is similar to a mail carrier delivering mail: each piece (the outer factor) must be delivered to every mailbox (each term inside the parentheses). If a carrier only delivers to the first house, some mail goes undelivered, just as if you only multiply the outer factor by the first term, you'll get an incorrect result. [Student Name], mastering this ensures every part of the expression is accounted for, leading you to the correct solution.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Equations with Distribution are mathematical statements where two expressions are set equal, and one or both expressions contain terms that require the distributive property to simplify. The distributive property allows us to multiply a single term by each term inside a set of parentheses. For instance, in 2(4x - 6) = 20, the first step is to distribute the 2 to both 4x and -6. This yields 8x - 12 = 20. Once this step is complete, [Student Name], the equation transforms into a familiar two-step linear equation. You would then add 12 to both sides: 8x = 32, and finally, divide both sides by 8 to find x = 4. This methodical process ensures you simplify the equation correctly before isolating the variable. In real-world applications, [Student Name], this property helps calculate budgets for a school dance, where 2($15 + $8) might represent tickets and decorations costs for two groups, simplifying to 2 $15 + 2 $8.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hey [Student Name]! Let's walk through solving an equation with distribution step-by-step. Remember, distribution means multiplying the number outside the parentheses by every term inside. Take a look at this problem: 3(x - 5) + 7 = 16. We need to figure out what x is.

  1. 💡 Hint: Start by sharing the number outside the parentheses with everything inside. First, distribute the 3 to each term within the parentheses. So, 3 x becomes 3x, and 3 -5 becomes -15. The equation now looks like this: 3x - 15 + 7 = 16.
  1. 💡 Hint: Now, tidy up the side with the variable by combining any simple numbers. Next, combine the like terms on the left side of the equation. We have -15 and +7. Adding these together gives us -8. So, our equation simplifies to: 3x - 8 = 16.
  1. 💡 Hint: To get the variable term by itself, undo any addition or subtraction by doing the opposite operation on both sides. To isolate the 3x term, we need to get rid of the -8. The opposite of subtracting 8 is adding 8. We must do this to both sides of the equation to keep it balanced: 3x - 8 + 8 = 16 + 8. This simplifies to 3x = 24.
  1. 💡 Hint: Finally, to solve for the variable, undo any multiplication or division. Now, x is being multiplied by 3. To find x, we perform the opposite operation, which is dividing by 3. We divide both sides by 3: 3x / 3 = 24 / 3. This gives us x = 8.

CHECK: Let's make sure our answer is correct! Substitute x = 8 back into the original equation: 3(8 - 5) + 7 = 16. Inside the parentheses, 8 - 5 is 3. So, 3(3) + 7 = 16. This becomes 9 + 7 = 16, which is 16 = 16. It checks out! You did great, [Student Name]!

Standard

Let's solve another equation, [Student Name]! This time, we'll tackle 5(2y + 4) - 12 = 38. Remember the goal is to isolate the variable y and find its value. We'll follow a standard process, keeping the equation balanced as we go.

  1. Our first step is to apply the distributive property. The 5 outside the parentheses needs to be multiplied by each term inside: 2y and 4. So, 5 2y becomes 10y, and 5 4 becomes 20. The equation transforms into: 10y + 20 - 12 = 38.
  1. Next, we need to simplify the left side of the equation by combining any like terms. In this case, we have the constant terms +20 and -12. Adding these together (20 - 12) gives us 8. So, our equation is now more concise: 10y + 8 = 38.
  1. Now, we want to start isolating the term with our variable, 10y. To do this, we need to undo the addition of 8. The inverse operation of adding 8 is subtracting 8. We must subtract 8 from both sides of the equation to maintain balance: 10y + 8 - 8 = 38 - 8. This simplifies our equation to: 10y = 30.
  1. Finally, y is being multiplied by 10. To solve for y, we perform the inverse operation, which is division. We divide both sides of the equation by 10: 10y / 10 = 30 / 10. This yields our solution: y = 3.

CHECK: To confirm our answer, substitute y = 3 back into the original equation: 5(23 + 4) - 12 = 38. First, 23 is 6. So, 5(6 + 4) - 12 = 38. Inside the parentheses, 6 + 4 is 10. This makes it 5(10) - 12 = 38. Then, 50 - 12 = 38. Finally, 38 = 38. Our solution is correct!

Challenge

Alright, [Student Name], let's tackle a trickier equation that includes distribution and variables on both sides: 2(3z - 6) + 4 = 5z + 10. This one requires careful attention to avoid common pitfalls.

  1. Distribute Carefully: Begin by distributing the 2 to every term inside the parentheses. 2 3z gives 6z, and 2 -6 gives -12. The equation becomes 6z - 12 + 4 = 5z + 10. A common error here is to only multiply 2 by 3z and forget about the -6. Always check that you've distributed to all terms within the parentheses.
  1. Combine Like Terms (One Side at a Time): Before moving terms across the equals sign, simplify each side independently. On the left side, we have -12 + 4, which combines to -8. The equation is now 6z - 8 = 5z + 10. A common mistake is trying to combine 6z with 5z at this stage, or -8 with 10. Remember, you can only combine terms that are on the same side of the equals sign and are of the same type (variables with variables, constants with constants).
  1. Gather Variable Terms: Now, let's get all the z terms onto one side. It's usually easier to move the smaller variable term. So, we'll subtract 5z from both sides: 6z - 5z - 8 = 5z - 5z + 10. This simplifies to z - 8 = 10. An error to avoid here is adding 5z instead of subtracting, which would create 11z and 0z on the other side, making the problem harder or incorrect.
  1. Gather Constant Terms: With the z term isolated on one side, move the constant term -8 to the other side. Add 8 to both sides: z - 8 + 8 = 10 + 8. This leaves us with z = 18. Incorrectly subtracting 8 instead of adding would lead to z = 2, which is wrong.

CHECK: Substitute z = 18 into the original equation: 2(318 - 6) + 4 = 518 + 10. 2(54 - 6) + 4 = 90 + 10. 2(48) + 4 = 100. 96 + 4 = 100. 100 = 100. It’s correct!

Distributing Only to the First Term
Mistake:
[Student Name] might distribute the number outside the parentheses to only the first term inside, forgetting to multiply it by all subsequent terms within the parentheses.
Why tempting:
It's easy to stop after the first multiplication, especially when focusing on the variable term, leading to an incomplete distribution.
Correction:
Remember the distributive property means multiplying the outside number by every term within the parentheses. Each term gets a 'delivery' from the outside factor.
Forgetting Sign Changes with Negative Distribution
Mistake:
When distributing a negative number or a subtraction sign (which implies distributing -1), [Student Name] often forgets to change the sign of all terms inside the parentheses.
Why tempting:
It's common to correctly multiply the numbers but neglect to apply the negative sign consistently, especially a negative times a negative.
Correction:
Treat the negative sign as part of the number being distributed. Multiply the outside number and its sign by each term inside the parentheses, remembering that a negative times a negative equals a positive.
Combining Unlike Terms Before Distributing
Mistake:
[Student Name] might try to simplify terms inside the parentheses by combining them, even when they are unlike terms (like a variable and a constant), before applying the distributive property.
Why tempting:
The order of operations (PEMDAS/GEMDAS) emphasizes simplifying inside parentheses first. However, this only applies if terms are 'like' and can actually be combined.
Correction:
If terms within the parentheses are not like terms (e.g., '3x' and '7'), they cannot be combined. In such cases, you must apply the distributive property first to remove the parentheses.
Distributing Past the Parentheses
Mistake:
[Student Name] might mistakenly distribute a factor to terms that are outside the parentheses and are added or subtracted, treating them as if they were inside.
Why tempting:
The visual proximity of the number to the entire expression might lead to an incorrect assumption that it multiplies everything on that side of the equation.
Correction:
The distributive property only applies to the terms directly adjacent to and multiplying the expression within the parentheses. Other terms on that side of the equation are separate and should not be included in the distribution.
Ignoring an Implied -1 When a Negative Sign is Outside Parentheses
Mistake:
When an expression like -(x + 3) appears, [Student Name] might only change the sign of the first term, resulting in -x + 3 instead of -x - 3.
Why tempting:
The absence of a visible number can be confusing. Students might forget that a negative sign in front of parentheses implies a factor of -1.
Correction:
Always remember that a negative sign directly preceding parentheses implies distributing -1 to every term inside. Multiply each term by -1, changing all their signs.

Strategy
Distribute and Conquer
When:
Use this whenever you see a number or variable directly outside parentheses, indicating multiplication of the terms within.
How:

Your first step should always be to apply the distributive property. Multiply the term outside by every single term inside the parentheses. This eliminates the parentheses and transforms the equation into a simpler form, like a two-step or multi-step equation without grouping symbols.

Example:
For 4(y - 6) = 28, immediately rewrite it as 4y - 24 = 28. This simplifies the equation significantly, making it easier to solve for 'y'.
Sign-Check Sweep
When:
Apply this strategy any time you distribute a negative number or a subtraction sign (which implies distributing -1) into parentheses.
How:

After performing the distribution, quickly 'sweep' through the new terms. Mentally verify if a negative multiplied by a negative correctly resulted in a positive term, and a negative multiplied by a positive resulted in a negative term. This rapid check helps catch frequent sign errors.

Example:
If you distributed -3(x - 9), confirm that you correctly obtained -3x + 27, not -3x - 27. Missing this plus sign is a common trap.
Combine Like Terms Early and Often
When:
This strategy is crucial after distribution and before you start isolating the variable, or if you have multiple like terms on one side of the equation.
How:

Once you've distributed, look for any like terms (terms with the same variable raised to the same power, or constants) on each side of the equation. Combine them before you begin moving terms across the equals sign. This reduces the number of terms you have to work with, making subsequent steps cleaner and less prone to error.

Example:
In 7x + 12 - 3x = 48, combine 7x and -3x to get 4x + 12 = 48 before subtracting 12 from both sides.
Memory Hook
The Pizza Delivery Rule
When:
This helps [Student Name] remember that the number outside the parentheses must be 'delivered' (multiplied) to every single 'topping' (term) inside the parentheses.
Example:
Think of 5(x + 2) = 35. The '5' is the delivery person, and it needs to deliver to 'x' AND to '2'. So, it's not just 5x + 2, but 5x + 10 = 35. Every customer gets their pizza!
Negative Nancy Changes Everyone's Mood
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] remember to change the sign of all terms inside the parentheses when a negative number is being distributed.
Example:
Imagine '-2' is Negative Nancy outside the parentheses: -2(3 - y) = 14. Nancy interacts with '3' to make it '-6', and with '-y' to make it '+2y'. She changes both their 'moods' (signs). So, -6 + 2y = 14, not -6 - 2y = 14.
