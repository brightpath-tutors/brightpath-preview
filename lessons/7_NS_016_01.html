Terminating vs Repeating

Skill ID: 7.NS.016.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can identify and differentiate between terminating and repeating decimal expansions of rational numbers.
Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward02524C0

Terminating decimals are those whose decimal expansion ends, meaning the division results in a remainder of zero. For example, the fraction 3/8, when converted to a decimal by dividing 3 by 8, yields exactly 0.375. The digits '3', '7', and '5' appear, and then the decimal terminates. In contrast, repeating decimals are those whose decimal expansion continues infinitely, but with a specific sequence of digits repeating endlessly. Consider the fraction 2/11. Dividing 2 by 11 gives 0.181818..., where the block of digits '18' repeats indefinitely. This is often written as 0.18 with a bar over the '18'. The mechanism for this behavior lies in the prime factorization of the fraction's denominator in its simplest form. If the prime factors of the simplified denominator are only 2s and/or 5s, like 3/8 (denominator 8 = 2x2x2), the decimal will terminate. If other prime factors are present, such as 2/11 (denominator 11), the decimal will repeat because the division algorithm's remainders will eventually cycle. Thinking of it like a mathematical 'elevator' that either reaches the ground floor (terminates) or gets stuck infinitely looping between floors (repeats) can help, [Student Name]. This fundamental distinction helps [Student Name] classify how rational numbers behave as decimals.

Stability — Clear & Structured02524C3

Let's calmly break down terminating and repeating decimals. First, a terminating decimal is a decimal that ends after a finite number of digits. Imagine a sprint race where the finish line is clearly marked; once you cross it, the race is over. For example, when you convert the fraction 1/4 into a decimal, you get 0.25. The decimal expansion stops after two digits. There are no more digits to consider. Second, a repeating decimal is a decimal that continues infinitely, but with a pattern of one or more digits that repeats without end. Think of a song on repeat: it never truly ends, it just plays the same segment over and over. A common example is the fraction 5/6. Dividing 5 by 6 yields 0.8333... Here, the digit '3' repeats indefinitely. We can write this as 0.83 with a bar over the '3'. Understanding these types is crucial when [Student Name] needs to convert fractions to decimals or interpret data. It helps [Student Name] know if a number can be expressed perfectly or if it will always have an ongoing pattern, such as when dealing with money or measurements.

Focusing Only on the Numerator
Mistake:
[Student Name] might mistakenly think a fraction like 1/3 is terminating because the numerator '1' is a simple, ending number. [they] ignores the denominator's role in determining the decimal type.
Why tempting:
It's often easier to look at the top number rather than perform division or analyze the denominator.
Correction:
The nature of the decimal (terminating or repeating) is primarily determined by the prime factors of the denominator after the fraction is simplified. Always consider the full fraction.
Not Simplifying Fractions First
Mistake:
[Student Name] might try to divide 6/12 or analyze its denominator (12 = 2^2 * 3) and incorrectly conclude it's a repeating decimal due to the prime factor 3.
Why tempting:
It seems like an extra, unnecessary step, and the goal is just to identify the decimal type.
Correction:
Always simplify the fraction to its lowest terms before examining the denominator's prime factors or performing division to accurately determine if it terminates or repeats.
Incorrect Prime Factorization of Denominator
Mistake:
[Student Name] might see a denominator like 15 and incorrectly think its only relevant prime factor is 5 (missing 3), leading [them] to believe the fraction is terminating.
Why tempting:
Prime factorization requires careful breakdown, and it's easy to miss a prime factor or stop too early.
Correction:
Carefully find all prime factors of the denominator. If any prime factor other than 2 or 5 exists, the decimal is repeating; otherwise, it's terminating.
Premature Conclusion in Long Division
Mistake:
When performing long division, if the remainder doesn't immediately become zero after a few steps, [Student Name] might prematurely conclude it's repeating, even if it eventually terminates.
Why tempting:
Long division can be tedious, and sometimes patterns appear early that are not the true repeating block or a zero remainder.
Correction:
Continue dividing until the remainder is definitively zero (indicating a terminating decimal) or a remainder repeats, clearly establishing the repeating block.
Confusing Long Terminating with Repeating
Mistake:
[Student Name] might encounter a terminating decimal with many digits, such as 0.0625, and assume it must be repeating because it looks 'long' or complex.
Why tempting:
Long strings of digits after the decimal point can visually trick students into thinking there's an ongoing, infinite pattern.
Correction:
A terminating decimal has a finite number of digits after the decimal point; it eventually ends. A repeating decimal has a block of digits that explicitly repeats infinitely.

Strategy
The 'Denominator's Prime' Rule
Example:
For 7/25, the denominator 25 = 5^2. Only 5s, so it terminates (0.28). For 5/12, the denominator 12 = 2^2 × 3. The '3' means it repeats (0.4166...).
Simplify First, Always!
Example:
6/15 simplifies to 2/5. Denominator 5 means it terminates (0.4). Without simplifying, 15 (3×5) would incorrectly suggest repeating.
Long Division Pattern Check
Example:
7/9 = 0.777... (remainder 7 repeats). 3/8 = 0.375 (remainder 0).
Memory Hook
Example:
For 3/20, the denominator 20 = 2 × 2 × 5. Since only 'Two and Five Stay Alive,' 3/20 terminates (0.15). For 1/14, the denominator 14 = 2 × 7. Since 7 is not 2 or 5, it's repeating (0.071428...).
Example:
Think of 1/4 = 0.25. It stops, like a stop sign. Think of 2/3 = 0.666... It keeps going, never stopping. So, 0.25 is 'Terminal Stop Sign,' and 0.666... is not.
