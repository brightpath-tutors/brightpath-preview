Finding the Percent

Skill ID: 6.RP.014.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can calculate the percentage a part represents of a whole quantity using division and multiplication.
Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Finding the percent means determining what portion a specific part makes up of a given whole, and then expressing that proportion as a value "per hundred." It's a way to standardize comparisons. Precisely, you are converting a ratio of "part to whole" into an equivalent ratio with 100 as the second term. The mechanism involves two steps, [Student Name]. First, you divide the part by the whole to get a decimal. For instance, if you scored 18 points on a 20-point quiz, you'd divide 18 by 20. This gives you 0.9. The second step is to multiply this decimal by 100 and add the percent symbol. So, 0.9 multiplied by 100 is 90, making your quiz score 90%. Think of it like a mathematical analogy to converting a score like "18 out of 20" into a standardized "grade out of 100." This consistent process helps [Student Name] easily understand proportional relationships.

Confidence — Encouraging Tone

You're closer to finding percentages than you think, [Student Name]! You’ve already built a strong foundation with ratios and fractions. Remember when you learned about understanding ratios (6.RP.014.02)? You know that if 3 out of 4 friends like pizza, that's a ratio of 3:4. You also mastered interpreting fractions as division (5.NF.009) – so you know 3/4 means 3 divided by 4, which is 0.75. Finding the percent simply builds on these awesome skills! It's like taking your existing ratio, converting it to a decimal using division, and then just multiplying that decimal by 100 to express it "per hundred." So, 0.75 becomes 75%. [they] can absolutely do this because [they] already understands the core ideas. This isn't entirely new; it’s a natural extension of what [they] already knows.

Engagement — Real-World Hook

Did you know that understanding percentages can actually help your favorite US sports teams make crucial decisions, or even help you snag a great deal at the mall? It's true! Take baseball, for instance. A player's batting average, which is essentially a percentage, helps coaches decide who plays. If a player gets 12 hits out of 40 at-bats, how do you find their batting percentage? We use the same skill! You divide the hits (12) by the at-bats (40), which gives you 0.3. Then, [Student Name], you multiply by 100 to get 30%. This means the player hits 30% of the time. This skill is vital beyond sports too, like when you see a "25% off" sign at Target for a $40 shirt. That 25% tells [Student Name] exactly how much [they] saves.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Finding the percent is a straightforward process to understand what part of a whole is represented by a specific number, expressed on a scale of 100. It answers the question, "What percent is this part of that whole?" Definition: To find the percent, you always divide the "part" by the "whole" and then multiply the resulting decimal by 100. Example: Imagine [Student Name] has $12 in [their] piggy bank and [they] wants to buy a new $30 action figure. To find what percent of the action figure's cost [they] has saved, we set up the calculation: $12 (part) ÷ $30 (whole) = 0.40. Then, we multiply the decimal by 100: 0.40 × 100 = 40%. So, [Student Name] has saved 40% of the money needed. Application: This method is consistently used for calculating grades (e.g., if you get 15 out of 20 questions right, that's 15 ÷ 20 = 0.75, or 75%), figuring out discounts, or understanding statistics.

When you divided 7 by 10 to get 0.7, you effectively found the decimal representation of that part-to-whole relationship. The bridge to a percentage is simply scaling that decimal to a base of 100. The new skill involves one final, easy step: multiplying that decimal by 100 and adding the percent symbol. For the pizza preference example, after calculating 7 ÷ 10 = 0.7, you multiply 0.7 by 100 to get 70. Add the percent symbol, and you have 70%! Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello, [Student Name]! Let's break down how to find a percent with a real-world example. Imagine your school's basketball team played 25 games this season. [they] won 17 of those games. What percentage of their games did the team win?

  1. 💡 Hint: What is the 'part' and what is the 'whole'? We want to know the percent of games won (part) out of the total games played (whole). First, identify the numbers we need. The 'part' is the number of games won, which is 17. The 'whole' is the total number of games played, which is 25.
  1. 💡 Hint: To find what part 17 is of 25, we can write it as a fraction. Fractions are just division problems! Next, we'll write the part as a fraction of the whole. This looks like 17/25. To convert this fraction to a decimal, divide the numerator by the denominator: 17 ÷ 25 = 0.68.
  1. 💡 Hint: Remember that 'percent' means 'per one hundred'. To change a decimal to a percent, you need to multiply it by 100. Finally, convert the decimal to a percentage by multiplying it by 100. So, 0.68 × 100 = 68. Add the percent symbol. The team won 68% of their games.

Check: Does 68% of 25 equal 17? We can check by calculating 0.68 × 25 = 17. Yes, it does! So, the answer is correct.

  1. Form a fraction and convert it to a decimal. Place the part over the whole to create a fraction: 42/60. To convert this fraction into a decimal, divide the numerator by the denominator: 42 ÷ 60 = 0.7.
  1. Convert the decimal to a percentage. To express a decimal as a percentage, multiply it by 100. So, 0.7 × 100 = 70. Remember to add the percent symbol.

Check: To verify our answer, we can calculate 70% of $60. Convert 70% back to a decimal (0.70) and multiply: 0.70 × $60 = $42. Since this matches the amount you saved, our calculation is correct.

Analogical Thinking — Analogy

[Student Name], finding a percent is a lot like thinking about a whole dollar bill, which is 100 cents. Let's say you have a $5 bill in your pocket. That's your 'whole.' If you spend $1 on a sticker, that $1 is the 'part' you used. To find what percentage of your $5 you spent, you ask: 'How many cents out of 100 cents is that $1, if my whole money was 100 cents?'

You take the part ($1) and divide it by the whole ($5): $1 ÷ $5 = 0.20. Now, imagine this 0.20 as '20 cents' if your whole dollar was just 1 unit. Since a percent is out of 100, you multiply by 100: 0.20 × 100 = 20. So, you spent 20% of your money. The $5 bill is the total, the $1 is the piece, and 20% tells you what portion that piece is, scaled to 100.

Analogical Thinking — Visual

[Student Name], picture 'Finding the Percent' as looking at a long bar, like a candy bar that represents a total amount. Let's say this whole candy bar is 100 units long, which represents 100%.

Now, imagine you have a smaller piece of something. You want to see how big that smaller piece is compared to the entire candy bar. You first figure out how many 'units' your smaller piece would take up if the entire thing was 1 unit long. This is like dividing your 'part' by your 'whole' to get a decimal. For example, if you have 12 apples out of 50 total apples, you're finding out how much of the 'whole 50' those 12 apples take up. (12 ÷ 50 = 0.24).

Then, you stretch or shrink that decimal piece to fit into the '100 units' long candy bar. So, 0.24 stretched to a 100-unit bar becomes 24 units. That '24 units' is your percentage: 24%. You're literally re-sizing your piece to compare it to a standard '100-unit' total.
