Rational Number Word Problems

Skill ID: 7.NS.017.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can solve real-world word problems involving addition and subtraction of positive and negative rational numbers.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Rational Number Word Problems are narrative challenges where the quantities you need to add or subtract are rational numbers. These numbers can be integers, fractions like 1/2, or decimals like 0.75. The key mechanism involves reading the problem carefully, identifying the rational numbers and the implied operation (addition or subtraction), setting up a real equation, and then solving it. For instance, if a diver is at -12.5 feet relative to the surface and then descends another 7.25 feet, the problem asks you to find [Student Name]'s new depth. You'd set up the equation: -12.5 + (-7.25) = -19.75 feet. This is like building a multi-story structure. Each floor represents a rational number, and going up or down (addition/subtraction) changes your position in the entire building. Just as you understand how each floor connects, you'll understand how rational numbers combine to solve these real-world scenarios, [Student Name]. These problems test your ability to translate everyday situations into precise mathematical language and then apply your knowledge of rational number operations to find the correct answer, ensuring [Student Name] masters the analytical process.

Stability — Clear & Structured
Rational Number Word Problems are simply real-world scenarios described in text that require you to add or subtract rational numbers (like fractions, decimals, or integers) to find a solution. The process is predictable: identify the numbers, determine the operation, and then calculate. For example: A baker starts with 3/4 of a pound of flour. [they] uses 1/2 of a pound for a cake. How much flour is left? Here, the rational numbers are 3/4 and 1/2. The word 'uses' implies subtraction. So, the equation is 3/4 - 1/2. To solve, you find a common denominator: 3/4 - 2/4 = 1/4. So, the baker has 1/4 of a pound of flour left. This skill is incredibly useful in daily life, [Student Name]. For instance, managing your allowance, tracking temperature changes throughout the day in a science project, or even figuring out recipe adjustments when cooking a meal, all involve working with these types of problems. [Student Name], mastering this helps you confidently navigate practical situations where quantities aren't always whole numbers.

Standard
[Student Name], let's work through a problem that involves scaling recipes using rational numbers. It's a common situation you might encounter in the kitchen! Problem: A recipe for chocolate chip cookies calls for 2 1/4 cups of flour. If [they] wants to make 1 1/2 times the recipe for the school bake sale, how much flour will [they] need? Also, the recipe requires 3/4 cup of brown sugar. How much brown sugar will be needed for the scaled recipe? 1. Identify initial quantities and the scaling factor. The original recipe requires 2 1/4 cups of flour and 3/4 cup of brown sugar. We want to make 1 1/2 times the recipe. So, our scaling factor is 1 1/2. 2. Convert all mixed numbers to improper fractions. To make multiplication easier, we'll convert the mixed numbers. The scaling factor 1 1/2 becomes 3/2. The original flour amount 2 1/4 becomes 9/4 (since 2 x 4 + 1 = 9). 3. Calculate the new quantities by multiplying each original amount by the scaling factor. For flour: Multiply the original amount by the scaling factor: (9/4) (3/2) = (9 3) / (4 2) = 27/8 cups. Convert this improper fraction back to a mixed number: 27 divided by 8 is 3 with a remainder of 3, so that's 3 3/8 cups of flour. For brown sugar: Multiply the original amount by the scaling factor: (3/4) (3/2) = (3 3) / (4 2) = 9/8 cups. Convert this to a mixed number: 9 divided by 8 is 1 with a remainder of 1, so that's 1 1/8 cups of brown sugar. 4. State the final answer. [Student Name] will need 3 3/8 cups of flour and 1 1/8 cups of brown sugar for the scaled recipe. Check: To verify our flour calculation, if we take 3 3/8 cups (27/8) and divide by the scaling factor 1 1/2 (3/2), we should get the original amount: (27/8) Ã· (3/2) = (27/8) * (2/3) = 54/24 = 9/4, which is 2 1/4 cups. This confirms our calculations are correct.
Challenge
Let's tackle a more challenging multi-step problem involving rational numbers, [Student Name]. This one requires careful attention to the order of operations and interpreting keywords. Problem: A small business owner, Mr. Harrison, starts the month with $3,500.00 in [their] business account. During the month, [they] had the following transactions: [they] paid monthly rent of $1,250.00, purchased supplies for $485.75, and deposited earnings of $2,100.00. At the end of the month, [they] decided to set aside 1/5 of the remaining balance for a special marketing campaign and then had a bank fee of $15.50 automatically deducted. What is the final available balance in Mr. Harrison's account after all these transactions and savings? 1. Calculate the balance after initial income and expenses. Initial balance: $3,500.00 Total expenses (rent + supplies): $1,250.00 + $485.75 = $1,735.75 Deposited earnings: $2,100.00 Current balance = $3,500.00 - $1,735.75 + $2,100.00 = $3,864.25. Common Error: A common mistake here is to incorrectly combine positives and negatives or to perform operations in the wrong order. It's best to group all additions and all subtractions, or to process them chronologically. 2. Calculate the amount set aside for the marketing campaign. The problem states 1/5 of the remaining balance is set aside. The remaining balance at this point is $3,864.25. Amount for marketing: (1/5) $3,864.25 = $772.85. New balance after setting aside funds = $3,864.25 - $772.85 = $3,091.40. Common Error: Students often multiply 1/5 by the initial balance instead of the current balance, or they might mistakenly add the marketing fund to the balance. The phrase "of the remaining" is crucial; it refers to the balance at that moment. 3. Apply the final bank fee. The bank fee of $15.50 is deducted from the current balance. Final available balance = $3,091.40 - $15.50 = $3,075.90. Mr. Harrison's final available balance in the account is $3,075.90. Check: Let's trace it back: $3,500 (start) - $1,250 - $485.75 + $2,100 = $3,864.25. Then, taking 4/5 (or 1 - 1/5) of that balance: $3,864.25 (4/5) = $3,091.40. Finally, subtract the fee: $3,091.40 - $15.50 = $3,075.90. The logic holds.

Error Trap — Trap 1
TRAP: Subtracting a Negative Incorrectly MISTAKE: When a problem describes a 'decrease by a negative value' or 'change of -X', students might incorrectly subtract the absolute value instead of recognizing that subtracting a negative is equivalent to adding a positive. WHY TEMPTING: The word 'decrease' or 'change' often signals subtraction, leading students to overlook the double negative. SOLUTION: Always write out the full expression, including the sign of the number being subtracted. Remember that a minus sign followed by a negative number becomes addition. EXAMPLE: A submersible's depth changes from -10.5 feet by 'decreasing -2.5 feet'. Wrong: -10.5 - 2.5 = -13.0 feet Right: -10.5 - (-2.5) = -10.5 + 2.5 = -8.0 feet
Error Trap — Trap 2
TRAP: Mixing Fraction and Decimal Forms MISTAKE: [Student Name] might try to perform operations directly on rational numbers presented in different forms (e.g., adding a fraction and a decimal) without first converting them to a common format. WHY TEMPTING: Students might rush, or not recognize the need for conversion, or lack fluency in converting between forms. SOLUTION: Before performing any arithmetic, convert all rational numbers in the problem to either all fractions or all decimals. Choose the form that makes the calculation simplest. EXAMPLE: A recipe calls for 1/2 cup of milk and 0.75 cups of water. How much liquid in total? Wrong: 1/2 + 0.75 (no direct sum, student might guess 1.25 or 0.8) Right: Convert 1/2 to 0.5: 0.5 + 0.75 = 1.25 cups. OR Convert 0.75 to 3/4: 1/2 + 3/4 = 2/4 + 3/4 = 5/4 cups.
Error Trap — Trap 3
TRAP: Misinterpreting 'Of' for Multiplication MISTAKE: When a problem uses phrases like 'a fraction OF a quantity' or 'a percentage OF an amount', [Student Name] may incorrectly add or subtract instead of multiplying. WHY TEMPTING: The word 'of' can sometimes be associated with part-whole thinking, leading to addition or subtraction when multiplication is required for finding a part of a given amount. SOLUTION: Recognize that 'of' in these contexts specifically means multiplication. If you're finding a fraction or percentage of a total, it's always a multiplication operation. EXAMPLE: A school track team ran 3/4 of a 2.5-mile course. How far did they run? Wrong: 2.5 + 3/4 = 2.5 + 0.75 = 3.25 miles (or 2.5 - 3/4 = 1.75 miles) Right: 3/4 2.5 = 0.75 2.5 = 1.875 miles
Error Trap — Trap 4
TRAP: Incorrect Order of Operations with Negative Rational Numbers MISTAKE: [Student Name] might misapply the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS), especially when dealing with negative fractions or decimals, leading to incorrect calculations. WHY TEMPTING: The presence of multiple operations and negative signs can make it easy to perform operations out of sequence, particularly confusing addition/subtraction with multiplication/division. SOLUTION: Strictly follow the order of operations: Parentheses/Brackets, Exponents/Orders, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). Treat negative numbers as signed values throughout. EXAMPLE: Calculate the result of -5.2 + 0.5 (-3.0). Wrong: (-5.2 + 0.5) (-3.0) = (-4.7) (-3.0) = 14.1 Right: -5.2 + (0.5 -3.0) = -5.2 + (-1.5) = -6.7
Error Trap — Trap 5
TRAP: Ignoring Contextual Clues for Signs MISTAKE: [Student Name] often struggles to correctly translate real-world contexts like 'debt', 'below sea level', 'loss', or 'temperature drop' into negative rational numbers within their mathematical model. WHY TEMPTING: Students might focus on the magnitude of the number and ignore the directional or comparative meaning implied by the words, treating all values as positive. SOLUTION: Carefully read the problem for keywords that indicate direction or position relative to a zero point. Assign negative signs to values representing decreases, losses, or positions below zero. EXAMPLE: A diver is 12.75 meters below the surface. [they] then ascends 4.5 meters. What is [their] new depth relative to the surface? Wrong: 12.75 + 4.5 = 17.25 meters (incorrectly treating initial depth as positive or ignoring context) Right: -12.75 + 4.5 = -8.25 meters (meaning 8.25 meters below the surface)
