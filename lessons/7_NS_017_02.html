TRAP: Subtracting a Negative Incorrectly MISTAKE: When a problem describes a 'decrease by a negative value' or 'change of -X', students might incorrectly subtract the absolute value instead of recognizing that subtracting a negative is equivalent to adding a positive. WHY TEMPTING: The word 'decrease' or 'change' often signals subtraction, leading students to overlook the double negative. SOLUTION: Always write out the full expression, including the sign of the number being subtracted. Remember that a minus sign followed by a negative number becomes addition. EXAMPLE: A submersible's depth changes from -10.5 feet by 'decreasing -2.5 feet'. Wrong: -10.5 - 2.5 = -13.0 feet Right: -10.5 - (-2.5) = -10.5 + 2.5 = -8.0 feet

TRAP: Mixing Fraction and Decimal Forms MISTAKE: [Student Name] might try to perform operations directly on rational numbers presented in different forms (e.g., adding a fraction and a decimal) without first converting them to a common format. WHY TEMPTING: Students might rush, or not recognize the need for conversion, or lack fluency in converting between forms. SOLUTION: Before performing any arithmetic, convert all rational numbers in the problem to either all fractions or all decimals. Choose the form that makes the calculation simplest. EXAMPLE: A recipe calls for 1/2 cup of milk and 0.75 cups of water. How much liquid in total? Wrong: 1/2 + 0.75 (no direct sum, student might guess 1.25 or 0.8) Right: Convert 1/2 to 0.5: 0.5 + 0.75 = 1.25 cups. OR Convert 0.75 to 3/4: 1/2 + 3/4 = 2/4 + 3/4 = 5/4 cups.

TRAP: Misinterpreting 'Of' for Multiplication MISTAKE: When a problem uses phrases like 'a fraction OF a quantity' or 'a percentage OF an amount', [Student Name] may incorrectly add or subtract instead of multiplying. WHY TEMPTING: The word 'of' can sometimes be associated with part-whole thinking, leading to addition or subtraction when multiplication is required for finding a part of a given amount. SOLUTION: Recognize that 'of' in these contexts specifically means multiplication. If you're finding a fraction or percentage of a total, it's always a multiplication operation. EXAMPLE: A school track team ran 3/4 of a 2.5-mile course. How far did they run? Wrong: 2.5 + 3/4 = 2.5 + 0.75 = 3.25 miles (or 2.5 - 3/4 = 1.75 miles) Right: 3/4 2.5 = 0.75 2.5 = 1.875 miles

TRAP: Incorrect Order of Operations with Negative Rational Numbers MISTAKE: [Student Name] might misapply the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS), especially when dealing with negative fractions or decimals, leading to incorrect calculations. WHY TEMPTING: The presence of multiple operations and negative signs can make it easy to perform operations out of sequence, particularly confusing addition/subtraction with multiplication/division. SOLUTION: Strictly follow the order of operations: Parentheses/Brackets, Exponents/Orders, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). Treat negative numbers as signed values throughout. EXAMPLE: Calculate the result of -5.2 + 0.5 (-3.0). Wrong: (-5.2 + 0.5) (-3.0) = (-4.7) (-3.0) = 14.1 Right: -5.2 + (0.5 -3.0) = -5.2 + (-1.5) = -6.7

TRAP: Ignoring Contextual Clues for Signs MISTAKE: [Student Name] often struggles to correctly translate real-world contexts like 'debt', 'below sea level', 'loss', or 'temperature drop' into negative rational numbers within their mathematical model. WHY TEMPTING: Students might focus on the magnitude of the number and ignore the directional or comparative meaning implied by the words, treating all values as positive. SOLUTION: Carefully read the problem for keywords that indicate direction or position relative to a zero point. Assign negative signs to values representing decreases, losses, or positions below zero. EXAMPLE: A diver is 12.75 meters below the surface. [they] then ascends 4.5 meters. What is [their] new depth relative to the surface? Wrong: 12.75 + 4.5 = 17.25 meters (incorrectly treating initial depth as positive or ignoring context) Right: -12.75 + 4.5 = -8.25 meters (meaning 8.25 meters below the surface)

How to apply: After calculating, visualize the problem on a number line. Start at the initial value. 'Move' right for adding a positive or subtracting a negative.   1. Strategy: 'Unit and Form Consistency' When to use: Whenever a problem involves mixed units (e.g., inches and feet) or different forms of rational numbers (e.g., fractions and decimals). Memory Hook
  1. Hook: 'Keep, Change, Flip for Division' What it helps remember: The correct procedure for dividing fractions, which is often a source of error in word problems involving sharing or splitting rational amounts.
