One-Step Subtraction Equations

Skill ID: 6.EE.016.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can solve one-step subtraction equations by using inverse operations to isolate the variable.
Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

A one-step subtraction equation is an algebraic statement where an unknown value, represented by a variable, is related to known numbers through a single subtraction operation, and it can be solved in just one mathematical step. The goal is to find the value of the variable that makes the equation true. For example, in the equation x - 15 = 20, 'x' is the unknown we need to find. To solve these equations, [Student Name], we use the principle of inverse operations. Subtraction's inverse is addition. So, if we have a number being subtracted from our variable, we add that same number to both sides of the equation. This keeps the equation balanced, much like a balanced scale: if you add 5 pounds to the left side, you must add 5 pounds to the right to maintain equilibrium. In x - 15 = 20, adding 15 to both sides gives us x - 15 + 15 = 20 + 15, which simplifies to x = 35. So, [Student Name], [they] found that 35 - 15 = 20. This precise method guarantees the correct solution.

Stability — Clear & Structured

A one-step subtraction equation is an algebraic problem where a single subtraction operation connects a variable to two known numbers. The objective is to determine the specific numerical value of the variable that satisfies the equation. For example, x - 8 = 15 is a one-step subtraction equation where 'x' is the unknown quantity. Let's solve x - 8 = 15. Our goal is to get 'x' by itself on one side of the equation. Since 8 is being subtracted from 'x', the inverse operation is addition. We add 8 to both sides to maintain the equation's balance: x - 8 + 8 = 15 + 8 This simplifies to x = 23. To check your answer, [Student Name], substitute 23 back into the original equation: 23 - 8 = 15, which is true! Imagine [Student Name] had a stack of baseball cards, gave away 8 of them, and now has 15 cards left. The equation x - 8 = 15 helps [they] find the original number of cards. This structured approach helps [Student Name] systematically solve these equations.

Bridge — Connects Prior Knowledge

[Student Name], you're already familiar with the fundamental building blocks of algebra from 6.EE.016.01. You know that a variable, often 'x', is a symbol that stands for an unknown number. You've practiced creating and interpreting expressions, like 10 - x or x - 3. These expressions represent mathematical phrases without an equals sign; they describe a quantity or a relationship, but they don't state a final result for the unknown. For instance, x - 3 simply means "three less than some number." Now, we're going to take those expressions and give them a purpose! When we add an equals sign and a specific value to an expression, it transforms into an equation. For example, changing the expression x - 3 into the equation x - 3 = 7 means we're no longer just describing; we're posing a question: "What number, when you subtract 3 from it, gives you 7?" This 'equals' sign is the bridge, telling us that what's on one side has the same value as what's on the other. This leads us directly to our new skill: solving one-step subtraction equations. To answer that question, we use inverse operations. Since 3 is being subtracted from 'x', we perform the inverse: add 3 to both sides of the equation. So, x - 3 + 3 = 7 + 3 simplifies to x = 10. You, [Student Name], have now gone from understanding what a variable is to actively finding its value, a powerful step in your mathematical journey!

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello, [Student Name]! Let's solve a common type of equation you'll see. Imagine you had some trading cards. You decided to sell 28 of them at the school's annual 'Trading Post' event. Now you have 75 trading cards left. How many cards did you have to begin with? We can write this as an equation: x - 28 = 75. Let's break it down step-by-step.

  1. 💡 Hint: The goal is to get 'x' by itself. What's currently happening to 'x'? How can we undo that action? We have x - 28 = 75. The 'x' is having 28 subtracted from it. To 'undo' subtraction, we use the inverse operation, which is addition. We must add 28 to both sides of the equation to keep it balanced. This is like making sure a seesaw stays level. x - 28 + 28 = 75 + 28
  1. 💡 Hint: Now, perform the operations on each side. What happens when you subtract 28 and then add 28? On the left side, -28 + 28 cancels out, leaving just 'x'. On the right side, 75 + 28 equals 103. x = 103
  1. 💡 Hint: So, what does your answer for 'x' mean in the original problem? This means you originally had 103 trading cards.
  1. 💡 Hint: It’s always a good idea to check your answer. Substitute your 'x' value back into the original equation. Let's check our work: Substitute x = 103 into x - 28 = 75. 103 - 28 = 75 75 = 75 Since both sides are equal, our answer is correct! You started with 103 cards.
Standard

Let's solve a one-step subtraction equation, [Student Name]. Imagine a local bakery started its day with a certain number of donuts. By lunchtime, they had sold 45 donuts. If they now have 62 donuts left, how many did they have at the start of the day? We can represent this situation with the equation: d - 45 = 62, where 'd' is the initial number of donuts.

  1. Understand the Equation: The equation d - 45 = 62 means that 'd' (our unknown starting number of donuts) minus 45 (the number sold) equals 62 (the number remaining).
  1. Isolate the Variable: Our goal is to find the value of 'd'. To do this, we need to get 'd' by itself on one side of the equation. Since 45 is being subtracted from 'd', we perform the opposite, or inverse, operation: addition. We add 45 to both sides of the equation to maintain balance. d - 45 + 45 = 62 + 45
  1. Calculate the Result: On the left side, -45 + 45 cancels out, leaving just 'd'. On the right side, 62 + 45 equals 107. d = 107
  1. Check Your Answer: To make sure our solution is correct, we substitute '107' back into the original equation for 'd'. 107 - 45 = 62 62 = 62 Since both sides are equal, we know that the bakery started with 107 donuts.
Challenge

Alright, [Student Name], let's tackle a slightly trickier problem. Suppose the school's drama club is putting on a play. They need to raise $450 to cover costumes and props. They've already held two bake sales, earning $95 from the first and $110 from the second. They decided to hold a car wash, but then realized they still needed to subtract money for cleaning supplies, which cost $25. If 'x' is the amount they earned before paying for supplies, and their final car wash profit was $120, how much did they raise from the car wash before supplies? The equation for the car wash part is x - 25 = 120.

  1. Identify the Unknown and Knowns: We need to find 'x', the gross earnings from the car wash. We know $25 was subtracted for supplies, and $120 was the net profit. (Common Error: Don't get distracted by the $450, $95, $110 amounts; those are for the overall play budget, not for solving this specific car wash equation.)
  1. Isolate 'x' using Inverse Operations: To find 'x', we must undo the subtraction of $25. The inverse operation is addition. Add 25 to both sides of the equation. x - 25 + 25 = 120 + 25 (Common Error: Accidentally subtracting 25 from both sides again. Remember, you want to cancel out the operation next to 'x', not repeat it.)
  1. Calculate the Value of 'x': Simplify both sides. On the left, -25 + 25 is 0, leaving 'x'. On the right, 120 + 25 is 145. x = 145 (Common Error: Making a calculation mistake in the addition, like 120 + 25 = 155. Double-check your arithmetic!)
  1. Check Your Solution: Substitute x = 145 back into the original equation: 145 - 25 = 120. This simplifies to 120 = 120, which is true. So, the drama club raised $145 from the car wash before paying for supplies.

Confusing Inverse Operations
Mistake:
Students mistakenly use subtraction instead of addition (or vice versa) to isolate the variable when solving one-step subtraction equations. They might subtract the constant from the other side, even when they should be adding.
Why tempting:
The presence of a subtraction sign in the equation might lead them to instinctively think 'do the opposite' as 'subtract from the other side', rather than 'add the number being subtracted'.
Correction:
Remember that to undo subtracting a number from the variable, you must add that same number to both sides of the equation to maintain balance and isolate the variable.
Incorrect Order for Subtrahend Variable
Mistake:
When the variable itself is being subtracted from a constant (e.g., 20 - x = 8), students often subtract the result from the constant, or incorrectly add, leading to errors in the sign of x or the value.
Why tempting:
It feels natural to always move numbers to the other side to 'solve for x', but the negative sign attached to x is often overlooked or mishandled in the process.
Correction:
For equations like 20 - x = 8, either add x to both sides first (giving 20 = 8 + x, then subtract 8 from both sides) or conceptualize it as 'what number, when subtracted from 20, leaves 8?'
Forgetting to Check the Solution
Mistake:
Students solve for the variable and move on without verifying if their answer makes the original equation true, missing an easy opportunity to catch calculation errors.
Why tempting:
Confidence in their computation or a desire to finish quickly can lead [Student Name] to skip this crucial verification step.
Correction:
Always take your calculated value for x and substitute it back into the original equation. If both sides are equal, you know your answer is correct.
Calculation Errors with Integers
Mistake:
Even if the setup for solving the equation is correct, students might make arithmetic errors when dealing with positive and negative numbers during the inverse operation step.
Why tempting:
Working with negative numbers can be tricky, and a quick mental calculation might lead to mistakes, especially when crossing zero or combining numbers with different signs.
Correction:
Slow down when performing arithmetic involving integers. Use a number line, rules for adding/subtracting signed numbers, or write out the steps clearly to avoid simple calculation errors.
Misinterpreting Subtraction of a Negative
Mistake:
Students might confuse x - (-a) with x - a, failing to recognize that subtracting a negative number is equivalent to adding a positive number.
Why tempting:
The presence of two negative signs (- -) can be visually confusing, and the rule for 'subtracting a negative' might not be fully ingrained.
Correction:
Always simplify expressions with double negatives first. Remember that 'minus a negative' immediately becomes 'plus a positive' before you apply inverse operations.

Parent ELI5

Bonjour [Parent Name] ! Vous voulez maîtriser les équations de soustraction à une étape pour mieux aider [Student Name] (ou pour vous-même !), [they] est au bon endroit. Imaginez une situation quotidienne : vous avez acheté un billet pour un match des Yankees de New York à 85 $. Après la sortie, il vous reste 40 $ sur les 100 $ que vous aviez emportés. Combien vous reste-t-il exactement après le match pour d'autres achats ? C'est le genre de problème qu'une équation de soustraction peut résoudre. Voici les étapes :

  1. Identifier l'inconnu : Dans notre exemple, vous savez que vous aviez 100 $, vous avez dépensé un certain montant (l'inconnu, appelons-le 'x'), et il vous reste 40 $. L'équation est donc 100 - x = 40. Si c'est plutôt 'x moins un nombre égal un autre nombre', comme 'x - 60 = 40', cela signifie 'j'avais une somme X, j'ai dépensé 60 $, il me reste 40 $'.
  2. Utiliser l'opération inverse : Pour isoler 'x', nous faisons l'opération contraire de celle qui est à côté de 'x'. Si vous avez x - 60 = 40, l'inverse de la soustraction est l'addition. Donc, ajoutez 60.
  3. Appliquer aux deux côtés : C'est la règle d'or ! Quoi que [they] fasse d'un côté de l'équation, [they] doit le faire de l'autre pour maintenir l'équilibre. Donc, pour x - 60 = 40, [they] ajoute 60 des deux côtés : x - 60 + 60 = 40 + 60. Ce qui simplifie en x = 100.
  4. Résoudre et vérifier : Calculez le résultat. Dans notre exemple, 'x' est 100. Pour vérifier, remplacez 'x' dans l'équation originale : 100 - 60 = 40. C'est correct ! [Parent Name], [they] va maîtriser ce concept et pourra enseigner à [Student Name] en toute confiance.
