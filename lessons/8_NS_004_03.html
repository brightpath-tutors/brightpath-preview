Repeating Decimal to Fraction

Skill ID: 8.NS.004.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Converting a repeating decimal to a fraction means expressing a non-terminating, but repeating, decimal number as a common fraction, typically in the form a/b where a and b are integers and b is not zero. A repeating decimal, like 0.1666... (where the 6 repeats indefinitely), represents a rational number. The mechanism involves an elegant algebraic trick. Suppose we want to convert 0.1666... into a fraction. First, let x = 0.1666... . To isolate the repeating part, we multiply by powers of 10. Multiplying by 10 gives 10x = 1.666... . Multiplying by 100 gives 100x = 16.666... . Now, we subtract the equations strategically to eliminate the repeating part: 100x - 10x = 16.666... - 1.666... . This simplifies to 90x = 15. Solving for x, we get x = 15/90, which simplifies to 1/6. Just as a telescope allows us to see distant stars by magnifying them, this algebraic method 'magnifies' the repeating decimal to reveal its hidden fractional form. [Student Name], this systematic approach works for any repeating decimal, allowing [them] to precisely define what seemingly goes on forever.

Stability — Clear & Structured

A repeating decimal is a decimal number that has a digit or a block of digits that repeat indefinitely after the decimal point. For example, 0.272727... is a repeating decimal where '27' repeats. To convert such a decimal to a fraction, we follow a consistent algebraic process. Let's take 0.272727... as an example. First, we define a variable: Let x = 0.272727... . Next, we multiply x by a power of 10 that shifts the repeating block to the left of the decimal. Since two digits repeat, we multiply by 100: 100x = 27.272727... . Now, we subtract our original equation (x) from this new one: 100x - x = 27.272727... - 0.272727... . This simplifies nicely to 99x = 27. Finally, solve for x by dividing: x = 27/99. [Student Name], this fraction can be simplified by dividing both numerator and denominator by 9, giving x = 3/11. This method provides a reliable way to express any repeating decimal as an exact fraction, which is crucial for exact calculations, not just approximations, [they] might need for a science project.

Misidentifying the Repeating Block
Mistake:
[Student Name] might assume a repeating decimal like 0.123123... has only 3 repeating, or 0.12333... has 23 repeating, due to misinterpreting the notation or pattern.
Why tempting:
The visual representation of repeating decimals, especially with the bar notation, can be confusing, making it easy to incorrectly pinpoint which digits actually repeat.
Correction:
Carefully observe the vinculum (the bar) over the digits. The sequence of digits directly under the bar is the repeating block. If there's no bar, identify the first sequence of digits that repeats infinitely.
Incorrect Powers of Ten for Alignment
Mistake:
When converting 0.123123..., [Student Name] might multiply by 100 instead of 1000 to align the repeating part, leading to an inexact subtraction and a non-terminating difference.
Why tempting:
It's easy to miscount the number of digits in the repeating block, especially with longer blocks, resulting in an incorrect choice for the power of 10 needed to shift the repeating part.
Correction:
Count the exact number of digits in the repeating block. If there are 'n' repeating digits, multiply the original decimal by 10^n (after isolating any non-repeating digits) to ensure one full block shifts to the left for clean subtraction.
Forgetting to Simplify the Fraction
Mistake:
After correctly converting a repeating decimal to a fraction, [Student Name] might present a fraction like 18/99 instead of its simplified form, losing points on an assignment or test.
Why tempting:
The conversion process can feel like the primary task, and students might consider the problem 'solved' once a fractional form is achieved, overlooking the essential final step of simplification.
Correction:
Always check if the numerator and denominator of the resulting fraction share any common factors. Divide both by their greatest common divisor (GCD) to ensure the fraction is in its simplest, irreducible form.
Incorrectly Handling Non-Repeating Digits
Mistake:
For a mixed repeating decimal like 0.2777..., [Student Name] might try to subtract equations where the non-repeating digit prevents the repeating parts from perfectly canceling out.
Why tempting:
Students often get accustomed to problems where the repeating part starts immediately after the decimal, and they forget the crucial initial step of moving any non-repeating digits past the decimal point.
Correction:
First, multiply the original decimal x by a power of 10 to move all non-repeating digits to the left of the decimal. Let this be equation A. Then, multiply x by a larger power of 10 to move one full repeating block further left. Let this be equation B. Subtract equation A from equation B.
Arithmetic Errors During Subtraction
Mistake:
When subtracting equations like 100x - 10x or 1000x - x, [Student Name] might make a simple arithmetic mistake with the whole numbers on the right side of the equation.
Why tempting:
The algebraic setup can be the main focus, causing students to rush or overlook careful calculation during the final subtraction, especially with borrowing or larger numbers.
Correction:
Always double-check the subtraction for both the coefficients of x and the constant terms. Write out the numbers vertically if needed, ensuring decimal points are aligned and that the repeating decimal parts cancel out completely.

Strategy
The 'Align and Subtract' Method
When:
For any repeating decimal, especially those with both non-repeating and repeating parts (e.g., 0.12333...).
How:

Let x equal the decimal. First, multiply x by a power of 10 (e.g., 10^a) to move all non-repeating digits to the left of the decimal. Then, multiply x by a larger power of 10 (e.g., 10^(a+b)) to move one full repeating block to the left, ensuring repeating parts align. Subtract the two equations and solve for x.

Example:
For 0.123123..., set x = 0.123123.... 1000x = 123.123123.... Subtract x from 1000x to get 999x = 123. Thus, x = 123/999 = 41/333.
The 'Quick Pure Repeater' Rule
When:
Specifically for purely repeating decimals (e.g., 0.d d d... or 0.ab ab ab...) where the repeating block starts immediately after the decimal point.
How:

For 0.d d d..., the fraction is d/9. For 0.ab ab ab..., the fraction is ab/99. The numerator is the repeating block, and the denominator is a number consisting of 'n' nines, where 'n' is the number of digits in the repeating block. Always simplify!

Example:
0.777... is 7/9. 0.454545... is 45/99, which simplifies to 5/11. This rule provides a shortcut for direct conversion.
The 'Check Your Work with a Calculator' Trick
When:
After you've completed a conversion and want to ensure your answer is correct, especially in a testing environment where calculators are allowed.
How:

Divide your resulting fraction (numerator by denominator) using a calculator. The decimal display should match the original repeating decimal. This can quickly catch arithmetic errors or issues with simplification.

Example:
If you converted 0.1666... to 1/6. On your calculator, input 1 ÷ 6. The display should show 0.16666666... (or 0.1 with a bar over the 6), verifying your work.
Memory Hook
The 'Nine Denominators' Trick
When:
How the number of repeating digits relates to the denominator of the fraction for purely repeating decimals.
Example:
Think of a basketball player, [Student Name], who shoots a perfect 0.333... from the free-throw line. That's 3 (the repeating digit) over 9 (one nine for one repeating digit), simplified to 1/3. If [they] had a 0.151515... streak, that's 15 (the repeating block) over 99 (two nines for two repeating digits), simplifying to 5/33. Just count the repeating digits, put them over that many nines, and simplify!
'Slide and Subtract' for Mixed Decimals
When:
The two-step multiplication process for decimals with non-repeating digits before the repeating part.
Example:
Imagine a high school track race. For 0.1666..., you have the non-repeating digit 1 (like a runner waiting at the start line) and the repeating 6 (the runner on the track). First, 'slide' the 1 past the decimal by multiplying x by 10 (10x = 1.666...). This gets everyone ready. Then, 'slide' another repeating 6 past by multiplying x by 100 (100x = 16.666...). Now, 'subtract' these two 'aligned' equations (100x - 10x = 16.666... - 1.666...) to make the repeating 6s disappear, leaving 90x = 15. Solve for x = 15/90 = 1/6.
