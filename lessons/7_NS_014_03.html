Dividing Rational Numbers

Skill ID: 7.NS.014.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Dividing rational numbers means splitting a quantity into equal parts, where those parts can be fractions, decimals, or integers. Precisely, it's finding how many times one rational number (the divisor) fits into another (the dividend). The key mechanism is transforming division into multiplication: to divide by a rational number, you multiply by its reciprocal. For example, to solve 5/6 ÷ (-2/3), we rewrite it as 5/6 × (-3/2). First, find the reciprocal of -2/3, which is -3/2. Then, multiply the numerators (5 × -3 = -15) and the denominators (6 × 2 = 12). This gives us -15/12, which simplifies to -5/4. Think of it like a mathematical “flip and multiply” dance! If [Student Name] needs to share 2.5 pounds of apples evenly among 5 friends, [they] performs 2.5 ÷ 5, which is the same as 2.5 × (1/5). This method makes dividing all rational numbers, whether positive or negative, straightforward. [Student Name], practicing this transformation is crucial for mastering division.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Dividing rational numbers is a consistent process that relies on a simple, predictable rule. Definition: Dividing a rational number by another non-zero rational number is equivalent to multiplying the dividend by the reciprocal of the divisor. The reciprocal of a fraction a/b is b/a. For decimals, you can convert them to fractions or perform standard division. Remember that a whole number, like 7, can be written as 7/1. Example: Let's divide 3/8 by -1/4. 1. Identify the dividend (3/8) and the divisor (-1/4). 2. Find the reciprocal of the divisor: the reciprocal of -1/4 is -4/1. 3. Change the operation from division to multiplication: 3/8 × (-4/1). 4. Multiply: (3 × -4) / (8 × 1) = -12/8. 5. Simplify the result: -12/8 reduces to -3/2. So, 3/8 ÷ (-1/4) = -3/2. Application: If [Student Name] has 7.5 gallons of paint and each wall requires 1.5 gallons, [they] can figure out how many walls [they] can paint by calculating 7.5 ÷ 1.5, which is 5 walls. This systematic approach ensures accurate results every time, [Student Name].

Bridge — Connects Prior Knowledge

Welcome, [Student Name]! Before we dive deeper, let's quickly review our past knowledge. You've become skilled at multiplying rational numbers (7.NS.014.01), understanding that multiplying two negative numbers yields a positive product, like (-3) × (-5) = 15, and multiplying a positive and a negative yields a negative product, like 4 × (-2) = -8. You also know that properties of operations, such as the commutative and associative properties, apply to rational numbers (7.NS.014.02), which helps you simplify expressions. These skills, like handling signs and fractions, are vital building blocks.

Now, let's connect that strong foundation to division. Division is essentially the inverse operation of multiplication. When we divide, we’re asking, 'What number, when multiplied by the divisor, gives us the dividend?' Because of this inverse relationship, we can transform any division problem into a multiplication problem using the reciprocal. For example, 10 ÷ 2 = 5 because 5 × 2 = 10. Similarly, 10 ÷ (1/2) = 20 because 20 × (1/2) = 10. This idea of the reciprocal is the bridge we walk across.

Your new skill is applying this bridge: to divide rational numbers, you will 'Keep, Change, Flip'. Keep the first rational number (the dividend), change the division sign to a multiplication sign, and flip the second rational number (the divisor) to its reciprocal. Then, you simply apply your excellent multiplication skills, including handling the signs. So, [Student Name], dividing -3/4 by 1/2 becomes -3/4 × 2/1 = -6/4 = -3/2. You're simply leveraging what you already know!

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello [Student Name]! Let's tackle dividing rational numbers step-by-step. Imagine you need to figure out the result of dividing a negative mixed number by a positive fraction: (-5 1/4) ÷ (3/8). This problem combines fractions, mixed numbers, and negative signs, but we'll break it down.

  1. 💡 Hint: Before you can divide mixed numbers, you need to change them into improper fractions. Think about how many fourths are in 5 whole numbers. First, convert the mixed number -5 1/4 into an improper fraction. Keep the negative sign for now and attach it to the final result of the proper fraction. 5 wholes times 4 fourths per whole equals 20 fourths. Add the 1/4, so you have 21 fourths. So, -5 1/4 becomes -21/4.
  1. 💡 Hint: Remember the rule for dividing fractions: 'Keep, Change, Flip' (KCF). Keep the first fraction, change the division to multiplication, and flip (find the reciprocal of) the second fraction. Now, we apply the KCF rule. Keep the first fraction (-21/4) as it is. Change the division sign (÷) to a multiplication sign (×). Flip the second fraction (3/8) to its reciprocal, which is 8/3. Our new problem is: (-21/4) × (8/3).
  1. 💡 Hint: Multiply the numerators and multiply the denominators. Before multiplying, look for opportunities to cross-simplify to make your numbers smaller. Multiply the fractions. We have a negative times a positive, so our final answer will be negative. Cross-simplify: 4 goes into 8 two times, and 3 goes into 21 seven times. So, (-7/1) × (2/1). Multiply straight across: (-7 × 2) / (1 × 1) = -14/1, which simplifies to -14.

To check your answer: Multiply your quotient (-14) by the divisor (3/8). (-14) × (3/8). Simplify: (-7) × (3/4) = -21/4. This matches our original dividend, -21/4, so our answer -14 is correct. You did great, [Student Name]!

Standard

Let's work through a standard division problem involving decimals, [Student Name], such as (10.8) ÷ (-2.4). Dividing decimals can sometimes feel a bit different from fractions, but the principles of rational numbers still apply, especially regarding signs.

  1. First, determine the sign of your answer. You are dividing a positive number (10.8) by a negative number (-2.4). Remember that when you divide numbers with different signs, the answer is always negative. So, our final answer will be negative.
  1. Next, to make the division easier, convert the divisor into a whole number. You can do this by moving the decimal point to the right. If you move the decimal point one place to the right in -2.4, it becomes -24. You must do the same to the dividend, 10.8, which then becomes 108. Now the problem is 108 ÷ 24 (we already determined the negative sign, so we can focus on the absolute values for the calculation).
  1. Perform the long division: 108 ÷ 24. Think about how many times 24 fits into 108. 24 × 4 = 96, and 24 × 5 = 120. So, 24 fits into 108 exactly 4 times with a remainder. 108 - 96 = 12. To continue, add a decimal point and a zero to the dividend (108.0), bringing down the zero to make 120. Now, how many times does 24 fit into 120? Exactly 5 times (24 × 5 = 120). So, 108 ÷ 24 = 4.5.
  1. Finally, apply the sign we determined in step 1. Since our answer must be negative, the result is -4.5.

To check your work: Multiply the quotient (-4.5) by the divisor (-2.4). (-4.5) × (-2.4). A negative times a negative equals a positive. 4.5 × 2.4 = 10.8. This matches our original dividend, confirming our answer of -4.5.

Challenge

Let's tackle a more challenging problem, [Student Name], combining different types of rational numbers and operations: (-1/3 + 5/6) ÷ (-0.75). This requires careful steps and awareness of common pitfalls.

  1. First, simplify the expression inside the parentheses: (-1/3 + 5/6). You need a common denominator to add these fractions. The least common multiple of 3 and 6 is 6. So, rewrite -1/3 as -2/6. Now, (-2/6 + 5/6). Add the numerators: -2 + 5 = 3. So, the sum is 3/6. This simplifies to 1/2. Common Error to Avoid: Forgetting to find a common denominator or incorrectly converting one of the fractions. Always double-check your equivalent fractions!
  1. Next, convert the decimal divisor (-0.75) into a fraction. 0.75 is equivalent to 75/100, which simplifies to 3/4. Since it was -0.75, it becomes -3/4. Common Error to Avoid: Misconverting decimals to fractions, for instance, thinking 0.75 is 7.5/10 or not simplifying the fraction. Practice common decimal-to-fraction conversions like 0.25=1/4, 0.5=1/2, 0.75=3/4.
  1. Now, we have (1/2) ÷ (-3/4). Apply the 'Keep, Change, Flip' (KCF) rule. Keep the first fraction (1/2). Change the division sign to multiplication (×). Flip the second fraction (-3/4) to its reciprocal, which is -4/3. Our new problem is: (1/2) × (-4/3). Common Error to Avoid: Flipping the first fraction instead of the second, or forgetting the negative sign when flipping. The sign stays with the reciprocal!
  1. Finally, multiply the fractions. (1/2) × (-4/3). A positive times a negative results in a negative answer. Multiply numerators: 1 × (-4) = -4. Multiply denominators: 2 × 3 = 6. So, the result is -4/6. Simplify this fraction by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common factor, 2. The final answer is -2/3. Common Error to Avoid: Not simplifying the final fraction to its lowest terms. Always check if you can simplify!
