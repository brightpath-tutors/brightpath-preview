Plotting Rational Numbers

Skill ID: 6.NS.019.04  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can accurately plot positive and negative rational numbers, including fractions and decimals, on a number line.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Plotting rational numbers means precisely locating their exact position on a number line. A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not zero. This includes all integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals. To plot a rational number like 1/2, you divide the unit segment between 0 and 1 into two equal parts and mark the first division. For a negative rational number, like -1.75, you'd move left from 0, past -1, and then three-quarters of the way to -2. Imagine a number line as a meticulously measured road; plotting a rational number is like finding a specific address, even if it’s between two main cross streets. For example, to plot the number 5/4 on a number line, we can rewrite it as the mixed number 1 and 1/4. This tells us to locate the number between 1 and 2, specifically at the first quarter mark after 1. [Student Name], understanding this precision is key to higher-level math. [they] will apply this concept extensively in future topics.

Stability — Clear & Structured
Plotting rational numbers simply means marking their exact position on a number line. A rational number is any number that can be written as a fraction, which includes integers, fractions, and decimals that terminate or repeat. This process helps us visualize the magnitude and order of these numbers. To do this, we first identify the integers that the rational number falls between. Then, we divide that specific segment of the number line into equal parts based on the denominator of the fraction or the decimal place value. For example, to plot -2/5: First, locate 0 and -1 on the number line. Next, divide the segment between 0 and -1 into 5 equal parts. Then, count two divisions to the left from 0 and mark that spot. This is the precise location of -2/5. [Student Name], this systematic approach allows [they] to accurately place any rational number. Being able to plot -2/5 or 3.25 is essential for understanding concepts like temperature drops or distances on a map, ensuring you always know 'where' a number truly is.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Hello [Student Name]! Let's plot the rational number 2 1/4 on a number line. This is a common task in math class, and breaking it down makes it easy! 1. 💡 Hint: First, think about the whole number part of 2 1/4. Locate the whole number 2 on your number line. This tells you that 2 1/4 will be somewhere to the right of 2 but before 3. It's like you've driven past the second mile marker and are now looking for a spot before the third. 2. 💡 Hint: Next, focus on the fraction 1/4. The denominator tells you how many equal parts to divide the space into. The denominator is 4, so you need to divide the space between 2 and 3 into 4 equal segments. Draw three small tick marks between 2 and 3 to create these four equal parts. 3. 💡 Hint: Finally, the numerator tells you which of those parts to mark. The numerator is 1, so count one tick mark to the right starting from 2. Place a clear point at this first tick mark. That point represents 2 1/4 on the number line. You've found the exact spot! Check: Does 2 1/4 make sense to be between 2 and 3? Yes. Is it closer to 2 than to 3? Yes, because 1/4 is less than 1/2. Your plot is accurate!
Standard
Let's plot the rational number -1.75 on a number line, [Student Name]. Rational numbers include decimals, and they can be negative too! Plotting them involves finding their precise spot. 1. First, identify the whole number part and the sign. The number is -1.75, which means it's negative and has a whole number part of 1. So, we know it's to the left of 0 and will be between -1 and -2 on the number line. We are moving in the negative direction. 2. Next, look at the decimal part, 0.75. To help visualize this on a number line, it's often useful to think of 0.75 as a fraction. We know that 0.75 is equivalent to 3/4. Now, we need to divide the segment between -1 and -2 into 4 equal parts because the denominator is 4. 3. Starting from -1, move to the left (because it's negative) and count three of those equal parts. Place a clear point at the third tick mark to the left of -1. This point is the location of -1.75 on the number line. Check: Does -1.75 fall between -1 and -2? Yes. Is it closer to -2 than to -1? Yes, because 0.75 is more than half of the way from -1 to -2. The plot is correct!
Challenge
Today, [Student Name], we're going to plot two rational numbers, -3/2 and 1 1/5, on the same number line and then figure out which one is closer to 0. This problem combines a few steps! 1. Plot -3/2. First, convert -3/2 to a mixed number or decimal: -3/2 = -1 1/2 or -1.5. This means it’s between -1 and -2. Locate -1 on the number line and divide the space between -1 and -2 into two equal parts (since the denominator is 2). Then, count one part to the left from -1. Mark this spot. Common Error: Forgetting to move left for negative numbers. Avoid by always checking the sign first and remembering negative means 'left' of zero. 2. Plot 1 1/5. This is a positive mixed number. Locate 1 on the number line, then divide the space between 1 and 2 into five equal parts (because the denominator is 5). Count one part to the right from 1. Mark this spot. Common Error: Incorrectly dividing the segment into the wrong number of parts. Avoid by carefully checking the denominator. 3. Determine which number is closer to 0. Visually inspect your number line. -3/2 (or -1.5) is 1.5 units away from 0. 1 1/5 (or 1.2) is 1.2 units away from 0. The number 1 1/5 is closer to 0. Common Error: Thinking the 'bigger' negative number is closer to zero. Remember, 'closer to 0' means a smaller absolute distance. Avoid by mentally taking the 'absolute value' or just looking at the distance on the line. Check: -1.5 is definitely to the left of 0, and 1.2 is to the right. Both are plotted correctly based on their values. Comparing distances: 1.2 is less than 1.5, so 1 1/5 is indeed closer to 0.
