Writing Algebraic Expressions

Skill ID: 6.EE.003  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Writing Algebraic Expressions means translating words that describe mathematical operations into a compact, symbolic form. Instead of saying "a number increased by three," we write "n + 3." Here, 'n' represents the unknown number. This is like turning a sentence into a mathematical shorthand. For example, if you have "5 dollars more than a certain amount," we could write "a + 5," where 'a' is the initial amount. The 'mechanism' involves identifying keywords: "increased by" means addition, "less than" means subtraction (careful with order!), "times" means multiplication, and "divided by" means division. Think of it like a secret code: each word has a mathematical symbol counterpart. Just as a chef follows a recipe step-by-step to create a dish, [Student Name], [they] will follow specific keywords to build an expression. So, "the product of 7 and a number" translates to "7n" or "7 × n". [they] will learn to recognize these keywords to write precise algebraic expressions, which are fundamental in higher math.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Let's clearly define Writing Algebraic Expressions. It is the process of translating verbal descriptions or real-world scenarios into mathematical statements that include numbers, variables, and operation symbols. A variable is simply a letter, like 'x' or 'y', used to represent an unknown quantity. For instance, if you hear "four more than a number," the definition guides us. "Four" is a number, "more than" indicates addition, and "a number" is our unknown, so we represent it with a variable, let's use 'k'. Therefore, "four more than a number" becomes k + 4. Another example: "the difference between 10 and a number." "Difference" means subtraction. If we choose 'm' for the number, we write 10 - m. Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hello [Student Name]! Let's break down how to write an algebraic expression for a word problem. This is a foundational skill in algebra, and with a little practice, [they] will be a pro!

Problem: Write an algebraic expression for "7 fewer than a number of points scored in a basketball game."

  1. 💡 Hint: Identify the unknown quantity. The problem mentions "a number of points scored in a basketball game." This is something we don't know the exact value of yet. In algebra, when we have an unknown quantity, we represent it with a variable. It can be any letter, but often we choose one that relates to the context. Let's use 'p' for points. So, our unknown is 'p'.
  1. 💡 Hint: Look for keywords indicating an operation. The phrase "7 fewer than" tells us we need to subtract. "Fewer than" usually means we're taking something away from another quantity. It's important to get the order right: "7 fewer than a number" means you start with the number and then take 7 away. If it said "7 less a number," it would be 7 minus the number.
  1. 💡 Hint: Combine the unknown and the operation. We identified the unknown as 'p' and the operation as subtracting 7. Since it's "7 fewer than p," we write 'p' first, then the subtraction sign, and then the number 7. So, the expression is: p - 7

Check: Let's test it with a real number. If a team scored 30 points (p = 30), then "7 fewer than 30 points" would be 30 - 7 = 23. This makes sense. If we had written 7 - p, and p was 30, we'd get 7 - 30 = -23, which doesn't make sense for "7 fewer than 30 points." The order is crucial!

Standard

Let's write an algebraic expression for this situation, [Student Name]. Expressions help us describe mathematical relationships concisely, even when we don't know all the numbers yet.

Problem: Write an algebraic expression for "The cost of 5 hot dogs if each hot dog costs 'd' dollars."

  1. Identify the unknown quantity: The problem states "each hot dog costs 'd' dollars." Here, 'd' is already given as our variable, representing the unknown cost of one hot dog. If it weren't given, we'd pick a letter ourselves.
  1. Determine the operation: We want to find the total cost of 5 hot dogs. If one hot dog costs 'd' dollars, then 5 hot dogs would cost 'd' added to itself 5 times, or 5 multiplied by 'd'. The phrase "cost of 5 hot dogs if each hot dog costs 'd'" implies multiplication.
  1. Construct the expression: We combine the number of hot dogs (5) with the cost of each hot dog ('d') using multiplication. In algebra, when we multiply a number and a variable, we usually write the number first, right next to the variable, without an explicit multiplication symbol. So, the expression is: 5d

Check: Let's assume a hot dog costs $3 (so d = 3). The total cost for 5 hot dogs would be 5 $3 = $15. Our expression, 5d, would become 5 3 = 15. The expression correctly represents the situation. If d was $2.50, then 5 * 2.50 = $12.50. This expression works for any cost 'd'.

Challenge

This problem combines a few steps, [Student Name], so let's tackle it carefully. It’s easy to get the order of operations wrong or miss a step, but we’ll look out for those common errors.

Problem: Write an algebraic expression for "The amount of money remaining from a $50 gift card after buying 3 movie tickets, if each ticket costs 't' dollars."

  1. Identify the total cost of the tickets: You're buying 3 movie tickets, and each ticket costs 't' dollars. Common Error: Simply writing '3 + t' or 't - 3'. This mixes up addition/subtraction with repeated costs. How to Avoid: "Each ticket costs 't' dollars" means if one ticket is 't', three tickets are 't' plus 't' plus 't', which is 3 multiplied by 't'. So, the total cost for tickets is 3t.
  1. Determine the starting amount and the operation: You started with a $50 gift card. You are buying things, which means money is being taken away from the gift card. "Remaining from" signals subtraction. The amount taken away is the total cost of the tickets. Common Error: Writing 3t - 50. This implies you started with the cost of tickets and then subtracted $50, which doesn't make sense for a gift card balance. How to Avoid: The gift card amount is what you start with ($50), and the cost of the tickets (3t) is what you subtract from it.
  1. Combine the parts to form the final expression: We start with the $50 gift card and subtract the total cost of the tickets, which we found to be 3t. The expression is: 50 - 3t

Check: Suppose each ticket costs $12 (t = 12). Total cost of tickets: 3 * 12 = $36. Amount remaining: $50 - $36 = $14. Using our expression: 50 - 3(12) = 50 - 36 = 14. The expression works!
