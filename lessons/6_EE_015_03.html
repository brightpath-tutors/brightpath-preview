One-Step Addition Equations

Skill ID: 6.EE.015.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward02293C0

One-Step Addition Equations are fundamental algebraic statements where a single unknown value, represented by a variable (like 'x'), is added to a known number, equaling another known number. The 'one-step' refers to the single inverse operation needed to find the variable's value. For example, in the equation x + 7 = 15, 'x' is the unknown, '7' is added to it, and the sum is '15'. To solve this, we use the inverse operation of addition, which is subtraction. Just like balancing a scale, if you take 7 from one side, you must take 7 from the other side to keep it balanced. So, x + 7 - 7 = 15 - 7, which simplifies to x = 8. This method ensures the equation remains true. [Student Name], mastering this balancing act is key to understanding more complex algebra later on. Imagine 'x' as a mystery box of baseball cards; if you know you added 7 more cards to get a total of 15, you can figure out how many were originally in the box. This skill is like being a detective, [they] finds the missing piece of the puzzle.

Stability — Clear & Structured02293C3

A One-Step Addition Equation is an algebraic equation where a variable is combined with a number using addition, and the result is equal to another number. The goal is to find the value of the unknown variable. To solve these equations, we use the property of inverse operations. Subtraction is the inverse of addition. Therefore, to isolate the variable, you must subtract the number added to the variable from both sides of the equation. For instance, consider the equation: x + 9 = 20. Here, 'x' is our unknown. We see that 9 is added to 'x'. To find 'x' by itself, we perform the inverse operation: subtract 9 from both sides of the equation. So, we write: x + 9 - 9 = 20 - 9. This simplifies to x = 11. To apply this, imagine [Student Name] is saving up for a new video game that costs $60. [they] already has $42, and wants to know how much more money [they] needs. This can be represented as: $42 + x = $60. To solve for x, [they] would subtract $42 from both sides: x = $60 - $42, meaning x = $18. [Student Name], this structured approach makes solving predictable.

Standard02293W0

Let's solve a one-step addition equation, [Student Name]! Imagine a basketball team scored 78 points in the first three quarters, and then added some points in the fourth quarter to reach a total of 102 points. We want to find out how many points they scored in the fourth quarter. We can write this as an equation: 78 + p = 102, where 'p' represents the points scored in the fourth quarter.

  1. Identify the unknown: In the equation 78 + p = 102, the variable 'p' is what we need to find. It's currently being added to 78.
  1. Isolate the variable: To get 'p' by itself on one side of the equation, we need to undo the addition of 78. The inverse operation of addition is subtraction. So, we subtract 78 from both sides of the equation to maintain balance. 78 + p - 78 = 102 - 78
  1. Simplify both sides: Perform the subtraction. On the left side, 78 - 78 is 0, leaving just 'p'. On the right side, 102 - 78 equals 24. p = 24
  1. Check your solution: Always plug your answer back into the original equation to ensure accuracy. If p = 24, then 78 + 24 = 102. Since 78 + 24 does indeed equal 102, our solution is correct. The basketball team scored 24 points in the fourth quarter.

Subtracting from the Variable Side
Mistake:
[Student Name] might try to subtract the constant (the number being added to the variable) directly from the variable's side without also subtracting it from the other side, or mistakenly subtract the variable from the constant. Forgetting to apply the inverse operation consistently.
Why tempting:
The goal is to 'get rid' of the number next to the variable, and some students might just remove it mentally without balancing the equation.
Correction:
Remember that an equation is like a balanced scale. Whatever you do to one side, you must do the exact same thing to the other side to keep it balanced. To isolate the variable, subtract the constant from both sides of the equals sign.
Adding Instead of Subtracting
Mistake:
When solving an addition equation like 'x + 10 = 30', [Student Name] might mistakenly add the constant (10) to the number on the other side of the equation (30), thinking [they] needs to combine all numbers.
Why tempting:
The 'plus' sign in the equation can make students think they should continue adding, instead of performing the inverse operation to isolate the variable.
Correction:
To undo addition, you must use its inverse operation, which is subtraction. Think about what number, when added to the constant, gives you the total. You need to 'take away' the constant from the total.
Ignoring the Balance of the Equals Sign
Mistake:
[Student Name] might perform an operation (like subtracting a number) on one side of the equation to isolate the variable, but completely forget to perform the exact same operation on the other side of the equals sign.
Why tempting:
Focusing too much on getting the variable by itself can lead to overlooking the fundamental rule of maintaining balance in an equation.
Correction:
Always visualize the equals sign as a balance point. Imagine a seesaw! If you take 5 pounds off one side, you must take 5 pounds off the other side to keep it level. Write out the operation on both sides to avoid this mistake.
Variable on the Right Side Confusion
Mistake:
When the variable is on the right side of the equation (e.g., 28 = 5 + p), [Student Name] might get confused about which number to subtract or try to move the variable, making the problem more complex than it is.
Why tempting:
Students often get used to seeing the variable on the left side, so a different arrangement can feel unfamiliar and lead to overthinking or incorrect steps.
Correction:
The position of the variable does not change the rule: isolate it by performing the inverse operation on both sides. Treat '28 = 5 + p' exactly the same as 'p + 5 = 28'. You still subtract the constant from both sides.
Mental Math Error at the Final Step
Mistake:
Even after correctly setting up the equation for subtraction (e.g., x = 32 - 14), [Student Name] might make a simple arithmetic mistake during the final calculation, leading to an incorrect answer.
Why tempting:
Overconfidence in mental math, rushing to finish, or a lack of focus on basic subtraction facts can cause these errors, especially with larger numbers.
Correction:
Always double-check your subtraction, especially when you're under pressure or the numbers are tricky. It's perfectly fine to quickly write out the subtraction problem on scratch paper or use a number line to verify your answer.

Exam Prep
Overview

To ace exams on one-step addition equations, [Student Name], you need to know a few things cold. First, master the concept of inverse operations: to undo addition, you subtract. This means if you see x + 7 = 15, your brain should immediately go to x = 15 - 7. Second, deeply understand that an equation is a balance; whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other to keep it true. Finally, always check your answer by plugging it back into the original equation. If 8 + 7 = 15 works, then x = 8 is correct.

On standardized tests like the FSA or SBAC, one-step addition equations often appear in various formats. You might see them as straightforward algebraic problems asking you to solve for a variable, or embedded within multi-step word problems, like figuring out how many more points a basketball team needs to reach a target score. Be ready to apply your knowledge in both abstract and contextual situations, paying close attention to units and practical solutions.

Strategy
The 'Balance Beam' Method
When:
Use this when you're starting out or feel unsure about which operation to perform on which side.
How:

Imagine the equals sign as the fulcrum of a balance beam. To keep the equation level, any operation you perform on one side must also be performed on the other. If you have 'x + 10 = 25', imagine removing 10 from the 'x + 10' side. You must also remove 10 from the '25' side.

Example:
Equation: z + 8 = 19. Think: 'I need to get rid of +8 on the left, so I'll subtract 8. To keep it balanced, I'll subtract 8 from 19 on the right.' So, z + 8 - 8 = 19 - 8, which means z = 11.
Inverse Operation Inspection
When:
Apply this strategy for quick problem-solving once you're comfortable with the concept, especially with multiple-choice questions.
How:

Look at the equation and immediately identify the operation being performed on the variable. If it's addition, know that you'll use subtraction to solve it. This allows you to quickly set up the solution without extra steps.

Example:
Equation: 50 = p + 18. See the '+ 18' next to 'p'. Immediately think: 'Inverse of addition is subtraction.' So, 50 - 18 = p. Calculate 50 - 18 = 32. Therefore, p = 32.
The 'Plug It Back In' Check
When:
Always use this as a final verification step, particularly for critical test questions or when you want to be absolutely sure of your answer.
How:

Once you've found a value for your variable, substitute that value back into the original equation. If both sides of the equation are equal after substitution, your answer is correct.

Example:
Equation: 17 + a = 35. You solved and found a = 18. Check: Replace 'a' with 18 in the original equation: 17 + 18 = 35. Since 35 = 35, your answer a = 18 is correct.
Memory Hook
Operation Opposite!
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] remember to always use the opposite (inverse) operation to solve for the variable. If you see 'plus,' you need to 'minus'!
Example:
Equation: k + 12 = 25. See the plus (+12)? Do the opposite: minus (-12) from both sides! k = 25 - 12, so k = 13.
The Equal-Sides Rule: Mirror, Mirror!
When:
This helps [Student Name] remember that an equation is like a mirror, or a balance scale. Whatever action you perform on one side, you must perform the exact same action on the other side.
Example:
Equation: x + 9 = 20. If you subtract 9 from the 'x + 9' side, it's like looking in a mirror: you must also subtract 9 from the '20' side. x + 9 - 9 = 20 - 9. So, x = 11.
