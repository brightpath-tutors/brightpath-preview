One-Step Addition Equations

Skill ID: 6.EE.015.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can identify and solve one-step addition equations by isolating the variable using inverse operations.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
One-Step Addition Equations are fundamental algebraic statements where a single unknown value, represented by a variable (like 'x'), is added to a known number, equaling another known number. The 'one-step' refers to the single inverse operation needed to find the variable's value. For example, in the equation x + 7 = 15, 'x' is the unknown, '7' is added to it, and the sum is '15'. To solve this, we use the inverse operation of addition, which is subtraction. Just like balancing a scale, if you take 7 from one side, you must take 7 from the other side to keep it balanced. So, x + 7 - 7 = 15 - 7, which simplifies to x = 8. This method ensures the equation remains true. [Student Name], mastering this balancing act is key to understanding more complex algebra later on. Imagine 'x' as a mystery box of baseball cards; if you know you added 7 more cards to get a total of 15, you can figure out how many were originally in the box. This skill is like being a detective, [they] finds the missing piece of the puzzle.

Remember in 6.EE.015.02, when you learned about variables and how to substitute values into expressions? You might have seen something like: 'If x = 5, what is x + 3?' and you correctly found 8. Today, [Student Name], we're building directly on that by turning the expression into an equation and asking you to find the 'x' yourself! Instead of being given 'x', you're going to discover it. Think of it this way: you already know how to check if a value for 'x' works in an equation. For example, if I gave you 'x + 4 = 10' and asked 'Is x = 6 a solution?', you'd substitute 6 for x (6 + 4 = 10) and confidently say 'Yes!'. Now, [they] will learn how to arrive at that '6' without being told. It's a natural next step, not a giant leap. You have all the foundational skills, [Student Name], and we'll just refine them to help you solve these problems with confidence.

Stability — Clear & Structured
A One-Step Addition Equation is an algebraic equation where a variable is combined with a number using addition, and the result is equal to another number. The goal is to find the value of the unknown variable. To solve these equations, we use the property of inverse operations. Subtraction is the inverse of addition. Therefore, to isolate the variable, you must subtract the number added to the variable from both sides of the equation. For instance, consider the equation: x + 9 = 20. Here, 'x' is our unknown. We see that 9 is added to 'x'. To find 'x' by itself, we perform the inverse operation: subtract 9 from both sides of the equation. So, we write: x + 9 - 9 = 20 - 9. This simplifies to x = 11. To apply this, imagine [Student Name] is saving up for a new video game that costs $60. [they] already has $42, and wants to know how much more money [they] needs. This can be represented as: $42 + x = $60. To solve for x, [they] would subtract $42 from both sides: x = $60 - $42, meaning x = $18. [Student Name], this structured approach makes solving predictable.
Bridge — Connects Prior Knowledge
Before tackling One-Step Addition Equations, [Student Name], you already mastered evaluating expressions with variables (6.EE.015.02). This means you understood that a letter like 'n' could stand for an unknown number. For example, if you were given the expression 'n + 5' and told that 'n = 10', you knew to substitute 10 for 'n' to get '10 + 5', resulting in 15. You were finding the value of an expression when the variable was known. That skill is absolutely crucial for today's lesson. Now, we're taking that understanding a step further. Instead of knowing what 'n' is and finding the total, we will know the total and need to find 'n'. For instance, if you saw 'n + 5 = 15', you're no longer just evaluating; you're solving to find what 'n' must be. You're using your knowledge that 'n + 5' equals 15 to deduce that 'n' must be 10. This is the bridge, [Student Name], from simple evaluation to truly solving for an unknown. The new skill is using inverse operations – subtracting 5 from both sides of 'n + 5 = 15' – to directly find that n = 10, instead of just guessing and checking. This systematic approach is what makes you an equation solver!

Standard
Let's solve a one-step addition equation, [Student Name]! Imagine a basketball team scored 78 points in the first three quarters, and then added some points in the fourth quarter to reach a total of 102 points. We want to find out how many points they scored in the fourth quarter. We can write this as an equation: 78 + p = 102, where 'p' represents the points scored in the fourth quarter. 1. Identify the unknown: In the equation 78 + p = 102, the variable 'p' is what we need to find. It's currently being added to 78. 2. Isolate the variable: To get 'p' by itself on one side of the equation, we need to undo the addition of 78. The inverse operation of addition is subtraction. So, we subtract 78 from both sides of the equation to maintain balance. 78 + p - 78 = 102 - 78 3. Simplify both sides: Perform the subtraction. On the left side, 78 - 78 is 0, leaving just 'p'. On the right side, 102 - 78 equals 24. p = 24 4. Check your solution: Always plug your answer back into the original equation to ensure accuracy. If p = 24, then 78 + 24 = 102. Since 78 + 24 does indeed equal 102, our solution is correct. The basketball team scored 24 points in the fourth quarter.
Challenge
[Student Name], sometimes one-step addition equations involve fractions or decimals, or they're part of a word problem that requires careful setup. Let's solve this problem: A recipe calls for 2/3 cup of sugar. If you already have 1/6 cup of sugar in your pantry, how much more sugar do you need? We can write this as 1/6 + s = 2/3, where 's' is the additional sugar needed. 1. Understand the Goal and Find a Common Denominator: Our goal is to find 's'. Before we can easily subtract fractions, they need a common denominator. The denominators are 6 and 3. The least common multiple is 6. So, we'll convert 2/3 to an equivalent fraction with a denominator of 6: 2/3 2/2 = 4/6. Our equation becomes: 1/6 + s = 4/6. Common Error: Forgetting to find a common denominator or incorrectly converting fractions. Avoid this by always checking if denominators match before adding or subtracting. 2. Isolate the Variable: To get 's' by itself, we need to subtract 1/6 from both sides of the equation. This is the inverse operation of adding 1/6. 1/6 + s - 1/6 = 4/6 - 1/6 3. Perform the Subtraction: On the left side, 1/6 - 1/6 cancels out, leaving 's'. On the right side, subtract the numerators while keeping the common denominator. s = (4 - 1)/6 s = 3/6 Common Error:* Incorrectly subtracting numerators or denominators. Remember, you only subtract the numerators; the denominator stays the same. 4. Simplify and Check: Simplify 3/6 to 1/2. So, s = 1/2. Let's check: 1/6 + 1/2 = 2/3. Convert 1/2 to 3/6: 1/6 + 3/6 = 4/6. And 4/6 simplifies to 2/3. It checks out! You need 1/2 cup more sugar.

[Parent Name], let's conquer One-Step Addition Equations! Think of an equation as a perfectly balanced scale. What’s on one side must exactly equal what’s on the other. For example, x + 7 = 15 means 'some unknown amount (x) plus 7 is the same as 15.' To find that 'x', [they] needs to get 'x' all by itself on one side of the scale. Here's how: 1. Understand the Goal: Your mission is to isolate 'x' (or whatever letter is used) on one side of the equals sign. 2. Identify the Operation: In x + 7 = 15, 'x' is having '7' added to it. 3. Use the Inverse Operation: To 'undo' addition, we use subtraction. So, to get rid of the '+ 7', [they] must subtract 7. 4. Keep the Balance: The crucial rule for equations: whatever [they] does to one side of the equals sign, [they] must do to the other side.
