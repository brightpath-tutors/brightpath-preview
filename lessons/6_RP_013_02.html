Finding the Whole from Percent

Skill ID: 6.RP.013.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward02141C0

Finding the Whole from Percent is the process of determining the total quantity when you only know a portion of it and the percentage that portion represents. It's essentially working backward from a percentage to discover the full amount. For example, if you know that 15 is 30% of a larger number, our goal is to find that larger number, the 'whole.' The core mechanism involves recognizing that the 'part' is equal to the 'percent' (as a decimal) multiplied by the 'whole.' To isolate the whole, we divide the part by the percent expressed as a decimal. Consider this equation: Part = Percent × Whole. To find the Whole, we rearrange it to: Whole = Part ÷ Percent. Let's say [Student Name] knows that 45 students, which is 60% of the entire 6th grade, prefer pizza for lunch. To find the total number of 6th graders (the whole), [they] would calculate: Whole = 45 ÷ 0.60 = 75 students. Just as you might know a slice of pie is a certain fraction of the whole pie, and use that fraction to figure out how many slices make the whole pie, you're doing the same with percentages here. [Student Name], this systematic approach ensures accuracy in finding the full quantity.

Hello [Student Name]! Let's figure out how to find the total number of items when you only know a part and its percentage. Imagine a school bake sale for charity. 25% of the cookies were sold in the first hour, and that was 60 cookies. How many cookies were there in total at the start of the sale?

  1. 💡 Hint: What do we know and what are we trying to find? We know that 60 cookies represent 25% of the total number of cookies. We need to find that total number, which is our 'whole'.
  2. 💡 Hint: How can we express the percentage as a useful number? Percents are fractions out of 100. So, 25% is the same as 25/100, which simplifies to 1/4. This means 60 cookies are 1/4 of the total cookies.
  3. 💡 Hint: How do we use the part and the fraction to find the whole? If 60 is 1/4 of the total, then to find the total, we need to multiply 60 by 4. Whole = Part / Percent (as a decimal) Whole = 60 / 0.25 Whole = 240 Or, using the fraction: If 1/4 of the total is 60, then the total is 60 * 4 = 240.

Check: Does 25% of 240 equal 60? 0.25 * 240 = 60. Yes, it does! So, there were 240 cookies in total.

Standard02141W0

A soccer team won 75% of their games this season. If they won 15 games, how many games did they play in total?

  1. Identify the knowns and unknowns: We know the 'part' (15 games won) and the 'percent' it represents (75%). We need to find the 'whole' (total games played).
  2. Convert the percent to a decimal or fraction: 75% can be written as 0.75 (by dividing 75 by 100) or as the fraction 75/100, which simplifies to 3/4.
  3. Set up the equation: We know that "Part is Percent of Whole". So, 15 = 0.75 × Whole.
  4. Solve for the Whole: To isolate 'Whole', divide the 'Part' by the 'Percent' (as a decimal): Whole = 15 / 0.75 Whole = 20 So, the soccer team played 20 games in total.

Check: To verify our answer, we can calculate 75% of the total games (20): 0.75 × 20 = 15. This matches the number of games won that was given in the problem, so our answer is correct. Great job, [Student Name]!

Challenge02141W0

An art gallery sold a painting for $360, which was 40% more than the price they paid for it. What was the original price the gallery paid? If the gallery had wanted to make only a 25% profit, what would the selling price have been?

Hello [Student Name]! This problem has a few twists, but we can break it down.

  1. Find the original price (the 'whole' for the first part): Understanding the "40% more": The selling price ($360) is not just 40% of the original price; it's the original price plus 40% of the original price. This means $360 represents 100% (original price) + 40% (profit) = 140% of the original price. Common Error: A common mistake is to try and find 40% of $360 and subtract it. This is incorrect because the 40% profit is based on the original price, not the selling price. Calculation: We know Part = $360, and Percent = 140% (or 1.40). Original Price = Part / Percent = $360 / 1.40 = $257.14 (rounded to two decimal places). So, the gallery paid $257.14 for the painting.
  1. Calculate the selling price for a 25% profit: Understanding the new profit: Now we want to find a new selling price where the profit is 25% of the original price ($257.14). Calculation: The new selling price would be 100% (original price) + 25% (profit) = 125% of the original price. * Selling Price = 1.25 × $257.14 = $321.43 (rounded).

Check: Is $360 indeed 40% more than $257.14? $257.14 × 1.40 = $360.00. Yes. Is $321.43 25% more than $257.14? $257.14 × 1.25 = $321.425, which rounds to $321.43. Yes.

Dividing the Percent by the Part
Mistake:
[Student Name] might incorrectly divide the given percentage by the part instead of dividing the part by the converted percentage.
Why tempting:
It's tempting because they see two numbers and one is a percent, so they might try to 'simplify' the percent by the other number first.
Correction:
Always convert the percent to a decimal or fraction first, then divide the known part by this converted percentage to find the whole.
Forgetting to Convert the Percent
Mistake:
[Student Name] might use the percentage number directly in calculations (e.g., 25 instead of 0.25 or 25/100) when setting up an equation.
Why tempting:
Percentages are often presented as whole numbers (like 25%), making it easy to forget they represent parts of 100.
Correction:
Before any calculation, convert the percentage into its decimal equivalent (divide by 100) or a fraction with a denominator of 100.
Multiplying Instead of Dividing to Find the Whole
Mistake:
[Student Name] might mistakenly multiply the known part by the converted percentage, assuming multiplication is always used with percents.
Why tempting:
Many percentage problems involve finding a part of a whole, which uses multiplication, so [they] might apply that logic here too.
Correction:
Remember that 'Part = Percent × Whole'. To find the Whole, you must rearrange the equation to 'Whole = Part / Percent'.
Misidentifying the 'Part' in Context
Mistake:
In word problems, [Student Name] might confuse what number represents the 'part' and what needs to be the 'whole' to be found.
Why tempting:
The phrasing of word problems can sometimes be tricky, making it unclear which number relates to the given percentage directly.
Correction:
Look for phrases like 'is' (usually signals the part) and 'of what number' (signals the whole). Clearly define which quantity is the part given the percentage.
Ignoring the 'Percent Off' or 'Percent Remaining' Context
Mistake:
When a problem states a 'percent off' or 'percent discount,' [Student Name] might use the discount percentage instead of the percentage representing the sale price.
Why tempting:
The discount percentage is explicitly stated and seems like the most relevant number to use in calculations.
Correction:
If an item is '20% off,' the price you pay represents 100% - 20% = 80% of the original price. Use 80% (or 0.80) to find the original whole.

Exam Prep
Overview

To ace questions on finding the whole from a percent, [Student Name] must master the core concept that a percent represents a part out of 100, and how this relates to a specific 'part' amount and the larger 'whole' amount. Knowing how to convert percentages to decimals or fractions is crucial. [they] should be able to fluently set up and solve proportions (Part/Whole = Percent/100) or algebraic equations (Part = Percent × Whole, then Whole = Part / Percent). Understanding inverse operations is key.

On standardized tests like the STAAR or MCAS, these problems often appear as multi-step word problems involving real-world scenarios such as calculating original prices of discounted items, determining total attendance based on a subset, or finding the full capacity of something given a partial amount. Visual models, like tape diagrams or double number lines, might be provided, or [Student Name] might be expected to draw them. Multiple-choice options will often include common trap answers, such as results from multiplying instead of dividing, or using the percentage without converting it.

Strategy
The Percent Proportion Method
When:
Use this method when [Student Name] feels comfortable with fractions and setting up ratios, or when visualising 'part over whole' helps.
How:

Set up the proportion: Part / Whole = Percent / 100. Substitute the given 'part' and 'percent,' then solve for the 'whole' by cross-multiplication and division. For example, if 15 is 25% of what number, set up: 15 / Whole = 25 / 100. Cross-multiply to get 25 × Whole = 15 × 100, then 25 × Whole = 1500. Divide 1500 / 25 to find Whole = 60.

Example:
If 15 is 25% of what number? (15 / Whole) = (25 / 100) -> 25 * Whole = 1500 -> Whole = 60.
The Equation Rearrangement Method
When:
This strategy is effective if [Student Name] prefers algebraic thinking and is confident converting percentages to decimals.
How:

Start with the base formula: Part = Percent × Whole. First, convert the percentage to a decimal. Then, substitute the known 'part' and the decimal 'percent' into the equation. To isolate the 'whole,' divide the 'part' by the decimal 'percent.' For example, if 18 is 40% of what number, the equation is 18 = 0.40 × Whole. Divide 18 by 0.40 to get Whole = 45.

Example:
18 is 40% of what number? 18 = 0.40 * Whole -> Whole = 18 / 0.40 -> Whole = 45.
The Unit Percent Method
When:
This method is great for building conceptual understanding and can be used for mental math or when dealing with 'nice' percentages.
How:

Find what 1% of the whole represents. Divide the given 'part' by the 'percent' (as a whole number) to find the value of 1%. Then, multiply this value by 100 to find the total 'whole'. For instance, if 20 is 25% of a number, first find 1%: 20 / 25 = 0.8. Then, multiply by 100: 0.8 × 100 = 80. This shows the whole is 80.

Example:
20 is 25% of what number? 20 / 25 = 0.8 (this is 1% of the whole). 0.8 * 100 = 80 (the whole).
Memory Hook
Is Over Of = Percent Over 100
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] set up the classic percent proportion correctly every time. 'Is' refers to the part, 'of' refers to the whole.
Example:
If the problem is '24 is 30% of what number?', [Student Name] can think: '24 (is) / Whole (of) = 30 (percent) / 100'. Then, 30 × Whole = 24 × 100, so Whole = 2400 / 30 = 80.
Part ÷ Decimal Percent = Whole
When:
This reminds [Student Name] of the specific calculation needed to find the whole and emphasizes the crucial step of converting the percent to a decimal first.
Example:
For the problem '45 is 75% of what number?', [Student Name] converts 75% to 0.75. Then, [they] applies the hook: '45 (Part) ÷ 0.75 (Decimal Percent) = Whole'. The calculation is 45 / 0.75 = 60. The whole number is 60.
