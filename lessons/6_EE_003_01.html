Writing Algebraic Expressions

Skill ID: 6.EE.003.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can translate verbal phrases representing real-world situations into algebraic expressions, using variables to represent unknown quantities.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Writing algebraic expressions is the precise process of translating verbal phrases into mathematical statements that incorporate numbers, operation symbols, and one or more variables. Think of it like a universal translator for math, where you convert everyday language into the language of algebra. For instance, if a problem states, "five more than a number," you identify "five" as a constant, "more than" as addition, and "a number" as an unknown quantity. We represent this unknown with a variable, often a letter like 'x' or 'n'. So, "five more than a number" becomes x + 5. Similarly, "twice a number, decreased by three" would translate to 2y - 3. The variable, 'y' in this case, stands in for any unknown value the problem might specify. This foundational skill allows [Student Name] to build mathematical models for real-world scenarios, setting the stage for solving complex problems later. Understanding this mechanism is key to unlocking the power of algebra, enabling [they] to express complex ideas concisely.
Confidence — Encouraging Tone
[Student Name], you've already mastered a big part of writing algebraic expressions without even realizing it! In 5th grade, you worked with standards like 5.OA.002, where you learned to write and interpret numerical expressions. For example, when you read "add 7 and 3," you knew exactly how to write that as 7 + 3. You understood that "multiply 5 by the sum of 2 and 4" becomes 5 (2 + 4). Writing algebraic expressions builds directly on this amazing skill. The only new ingredient is using a letter, like 'x' or 'n', when we don't know the exact number yet. Instead of "add 7 and 3," what if it's "add 7 and a number*"? You still recognize "add 7" and "a number" is just a new way to say "something we don't know." You're just adding a letter instead of a specific digit. This is a small, natural step, [Student Name], and [they] already possesses the core understanding to conquer it with ease.

To write an algebraic expression, we follow a clear, predictable structure, [Student Name]. You identify "product" as multiplication, "4" as a specific number, and "a number" as an unknown. This translates directly to 4 * n or simply 4n. We often use 'n' or 'x' for "a number." Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Hey [Student Name]! Let's work through writing an algebraic expression for a real-world situation. Imagine you're at the school carnival. It costs $5 to get in, and then each game you play costs an additional $1.50. If you don't know exactly how many games you'll play, how can we write an expression for the total cost? Here’s how we break it down: 1. 💡 Hint: First, think about what costs stay the same no matter what. This is your fixed amount. The entrance fee is a one-time cost that doesn't change. It's $5, whether you play one game or ten. So, we start our expression with that fixed amount. 2. 💡 Hint: Next, consider what costs change depending on how many games you play. This is where a variable comes in. Each game costs $1.50. If you play one game, it's $1.50. If you play two, it's $1.50 + $1.50, or $1.50 × 2. If you play 'g' games (where 'g' represents the number of games), the cost for games is $1.50 multiplied by 'g', written as 1.50g. 3. 💡 Hint: Now, combine the fixed cost and the changing cost to find the total. To find the total cost, you add the entrance fee to the cost of the games. So, the expression is: 5 + 1.50g. Check your work: Let’s say you played 4 games. Using our expression, the total cost would be 5 + (1.50 × 4) = 5 + 6 = $11. Does that make sense? Yes, $5 for entry plus $6 for four games equals $11. This expression works no matter how many games you play!
Standard
[Student Name], let's write an algebraic expression for a common situation. Imagine your favorite ice cream shop sells single scoops for $3.50 and offers extra toppings for $0.75 each. We want to write an expression for the total cost of one scoop of ice cream with an unknown number of toppings. Here are the steps to follow: 1. Identify the fixed cost: The cost of the single scoop of ice cream doesn't change. It's always $3.50. This is the starting point for our expression, as it's a fixed amount that will always be part of the total. 2. Identify the variable cost: The cost of the toppings changes depending on how many you choose. Each topping costs $0.75. If you choose 't' number of toppings (where 't' stands for toppings), then the total cost for the toppings will be $0.75 multiplied by 't'. We write this as 0.75t. 3. Combine the costs to form the expression: To get the total cost, you need to add the fixed cost of the ice cream scoop to the variable cost of the toppings. So, you combine the parts from step 1 and step 2: 3.50 + 0.75t. Check: Let's say you get 2 toppings. Plug '2' in for 't' in our expression: 3.50 + (0.75 × 2) = 3.50 + 1.50 = $5.00. This makes sense: $3.50 for the scoop plus $1.50 for two toppings equals $5.00 total.
Challenge
Let's tackle a slightly trickier problem, [Student Name]. Your family is going to a baseball game. Tickets cost $25 per person, but there's a special promotion: if you buy your tickets online, you get a $3 discount per ticket. Also, everyone wants a hot dog, which costs $6 each. Write an expression for the total cost for 'p' people attending the game and buying one hot dog each. 1. Calculate the discounted ticket price: The original ticket price is $25, and there's a $3 discount per ticket. So, each discounted ticket costs $25 - $3 = $22. Common Error: Some might multiply the $3 discount by 'p' separately and subtract it from the total original price, like (25p) - 3p. While mathematically equivalent, it's often clearer to calculate the per-ticket discounted price first to avoid confusion with order of operations later. The discounted cost for 'p' tickets is 22p. 2. Calculate the total cost of hot dogs: Each person gets one hot dog, and hot dogs cost $6 each. If there are 'p' people, the total cost for hot dogs will be $6 multiplied by 'p', or 6p. Common Error: Forgetting to account for the number of people for all items. If the problem states 'everyone wants a hot dog', the number of hot dogs is 'p'. 3. Combine all costs: Now, add the total cost for the discounted tickets to the total cost for the hot dogs. The expression is 22p + 6p. Notice both parts have 'p'. Common Error: Some might try to put a plus sign where multiplication is needed or combine unlike terms if other variables were present. Here, since both are 'p' terms, you can simplify it to 28p, representing $28 total cost per person for a discounted ticket and a hot dog. Check: If 5 people go (p=5), the cost is (22 × 5) + (6 × 5) = 110 + 30 = $140. Or, using the simplified expression: 28 × 5 = $140. It works!

Hello [Parent Name]! Let's demystify 'Writing Algebraic Expressions' so [they] can confidently help [Student Name] this week. 1. What's an Expression? Think of it like a phrase in English, not a complete sentence. It has numbers and operations, but it doesn't have an equals sign. For example, '3 + 5' is an expression. 2. Introducing Variables: This is the key difference. Instead of always knowing the numbers, sometimes we have an 'unknown.' In algebra, we use a letter (like 'x', 'n', or 'g') to represent that unknown quantity. We call these letters 'variables' because their value can vary. 3. Why Use Them? Variables help us describe general situations. Imagine you're budgeting. If you have $100 and spend 'g' dollars on groceries, the expression '$100 - g$' represents how much money you have left. It works no matter how much 'g' is! 4. Translating Words to Math: The core skill is turning word phrases into these algebraic expressions. Look for keywords: 'Sum,' 'more than,' 'increased by' mean ADD (+). (Example: 'You earned $20 and then worked 'h' more hours at $15/hour.' → $20 + 15h$) 'Difference,' 'less than,' 'decreased by' mean SUBTRACT (-). (Example: 'Your gas tank holds 15 gallons, and you've used 'g' gallons.' → $15 - g$) 'Product,' 'times,' 'multiplied by' mean MULTIPLY ( or no symbol). (Example: 'You buy 'x' tickets to a baseball game, and each costs $25.' → $25x$) * 'Quotient,' 'divided by' mean DIVIDE (/). (Example: 'You split a $50 bill among 'y' friends.' → $50 / y$) [Parent Name], [they] will find that once you recognize these patterns, building expressions becomes second nature. Practice thinking about common tasks in these terms, and [they] will be a pro!
