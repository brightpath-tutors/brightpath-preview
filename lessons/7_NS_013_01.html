Multiplying Rational Numbers

Skill ID: 7.NS.013.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Multiplying rational numbers involves finding the product of two numbers that can be expressed as a fraction a/b, where b is not zero. This includes integers, fractions, and decimals, whether positive or negative. The fundamental mechanism involves two steps: first, determine the sign of the product using the rules for integer multiplication (positive × positive = positive, negative × negative = positive, positive × negative = negative); second, multiply the absolute values of the numbers. For example, to calculate (2/3) (-3/4), we first identify the signs: positive times negative yields a negative result. Next, we multiply the absolute values: (2/3) (3/4) = 6/12, which simplifies to 1/2. Therefore, (2/3) * (-3/4) = -1/2. Think of multiplying rational numbers like combining financial changes; if you have a debt (negative) that doubles (positive multiplier), your debt increases. [Student Name], understanding that a rational number is a ratio helps [them] see the underlying structure of both fractions and decimals. Mastering these steps will make you a pro, [Student Name]!

Remember how you've already mastered multiplying positive fractions and decimals? And how you know exactly what happens when you multiply positive and negative integers from your work with 7.NS.009? Well, [Student Name], multiplying rational numbers is simply putting those two amazing skills together! There's nothing truly new to learn regarding the core multiplication process or the rules for signs. You already know that if you multiply a negative by a positive, the result is negative. That rule doesn't change just because the numbers are fractions or decimals instead of whole numbers. So, if you're multiplying -0.5 by 0.6, you already know the answer will be negative, and then you just multiply 0.5 by 0.6 to get 0.3. The final answer is -0.3. See, [Student Name], you’re already halfway there! You have all the foundational skills you need to succeed with this topic, [Student Name].

Stability — Clear & Structured
Multiplying rational numbers is a straightforward process once you understand the two main components: determining the sign and performing the absolute value multiplication. A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a fraction p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not zero. This broad definition includes whole numbers, integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals. For example, let's consider (-1.5) (0.4). First, we determine the sign. A negative number multiplied by a positive number always results in a negative product. So, our answer will be negative. Second, we multiply the absolute values: 1.5 0.4. Multiplying these decimals gives us 0.6. Combining the sign and the product of the absolute values, we get -0.6. This skill is incredibly useful in real-life scenarios, [Student Name]. For instance, if you are tracking a stock that lost 1/8 of its value for three consecutive days, you'd multiply the current value by (7/8) repeatedly to find its new worth. [Student Name], this clear, step-by-step approach ensures you can tackle any problem.
Bridge — Connects Prior Knowledge
Before we dive into multiplying all types of rational numbers, let's quickly review some key ideas you already know. From your work with 7.NS.009, you understand that rational numbers encompass fractions, decimals, and integers. You’re also familiar with the basic rules for multiplying integers: positive times positive is positive (like 3 4 = 12), negative times negative is positive (like -3 -4 = 12), and positive times negative is negative (like 3 -4 = -12). These sign rules are fundamental, and you've used them many times, [Student Name]. Now, here's where we build the bridge: The fantastic news is that these exact same sign rules apply when you multiply any rational numbers, whether they are fractions like -1/2, or decimals like -0.75! The only new step is applying the sign rules to the absolute values of the fractions or decimals you already know how to multiply. So, to multiply -1/2 by 3/4, you first determine the sign (negative), and then multiply 1/2 by 3/4 (which is 3/8). Thus, -1/2 3/4 = -3/8. See, [Student Name]? You're just extending your existing knowledge!
