Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward02009C0

Fraction multiplication word problems ask you to find a fractional part of another quantity, which can be a whole number or another fraction. This often looks like finding 'a fraction of' something. When you encounter these problems, you are essentially determining a portion of an existing portion. For instance, if a recipe calls for 3/4 cup of sugar, and [Student Name] only wants to make 1/2 of the recipe, [they] needs to find 1/2 of 3/4. The mechanism is simple: multiply the numerators together and multiply the denominators together. So, (1/2) (3/4) = (13)/(24) = 3/8. Think of it like this: finding a fraction of a fraction is similar to finding a smaller piece of an already smaller piece, just as finding 1/2 of a pie is taking a piece, and then finding 1/2 of that* piece means taking an even tinier slice. This powerful skill lets [Student Name] solve problems like calculating how much fabric is needed for a smaller version of a quilt pattern or how much money is left after spending a fraction of a fraction of [their] allowance. Keep an eye out for keywords like 'of' which often signal multiplication.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)02009W0

Hello, [Student Name]! Let's tackle a word problem about multiplying fractions. Imagine you're baking some delicious cookies. The recipe calls for 3/4 of a cup of sugar, but you only want to make 2/3 of the recipe because you don't need a huge batch. How much sugar do you need?

  1. 💡 Hint: Understand what the problem is asking. The problem asks for a fraction of a fraction—you need 2/3 of the 3/4 cup of sugar. When you see "of" with fractions, it usually means you need to multiply them. So, you're finding 2/3 times 3/4.
  1. 💡 Hint: Set up the multiplication. Write down the fractions you need to multiply: (3/4) × (2/3). Remember, when you multiply fractions, you multiply the numerators (the top numbers) together, and then multiply the denominators (the bottom numbers) together.
  1. 💡 Hint: Perform the multiplication and simplify. Multiply the numerators: 3 × 2 = 6. Multiply the denominators: 4 × 3 = 12. So, your new fraction is 6/12. Now, simplify this fraction by finding the greatest common factor (GCF) of 6 and 12, which is 6. Divide both the numerator and the denominator by 6: 6 ÷ 6 = 1 and 12 ÷ 6 = 2. The simplified fraction is 1/2.
  1. 💡 Hint: State your answer and check your work. You need 1/2 cup of sugar. Does this make sense? You're making a smaller portion (2/3) of the recipe, so the amount of sugar should be less than the original 3/4 cup. Since 1/2 is indeed less than 3/4, our answer is reasonable.

Check: (3/4) × (2/3) = (3×2) / (4×3) = 6/12 = 1/2. The calculation is correct!

Standard02009W0

Let's look at another common scenario. Imagine a track at a middle school is 7/8 of a mile long. If [Student Name] is told by [their] gym teacher to run 2/5 of the track for warm-ups, how many miles did [they] run?

  1. Identify the fractions and the operation. The problem asks for 2/5 of 7/8 of a mile. This means we need to multiply the two fractions: 2/5 and 7/8.
  1. Multiply the numerators and the denominators. To multiply fractions, you simply multiply the top numbers (numerators) together and the bottom numbers (denominators) together. So, for (2/5) × (7/8): Numerator: 2 × 7 = 14 Denominator: 5 × 8 = 40 This gives us the fraction 14/40.
  1. Simplify the result. The fraction 14/40 can be simplified because both 14 and 40 are even numbers, meaning they can both be divided by 2. Divide the numerator by 2 (14 ÷ 2 = 7) and the denominator by 2 (40 ÷ 2 = 20). The simplified fraction is 7/20.
  1. State the answer and check. [Student Name] ran 7/20 of a mile. To check, we can estimate: 2/5 is a bit less than 1/2, and 7/8 is almost 1. So, 2/5 of 7/8 should be a bit less than 1/2, which 7/20 is. (7/20 is 0.35, 1/2 is 0.5). The answer makes sense, as running a fraction of a track should result in a distance shorter than the full track.

Check: (2/5) × (7/8) = (2×7) / (5×8) = 14/40 = 7/20. The calculation is accurate.

Challenge02009W0

Alright [Student Name], let's try a tricky one! A food truck started the day with 3/4 of a huge pan of macaroni and cheese. During lunch, they sold 1/2 of the mac and cheese they had. After lunch, a catering order came in, and they needed to use 1/3 of the remaining mac and cheese for that order. How much of the original full pan of mac and cheese did they use for the catering order?

  1. Calculate mac and cheese sold at lunch. They started with 3/4 of a pan and sold 1/2 of that. This is a multiplication problem: (3/4) × (1/2). Common error: Some might try to subtract 1/2 directly from 3/4, but "1/2 of" means to multiply. (3/4) × (1/2) = (3×1)/(4×2) = 3/8 of the pan was sold.
  1. Calculate the mac and cheese remaining after lunch. They started with 3/4 of a pan and sold 3/8 of the pan. So, we subtract: (3/4) - (3/8). To subtract, find a common denominator, which is 8. (6/8) - (3/8) = 3/8 of the pan remaining. Common error: Accidentally using the fraction of the portion sold (1/2) instead of the actual amount sold (3/8) when calculating the remainder.
  1. Calculate mac and cheese used for the catering order. They used 1/3 of the remaining 3/8 of a pan. Again, "of" means multiply: (1/3) × (3/8). Common error: Multiplying 1/3 by the original 3/4 of a pan, forgetting that it's 1/3 of the remaining amount. (1/3) × (3/8) = (1×3)/(3×8) = 3/24. This simplifies to 1/8.
  1. State the answer and check. The food truck used 1/8 of the original full pan of mac and cheese for the catering order. This makes sense because each step took a fraction of a smaller and smaller amount, leading to a small final fraction.

Check: (3/4) starting. Sold 1/2 of that: (3/4)(1/2) = 3/8. Remaining: (3/4)-(3/8) = 6/8-3/8 = 3/8. Used 1/3 of remaining: (1/3)(3/8) = 3/24 = 1/8. All correct!

Confusing 'of' with Addition/Subtraction
Mistake:
Students see the word 'of' in a word problem and incorrectly add or subtract fractions instead of multiplying them. This is common when the context is finding a 'part of a part'.
Why tempting:
In everyday language, 'of' can sometimes imply a subset or removal, leading students to associate it with subtraction from a whole or merely identifying a component without an operation.
Correction:
Remind [Student Name] that in mathematics, especially with fractions, 'of' between a fraction and a quantity (another fraction or whole number) signifies multiplication. When you take 'a fraction of a quantity', you are multiplying.
Multiplying Whole Numbers by Both Numerator and Denominator
Mistake:
When multiplying a fraction by a whole number, [Student Name] might incorrectly multiply the whole number by both the numerator and the denominator of the fraction.
Why tempting:
This mistake stems from a misunderstanding of how whole numbers interact with fractions, potentially generalizing rules from creating common denominators in addition/subtraction.
Correction:
Teach [Student Name] to always convert the whole number into a fraction by placing it over 1 (e.g., 5 becomes 5/1). Then, multiply the numerators and the denominators separately.
Not Simplifying Fractions (Before or After)
Mistake:
[Student Name] might perform the multiplication correctly but fail to simplify the resulting product to its lowest terms. Alternatively, [they] might miss opportunities to cross-simplify before multiplying, making the calculation harder.
Why tempting:
Students often focus solely on the primary operation (multiplication) and overlook simplification as a crucial final or initial step, or they struggle to find common factors.
Correction:
Encourage [Student Name] to look for common factors between any numerator and any denominator (cross-simplification) before multiplying. If not done before, always simplify the final product by dividing the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.
Misinterpreting 'Product is Smaller' for Proper Fractions
Mistake:
[Student Name] may become confused or doubt [their] answer when multiplying two proper fractions because the product is a smaller number than either of the original fractions.
Why tempting:
Most of [their] prior experience with whole number multiplication yields a product larger than the factors, making the 'smaller product' outcome for fractions counter-intuitive and seemingly incorrect.
Correction:
Use visual models like area models or fraction bars to demonstrate that finding a 'fraction of a fraction' literally means taking a smaller portion of an already existing part. This reinforces why the answer should be smaller.
Forgetting to Convert Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions
Mistake:
When a word problem involves mixed numbers, [Student Name] sometimes tries to multiply the whole number parts and the fraction parts separately, or forgets to convert the mixed numbers to improper fractions entirely.
Why tempting:
Students might view converting as an extra, unnecessary step, or try to apply a 'distributive' method that is incorrect for multiplication of mixed numbers.
Correction:
Emphasize the golden rule: 'Mixed Up? Make it Improper!' Always convert mixed numbers to improper fractions before performing any multiplication. This ensures the entire quantity is correctly calculated.

Strategy
The Keyword Catcher
When:
To identify multiplication in word problems.
How:

Look for phrases like 'of,' 'fraction of,' 'times,' or 'a portion of.' These strongly indicate multiplication.

Example:
If a problem says, 'What is 2/3 of 1 and 1/2 cups of flour?', it signals 2/3 * 3/2 = 1 cup.
Visualize Your Fraction
When:
For fraction-of-a-fraction problems or to visually check answers.
How:

Draw an area model (rectangle). Divide it for the first fraction, then perpendicularly for the second. The overlapping section is your product.

Example:
To solve '1/2 of 1/3 of a garden,' draw a rectangle divided into thirds, then halved. The overlap shows 1/6 of the garden.
Simplify First, Multiply Later
When:
Always, especially with larger numbers, to simplify calculations.
How:

Before multiplying, find common factors between any numerator and any denominator (cross-simplification). Divide them. Smaller numbers mean easier multiplication and often simplified final answers.

Example:
For 4/9 3/8, simplify 4 and 8 (to 1 and 2), and 3 and 9 (to 1 and 3). Then multiply 1/3 1/2 = 1/6.
Memory Hook
Of Means Multiply, So Give It a Try!
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] recall that the word 'of' in fraction problems signals multiplication. It's crucial for setting up the correct equation, preventing common errors like adding or subtracting.
Example:
If a word problem asks, 'What is 1/4 of a 2/3 pound bag of trail mix?', remember 'Of Means Multiply!' and set up the problem as 1/4 * 2/3. This calculation results in 2/12, which simplifies to 1/6 pound of trail mix.
Mixed Up? Make it Improper!
When:
This hook reminds [Student Name] that any mixed numbers involved in fraction multiplication must be converted into improper fractions before multiplying. Skipping this critical step leads to incorrect answers.
Example:
To calculate a problem like '1 and 1/3 3/4', [Student Name] should first convert the mixed number 1 and 1/3 into its improper fraction form, 4/3. Then, [they] can correctly multiply 4/3 3/4, which equals 12/12, or 1.
