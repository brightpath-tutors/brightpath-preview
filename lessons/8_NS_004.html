Academic — Concept-Forward00594C0

A repeating decimal is a decimal representation of a rational number where a sequence of one or more digits repeats indefinitely. For example, 0.333... is a repeating decimal because the digit '3' repeats forever. Converting a repeating decimal to a fraction involves a clever algebraic manipulation. Let's take 0.333... as an example. First, we define a variable, say 'x', equal to the repeating decimal: x = 0.333... Next, we multiply both sides of this equation by a power of 10 that shifts the repeating part to the left of the decimal. Since only one digit repeats, we multiply by 10: 10x = 3.333... Now, here's the magic step, [Student Name]: subtract the original equation from the new one. (10x - x) = (3.333... - 0.333...). This simplifies to 9x = 3. Finally, solve for x: x = 3/9, which simplifies to 1/3. Just like finding the 'root' of a mathematical plant, you're uncovering the hidden fractional form beneath the endless decimal growth. [they] will find this method works for any repeating decimal.

Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)00594W0

Alright, [Student Name], let's tackle converting a repeating decimal like 0.555... into a fraction step-by-step. Don't worry, we'll break it down together!

  1. 💡 Hint: First, give your repeating decimal a variable name. This helps us write it as an equation. Let x = 0.555... This initial step allows us to treat the decimal as an algebraic quantity, making it easier to manipulate.
  1. 💡 Hint: Next, we want to shift the repeating part. Multiply by a power of 10 that moves one full repeating block to the left of the decimal point. Since only the '5' is repeating (a single digit), we multiply both sides of our equation by 10. So, 10x = 10 * 0.555..., which becomes 10x = 5.555... This creates a new equation where the repeating part still aligns with the original.
  1. 💡 Hint: Now, subtract the original equation from the new one. This will make the repeating decimal parts cancel out completely. We have two equations: (Equation 1) x = 0.555... and (Equation 2) 10x = 5.555.... When we subtract Equation 1 from Equation 2, the magic happens: 10x - x = 5.555... - 0.555... This simplifies to 9x = 5. Notice how the endless repeating digits (.555...) vanish, leaving a simple whole number.
  1. 💡 Hint: Finally, solve for x to find your fraction. To isolate x, divide both sides of the equation 9x = 5 by 9. This gives us x = 5/9. And just like that, you've converted the repeating decimal into a neat fraction!

Check: To make sure [their] answer is correct, [they] can quickly divide 5 by 9 on a calculator. 5 ÷ 9 = 0.555... Yep, it matches!

Standard00594W0

Let's work through converting a slightly more complex repeating decimal, like 0.121212..., into its fractional form. This process builds on understanding how to isolate and eliminate the repeating pattern.

  1. Set x equal to the repeating decimal: x = 0.121212... This is our base equation, which helps us algebraically represent the decimal we want to convert.
  1. Identify the repeating block and multiply to shift it. In this decimal, the '12' is the repeating block, which consists of two digits. To move one full repeating block to the left of the decimal point, we multiply both sides of our initial equation by 100 (since 10 to the power of 2, for two repeating digits, is 100). This operation yields 100x = 12.121212... Now we have two equations with the repeating parts perfectly aligned after the decimal.
  1. Subtract the original equation from the multiplied equation. This is the crucial step where the repeating decimals cancel out. We align the equations for subtraction: 100x = 12.121212... - x = 0.121212... ------------------ 99x = 12 Notice how the .121212... repeating sections vanish, leaving us with a clean integer on the right side of the equation.
  1. Solve for x and simplify the fraction. To find the value of x, divide both sides of the equation 99x = 12 by 99: x = 12/99. Now, we must simplify the fraction. Both 12 and 99 are divisible by 3. Dividing both the numerator and denominator by 3, we get x = (12 ÷ 3) / (99 ÷ 3) = 4/33.

Challenge00594W0

Let's tackle a more challenging repeating decimal: 0.1666... This one has a non-repeating digit before the repeating part, requiring an extra initial step. Staying organized is key!

  1. Set up the initial equation: Let x = 0.1666... This decimal has a non-repeating part ('1') and a repeating part ('6'). Common Error: Some students might immediately try to multiply by 10 to deal with the '6' repeating. However, this is premature; we first need to get the non-repeating part clear of the decimal.

Here, '1' is the non-repeating digit, so we multiply by 10: 10x = 1.666... Call this Equation (A). To shift one full repeating block past the decimal in Equation (A), we multiply Equation (A) by 10 (since only one digit repeats): 10 (10x) = 10 (1.666...), which gives 100x = 16.666... Call this Equation (B). Avoid Error: Make sure you are multiplying the modified equation (Equation A) to shift the repeating block, not the original x. Multiplying original x by 100 would give 16.666..., but it's not useful for subtraction because we need another equation with 1.666... for alignment.
  1. Subtract the equations where the repeating parts align. Subtract Equation (A) from Equation (B): 100x = 16.666... (Equation B) - 10x = 1.666... Ignoring the Non-Repeating Part
Mistake:
When converting a mixed repeating decimal like 0.1666..., [Student Name] might incorrectly assume the repeating part starts immediately after the decimal point and apply the pure repeating method.
Why tempting:
It simplifies the initial setup, as the student is familiar with the process for pure repeating decimals like 0.666...
Correction:
First, multiply the decimal by a power of 10 to shift the non-repeating digits to the left of the decimal point, creating a new purely repeating decimal for further steps.
Incorrect Multiplier for Shifting Decimal
Mistake:
[Student Name] might use an arbitrary power of 10 (e.g., 10x, 100x) without ensuring the repeating blocks perfectly align for subtraction, leading to a non-zero decimal part after subtraction.
Why tempting:
The student might rush or not fully grasp that the goal of multiplication is to create two equations where the repeating parts are identical for cancellation.
Correction:
Identify the length of the repeating block (n). Multiply the original decimal (x) by 10^n to shift one full repeating block past the decimal, and then potentially by another power of 10 to isolate it from any non-repeating digits.
Arithmetic Errors in Subtraction
Mistake:
When subtracting the two equations (e.g., 100x - x), [Student Name] might make a calculation error, particularly with borrowing or misaligning digits, resulting in an incorrect integer on the right side.
Why tempting:
The process involves multiple steps, and a simple arithmetic mistake can occur if not careful, especially when dealing with decimal alignments in previous steps.
Correction:
Carefully align the decimal points and digits vertically when performing the subtraction. Double-check the subtraction to ensure accuracy.
Forgetting to Simplify the Fraction
Mistake:
[Student Name] correctly converts the repeating decimal to a fraction but submits it without simplifying it to its lowest terms.
Why tempting:
The main 'conversion' work feels complete, and simplification might be viewed as an optional step or is simply overlooked in the rush to finish.
Correction:
Always check the resulting fraction for common factors in the numerator and denominator. Divide both by their greatest common factor (GCF) until the fraction is in its simplest form.
Confusing Repeating and Terminating Decimals
Mistake:
[Student Name] might try to apply the algebraic method for repeating decimals to a terminating decimal, or incorrectly converts a terminating decimal by placing it over '9s' instead of powers of 10.
Why tempting:
Both types of decimals are rational numbers and can be expressed as fractions, leading to confusion about the appropriate conversion method.
Correction:
Clearly distinguish between terminating decimals (which end) and repeating decimals (which have a repeating pattern). Terminating decimals are placed over powers of 10 (e.g., 0.75 = 75/100), while repeating decimals require the algebraic method.

Strategy
The 'Two-Equation Tango'
When:
Use this for pure repeating decimals where the entire decimal part repeats, like 0.363636...
How:

Set x = 0.3636..., then multiply x by 100 (since two digits repeat) to get 100x = 36.3636.... Subtract x from 100x, so 99x = 36. Solve for x: x = 36/99, then simplify.

Example:
Convert 0.363636... to a fraction. 99x = 36, so x = 36/99 = 4/11.
'Isolate and Conquer' for Mixed Decimals
When:
Employ this strategy for mixed repeating decimals, like 0.12555..., where some digits don't repeat before the pattern begins.
How:

First, multiply the original decimal (x) by a power of 10 to shift the non-repeating part to the left of the decimal. For 0.12555..., 100x = 12.555.... Now, define a new variable (e.g., y = 0.555...) and use the 'Two-Equation Tango' on that, or continue with 100x and 1000x.

Example:
Convert 0.12555... to a fraction. Let x = 0.12555.... 100x = 12.555.... Now, let 100x be a new starting point. 1000x = 125.555.... Subtract: 1000x - 100x = 125.555... - 12.555... = 113. So 900x = 113, x = 113/900.
'Simplify or Bust!'
When:
Apply this strategy as the final step after converting any repeating decimal to an initial fraction.
How:

Always check if the numerator and denominator share any common factors. Divide both by their greatest common factor (GCF) until the fraction cannot be reduced further. Standardized tests expect answers in simplest form.

Example:
If [they] converts 0.484848... to 48/99. Both 48 and 99 are divisible by 3. So, 48 ÷ 3 = 16 and 99 ÷ 3 = 33. The simplified fraction is 16/33.
Memory Hook
The 'Nines' Denominator Shortcut
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] quickly recall the denominator for pure repeating decimals. If the repeating block has 'n' digits, the denominator will be 'n' nines.
Example:
For 0.777..., there's one repeating digit (7), so it's 7/9. For 0.232323..., there are two repeating digits (23), so it's 23/99. For 0.123123..., three repeating digits (123) mean 123/999 (which simplifies to 41/333). This is a great starting point before simplifying.
'Decimal Detective' for Mixed Cases
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] remember the first critical step for mixed repeating decimals: isolating the repeating part.
Example:
Imagine 0.1666... as a mystery. Your first job as a 'Decimal Detective' is to 'unmask' the non-repeating digit. Multiply by 10 to get 10x = 1.666.... Now the pure repeating part is clear, and [they] can apply the 'Two-Equation Tango' to 1.666... (or rather, just the 0.666... part of it) to solve the case!
