Multi-Step Rational Problems

Skill ID: 7.EE.006.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can evaluate multi-step expressions involving rational numbers (fractions and decimals) by applying the correct order of operations.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Multi-Step Rational Problems are mathematical expressions or equations that require two or more operations to be performed on rational numbers. Rational numbers include fractions (like 1/2), decimals (like 0.75), and integers (like -5). To solve these, you rigorously apply the order of operations (PEMDAS/GEMDAS). Think of it like a chef following a recipe: you wouldn't bake a cake before mixing the ingredients, just as you perform operations within parentheses before multiplication. For instance, consider the expression (2/5 + 0.7) 10 - 3.5. First, convert 2/5 to 0.4. Then, inside the parentheses, 0.4 + 0.7 equals 1.1. Next, multiply 1.1 10 to get 11. Finally, subtract 11 - 3.5, which results in 7.5. [Student Name], mastering this systematic approach is key to consistently arriving at the correct solution. [they] will see these problems frequently in higher math.

Remember 7.NS.017, where you mastered adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing rational numbers? You know how to calculate 3/4 + 0.5 or (1.5) (-2). We're just taking your ability to work with fractions and decimals (7.NS.017) and applying it within the familiar structure of PEMDAS (6.EE.017). It's like knowing how to dribble a basketball and how to shoot; now you're learning to dribble then shoot. You've got all the pieces; we're just fitting them together. [they] can definitely achieve this!

Let's calmly break down Multi-Step Rational Problems. First, the definition: a multi-step rational problem involves performing several mathematical operations on numbers that can be expressed as a fraction or ratio, including fractions, decimals, and integers. These problems demand careful application of the order of operations. For example, consider a school field trip budget for [Student Name]. Suppose the bus costs $150.00, and there are 25 students. Each student also buys a $5.25 lunch. How much does each student owe in total if the bus cost is split evenly? Example: (150.00 / 25) + 5.25. Step 1 (Division): 150.00 / 25 = 6.00. Step 2 (Addition): 6.00 + 5.25 = 11.25. So, each student owes $11.25. This application shows how these problems are crucial for calculating fair shares or total expenses, ensuring everyone contributes correctly. [they] will find this structured approach reliable.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Hello [Student Name]! Let's break down a problem step-by-step to see how multi-step rational problems work. It's like following a recipe, one instruction at a time. Problem: Maria bought a shirt for $24.50. She also bought 3 pairs of socks, each costing $3.25. If she paid with a $50 bill, how much change did she get? Step 1: Calculate the total cost of the socks. 💡 Hint: To find the total cost of multiple identical items, you multiply the number of items by the cost per item. Cost of socks = 3 pairs × $3.25/pair = $9.75 Step 2: Calculate Maria's total spending. 💡 Hint: Her total spending is the sum of the shirt's cost and the socks' total cost. Remember to align decimal points when adding decimals. Total spending = Cost of shirt + Cost of socks = $24.50 + $9.75 = $34.25 Step 3: Calculate the change Maria received. 💡 Hint: The change is the difference between the amount she paid and her total spending. Subtract the smaller value from the larger value. Change = Amount paid - Total spending = $50.00 - $34.25 = $15.75 So, [Student Name], Maria received $15.75 in change. Check: We can work backward. If she received $15.75 change and spent $34.25, then $15.75 + $34.25 = $50.00, which matches the amount she paid. This confirms our calculations are correct.
Standard
Let's work through a standard multi-step problem, [Student Name]. These problems often involve fractions and decimals, so paying attention to the order of operations and units is key. Problem: A baker used 2.5 cups of flour for a cake. Then, [they] used 1/2 of that amount for cookies. If the baker started with 8 cups of flour, how much flour is left? Step 1: Calculate the flour used for cookies. To find out how much flour was used for cookies, we need to take half of the amount used for the cake. Flour for cookies = 1/2 × 2.5 cups = 0.5 × 2.5 cups = 1.25 cups Step 2: Calculate the total flour used. Now, add the flour used for the cake and the cookies to find the total amount consumed. Total flour used = Flour for cake + Flour for cookies = 2.5 cups + 1.25 cups = 3.75 cups Step 3: Calculate the remaining flour. Subtract the total flour used from the initial amount of flour the baker started with. Remaining flour = Initial flour - Total flour used = 8 cups - 3.75 cups = 4.25 cups So, there are 4.25 cups of flour left. Check: We can add the remaining flour to the flour used: 4.25 cups (remaining) + 3.75 cups (used) = 8 cups, which matches the starting amount. This verifies our answer.
Challenge
This challenging problem, [Student Name], requires careful reading and understanding of fractions applied to changing quantities. Pay close attention to common pitfalls! Problem: A marathon runner completed 3/5 of a 26.2-mile race. Then, [they] ran an additional 1/4 of the remaining distance. How many miles does [they] still have to run to finish the race? Step 1: Calculate the distance completed in the first part of the race. To find 3/5 of 26.2 miles, we multiply the fraction by the total distance. Distance completed (Part 1) = (3/5) × 26.2 = 0.6 × 26.2 = 15.72 miles. Common Error: Some might mistakenly calculate 3/5 of 26, then 3/5 of 0.2 separately, leading to errors if not careful with decimals. Always convert fractions to decimals or multiply fractions directly with decimals. Step 2: Calculate the remaining distance after Part 1. Subtract the distance completed in Part 1 from the total race distance. Remaining distance = 26.2 miles - 15.72 miles = 10.48 miles. Common Error: Forgetting to find the remaining distance and instead using the total race distance for the next step. The problem specifically states "1/4 of the remaining distance." Step 3: Calculate the distance completed in the second part of the race. Now, take 1/4 of the remaining distance (10.48 miles). Distance completed (Part 2) = (1/4) × 10.48 = 0.25 × 10.48 = 2.62 miles. Common Error: Incorrectly converting 1/4 to a decimal (e.g., 0.4) or making calculation errors with decimals. Double-check your conversions and multiplication. Step 4: Calculate the total distance still left to run. Subtract the distance completed in Part 2 from the remaining distance after Part 1. Distance still left = 10.48 miles - 2.62 miles = 7.86 miles. So, [Student Name], [they] still has 7.86 miles to run. Check: Total completed distance = 15.72 + 2.62 = 18.34 miles. Total distance = 18.34 + 7.86 = 26.2 miles. This confirms the calculations.
