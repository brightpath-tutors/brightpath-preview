Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward02543C0

Multi-Step Rational Problems are mathematical expressions or equations that require two or more operations to be performed on rational numbers. Rational numbers include fractions (like 1/2), decimals (like 0.75), and integers (like -5). To solve these, you rigorously apply the order of operations (PEMDAS/GEMDAS). Think of it like a chef following a recipe: you wouldn't bake a cake before mixing the ingredients, just as you perform operations within parentheses before multiplication. For instance, consider the expression (2/5 + 0.7) 10 - 3.5. First, convert 2/5 to 0.4. Then, inside the parentheses, 0.4 + 0.7 equals 1.1. Next, multiply 1.1 10 to get 11. Finally, subtract 11 - 3.5, which results in 7.5. [Student Name], mastering this systematic approach is key to consistently arriving at the correct solution. [they] will see these problems frequently in higher math.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)02543W0

Hello [Student Name]! Let's break down a problem step-by-step to see how multi-step rational problems work. It's like following a recipe, one instruction at a time.

Problem: Maria bought a shirt for $24.50. She also bought 3 pairs of socks, each costing $3.25. If she paid with a $50 bill, how much change did she get?

Step 1: Calculate the total cost of the socks. 💡 Hint: To find the total cost of multiple identical items, you multiply the number of items by the cost per item. Cost of socks = 3 pairs × $3.25/pair = $9.75

Step 2: Calculate Maria's total spending. 💡 Hint: Her total spending is the sum of the shirt's cost and the socks' total cost. Remember to align decimal points when adding decimals. Total spending = Cost of shirt + Cost of socks = $24.50 + $9.75 = $34.25

Step 3: Calculate the change Maria received. 💡 Hint: The change is the difference between the amount she paid and her total spending. Subtract the smaller value from the larger value. Change = Amount paid - Total spending = $50.00 - $34.25 = $15.75

So, [Student Name], Maria received $15.75 in change.

Check: We can work backward. If she received $15.75 change and spent $34.25, then $15.75 + $34.25 = $50.00, which matches the amount she paid. This confirms our calculations are correct.

Standard02543W0

Let's work through a standard multi-step problem, [Student Name]. These problems often involve fractions and decimals, so paying attention to the order of operations and units is key.

Problem: A baker used 2.5 cups of flour for a cake. Then, [they] used 1/2 of that amount for cookies. If the baker started with 8 cups of flour, how much flour is left?

Step 1: Calculate the flour used for cookies. To find out how much flour was used for cookies, we need to take half of the amount used for the cake. Flour for cookies = 1/2 × 2.5 cups = 0.5 × 2.5 cups = 1.25 cups

Step 2: Calculate the total flour used. Now, add the flour used for the cake and the cookies to find the total amount consumed. Total flour used = Flour for cake + Flour for cookies = 2.5 cups + 1.25 cups = 3.75 cups

Step 3: Calculate the remaining flour. Subtract the total flour used from the initial amount of flour the baker started with. Remaining flour = Initial flour - Total flour used = 8 cups - 3.75 cups = 4.25 cups

So, there are 4.25 cups of flour left.

Check: We can add the remaining flour to the flour used: 4.25 cups (remaining) + 3.75 cups (used) = 8 cups, which matches the starting amount. This verifies our answer.

Challenge02543W0

This challenging problem, [Student Name], requires careful reading and understanding of fractions applied to changing quantities. Pay close attention to common pitfalls!

Problem: A marathon runner completed 3/5 of a 26.2-mile race. Then, [they] ran an additional 1/4 of the remaining distance. How many miles does [they] still have to run to finish the race?

Step 1: Calculate the distance completed in the first part of the race. To find 3/5 of 26.2 miles, we multiply the fraction by the total distance. Distance completed (Part 1) = (3/5) × 26.2 = 0.6 × 26.2 = 15.72 miles. Common Error: Some might mistakenly calculate 3/5 of 26, then 3/5 of 0.2 separately, leading to errors if not careful with decimals. Always convert fractions to decimals or multiply fractions directly with decimals.

Step 2: Calculate the remaining distance after Part 1. Subtract the distance completed in Part 1 from the total race distance. Remaining distance = 26.2 miles - 15.72 miles = 10.48 miles. Common Error: Forgetting to find the remaining distance and instead using the total race distance for the next step. The problem specifically states "1/4 of the remaining distance."

Step 3: Calculate the distance completed in the second part of the race. Now, take 1/4 of the remaining distance (10.48 miles). Distance completed (Part 2) = (1/4) × 10.48 = 0.25 × 10.48 = 2.62 miles. Common Error: Incorrectly converting 1/4 to a decimal (e.g., 0.4) or making calculation errors with decimals. Double-check your conversions and multiplication.

Step 4: Calculate the total distance still left to run. Subtract the distance completed in Part 2 from the remaining distance after Part 1. Distance still left = 10.48 miles - 2.62 miles = 7.86 miles.

So, [Student Name], [they] still has 7.86 miles to run.

Check: Total completed distance = 15.72 + 2.62 = 18.34 miles. Total distance = 18.34 + 7.86 = 26.2 miles. This confirms the calculations.

Ignoring Order of Operations (PEMDAS)
Mistake:
Students often perform operations from left to right without prioritizing multiplication/division over addition/subtraction, or exponents/parentheses first, leading to incorrect results.
Why tempting:
It feels natural to compute numbers in the order they appear in the problem, rather than following a strict mathematical hierarchy.
Correction:
Always apply PEMDAS/GEMDAS. Address Parentheses/Groupings first, then Exponents, then Multiplication/Division (from left to right), and finally Addition/Subtraction (from left to right).
Misapplying Negative Distribution
Mistake:
When subtracting an expression in parentheses, [Student Name] might only apply the negative sign to the first term inside, forgetting to distribute it to all terms within the parentheses.
Why tempting:
It's easy to overlook that the negative applies to the entire quantity being subtracted, not just the initial number or fraction.
Correction:
Remember that a negative sign before parentheses means you are subtracting the entire expression. Distribute the negative sign to every term inside the parentheses before combining like terms.
Incorrect Fraction Addition/Subtraction
Mistake:
[Student Name] might incorrectly add or subtract fractions by operating on the numerators and denominators independently, rather than finding a common denominator first.
Why tempting:
Multiplying and dividing fractions involve direct operations on numerators and denominators, so students might mistakenly apply this simpler logic to addition and subtraction.
Correction:
Before adding or subtracting fractions, you must find a common denominator for all fractions involved. Convert each fraction to an equivalent fraction with this common denominator, then add or subtract only the numerators while keeping the common denominator.
Flipping the Wrong Fraction in Division
Mistake:
When dividing fractions, [Student Name] might incorrectly flip the first fraction instead of the second (divisor), or forget to flip any fraction at all.
Why tempting:
The 'Keep, Change, Flip' rule can be misremembered or applied haphazardly, leading to confusion about which fraction gets inverted.
Correction:
To divide by a fraction, 'Keep' the first fraction as it is, 'Change' the division sign to multiplication, and 'Flip' (find the reciprocal of) the second fraction (the divisor). Then multiply across the numerators and denominators.
Decimal Misalignment in Addition/Subtraction
Mistake:
[Student Name] might misalign decimal points when adding or subtracting numbers with different numbers of decimal places, causing significant place value errors.
Why tempting:
Students might mistakenly align the last digits of the numbers as if they were whole numbers, rather than ensuring the decimal points are vertically aligned.
Correction:
Always align the decimal points vertically when adding or subtracting decimals. It's helpful to add trailing zeros to the shorter decimal to make both numbers have the same number of decimal places before performing the operation.
