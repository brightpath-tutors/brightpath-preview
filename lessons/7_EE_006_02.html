Multi-Step Rational Problems

Skill ID: 7.EE.006.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
Multi-Step Rational Problems are algebraic equations that require two or more operations to solve and involve at least one rational number (a fraction or a decimal) as a coefficient or constant. Imagine you're trying to find a treasure buried under several layers of earth, [Student Name]. Each layer is an operation you need to undo. You can't skip layers; you must systematically remove them to reach your goal. For example, consider the equation (3/4)x - 1/8 = 5/16. To solve this for 'x', you first need to add 1/8 to both sides, which simplifies the equation, then multiply by the reciprocal of 3/4 to isolate 'x'. This systematic approach is crucial. Just like simplifying complex fractions before combining them, each step simplifies the problem until 'x' stands alone. [Student Name], mastering this process allows you to tackle more complex real-world scenarios.

Those were single-step rational problems where you either added/subtracted a rational number or multiplied/divided by one. Multi-step rational problems are just an extension of these skills. Think of it like a scavenger hunt: you know how to find one clue, and now you're just putting those skills together to find a series of clues. We're simply adding a few more 'clues' or steps to the equation, like (1/3)x + 2 = 7/2. [Student Name], you already have the tools to solve each part; you just need to put them in the right order. This makes these problems totally achievable, building on what you've already mastered.
Engagement — Real-World Hook
Did you know that multi-step rational problems are essential for understanding sports statistics? Imagine you're the general manager for a Major League Baseball team, [Student Name], trying to calculate a player's on-base percentage (OBP). OBP is (Hits + Walks + Hit by Pitch) / (At-Bats + Walks + Hit by Pitch + Sacrifice Flies). This isn't just theory; it's how teams analyze performance and make crucial decisions, [Student Name]. It’s a powerful skill that connects directly to the real world of professional sports!
Stability — Clear & Structured
Multi-Step Rational Problems are simply equations that require several operations to isolate the variable, where some numbers are fractions or decimals. Our goal is to systematically undo operations until the variable stands alone. First, you'll work to get all variable terms on one side and constant terms on the other. Then, you'll perform inverse operations to solve for the variable. For example, let's solve (1/2)y + 0.75 = 2. First, subtract 0.75 from both sides: (1/2)y = 2 - 0.75, so (1/2)y = 1.25. Next, multiply both sides by 2 to isolate 'y': y = 1.25 * 2, so y = 2.5. [Student Name], this predictable sequence of steps helps you solve even complex problems reliably. You'll apply this in situations like calculating the amount of ingredients needed for a recipe if you're adjusting the serving size, ensuring all measurements are correct for [their] recipe.

Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Hello [Student Name]! Let's tackle a real-world multi-step rational problem together. Imagine you're helping your school's sports booster club. They are selling raffle tickets for a new basketball hoop. They started with a box of 450 tickets. On Monday, they sold 1/3 of the tickets. On Tuesday, they sold 0.4 of the remaining tickets. Your job is to figure out how many tickets are left to sell for the big game on Friday. Here’s how we can break it down: 1. 💡 Hint: First, think about how many tickets were sold on Monday. We need to find a fraction of the total. Step 1: Calculate tickets sold on Monday. Multiply the total tickets by the fraction sold on Monday: 450 tickets (1/3) = 150 tickets. So, 150 tickets were sold. 2. *💡 Hint: Now, figure out how many tickets are left after Monday's sales. This remaining amount is what Tuesday's sales are based on. Step 2: Calculate tickets remaining after Monday. Subtract Monday's sales from the total: 450 tickets - 150 tickets = 300 tickets remaining. 3. *💡 Hint: Next, calculate how many tickets were sold on Tuesday. This is a decimal portion of the remaining tickets. Step 3: Calculate tickets sold on Tuesday. Multiply the remaining tickets by the decimal portion sold: 300 tickets 0.4 = 120 tickets. So, 120 tickets were sold on Tuesday. 4. 💡 Hint: Finally, find the total tickets left after both days of sales. Step 4: Calculate tickets left to sell. Subtract Tuesday's sales from the remaining tickets after Monday: 300 tickets - 120 tickets = 180 tickets. There are 180 tickets left to sell. To check our work, we can add all the sold tickets (150 + 120) and the remaining tickets (180): 150 + 120 + 180 = 450. This matches our starting total, so the answer is correct!
Standard
Let's work through a problem, [Student Name], involving a school bake sale. Mrs. Chen is making cookies for the fundraiser. She plans to bake 120 cookies. She uses 2/5 of the dough for chocolate chip cookies. Then, she uses 0.25 of the remaining dough for oatmeal raisin cookies. The rest of the dough is used for sugar cookies. How much dough, in terms of the total original amount, did she use for sugar cookies? Here are the steps to solve this problem: 1. Calculate dough used for chocolate chip cookies. First, find out what fraction of the total dough was used for chocolate chip cookies. This is already given as 2/5. We can express this as a decimal: 2 / 5 = 0.40. So, 0.40 of the dough is for chocolate chip. 2. Calculate the remaining dough after chocolate chip cookies. If 0.40 of the dough was used, then 1 - 0.40 = 0.60 of the dough remains. This is the base for the next step. 3. Calculate dough used for oatmeal raisin cookies. Mrs. Chen used 0.25 of the remaining dough for oatmeal raisin. So, multiply the remaining dough fraction by 0.25: 0.60 0.25 = 0.15. This means 0.15 of the original total dough was used for oatmeal raisin. 4. Calculate dough used for sugar cookies. To find the portion for sugar cookies, subtract the chocolate chip and oatmeal raisin portions from the total dough (which is 1, or 100%). 1 - 0.40 - 0.15 = 0.45. So, Mrs. Chen used 0.45 of the total original dough for sugar cookies. If we were to find the actual number of cookies, it would be 0.45 120 = 54 cookies. To check: 0.40 (chocolate chip) + 0.15 (oatmeal raisin) + 0.45 (sugar) = 1.00 (total dough). Our fractions add up to the whole, so the answer is correct.
