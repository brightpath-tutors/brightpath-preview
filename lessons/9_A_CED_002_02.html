Creating Quadratic Equations

Skill ID: 9.A-CED.002.02  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Creating Quadratic Equations involves constructing an equation of the form ax² + bx + c = 0 (or y = ax² + bx + c) based on specific conditions or real-world scenarios. Unlike linear equations, which describe straight-line relationships, quadratic equations describe parabolic relationships, often involving area, projectile motion, or optimization. The 'mechanism' is to translate verbal descriptions or geometric properties into algebraic expressions where at least one term contains a variable raised to the power of two. For example, if a rectangular garden's length is 3 feet more than its width, and its area is 108 square feet, we can let the width be 'w'. Then the length is 'w + 3'. The area formula, length × width, becomes (w + 3)w = 108. Expanding this gives w² + 3w = 108, which simplifies to w² + 3w - 108 = 0. This is a quadratic equation. Think of it like a chef building a complex dish from individual ingredients: [Student Name], you're taking separate pieces of information (ingredients) and combining them to form a cohesive mathematical structure (the dish). [Student Name] uses this skill to model problems where quantities are multiplied or squared.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Creating Quadratic Equations is the process of translating a problem's conditions into an algebraic equation where the highest power of the variable is two. This type of equation is crucial for modeling situations involving areas, projectile motion, or optimization. The structure of a quadratic equation is typically ax² + bx + c = 0, where 'a' is not zero. For example, let's say [Student Name] is building a rectangular dog run. [they] has 80 feet of fencing, and [they] wants the area of the dog run to be 300 square feet. First, define variables: let the width be 'w' feet. Substituting our expressions, we get (40 - w)w = 300. Expanding this gives 40w - w² = 300. Rearranging into standard form: -w² + 40w - 300 = 0. This is the quadratic equation [Student Name] created to solve the problem. This skill allows you to move from a descriptive problem to a solvable mathematical model.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Hey [Student Name], let's work through creating a quadratic equation from a real-world scenario. Imagine you have a rectangular garden in your backyard, and you know a couple of things about its dimensions and total area.

Problem: A rectangular garden's length is 5 feet more than its width. If the area of the garden is 300 square feet, create a quadratic equation that can be used to find the width of the garden.

  1. 💡 Hint: Define your unknown and related quantities using a variable. Let's start by assigning a variable to the width, since the length is described in relation to it. If we let the width of the garden be w feet, then the length, which is 5 feet more than the width, can be expressed as w + 5 feet.
  2. 💡 Hint: Recall the formula for the area of a rectangle and substitute your expressions. The area of a rectangle is found by multiplying its length by its width (Area = Length × Width). We are given that the area is 300 square feet. So, we can set up the equation: (w + 5) * w = 300.
  3. 💡 Hint: Distribute the multiplication and move all terms to one side to get the standard quadratic form. Now, we need to simplify and rearrange this equation into the standard quadratic form, which is ax^2 + bx + c = 0. Distribute the w on the left side: w^2 + 5w = 300.
  4. 💡 Hint: Make one side of the equation equal to zero by subtracting the constant term. To get the standard form, subtract 300 from both sides of the equation: w^2 + 5w - 300 = 0. This is the quadratic equation we need to find the width w.

Check: If w=15, then 15^2 + 5(15) - 300 = 225 + 75 - 300 = 300 - 300 = 0. So, a width of 15 feet would mean a length of 20 feet, and an area of 300 square feet. The equation correctly represents the problem.

Standard

Imagine you're framing a cherished photo, [Student Name]. You're adding a border around it, and you need to figure out how wide that border should be based on the total space it will take up.

Problem: A rectangular painting is 10 inches long and 8 inches wide. A border of uniform width x is added around the entire painting. If the total area of the painting and its border is 120 square inches, create a quadratic equation that can be used to find the width of the border x.

  1. Define new dimensions: The original painting is 10 inches long and 8 inches wide. When a border of width x is added uniformly around it, the width x is added to both sides of the length and both sides of the width. So, the new total length becomes 10 + 2x inches, and the new total width becomes 8 + 2x inches.
  2. Use the area formula: The total area of the painting with its border is given as 120 square inches. The area of this new, larger rectangle is its new length multiplied by its new width. So, we can write the equation: (10 + 2x)(8 + 2x) = 120.
  3. Expand and simplify: To get the quadratic equation in standard form, ax^2 + bx + c = 0, we need to multiply the two binomials on the left side using the FOIL method. 10 8 + 10 (2x) + (2x) 8 + (2x) (2x) = 120 80 + 20x + 16x + 4x^2 = 120 4x^2 + 36x + 80 = 120
  4. Rearrange into standard form: Subtract 120 from both sides to set the equation equal to zero: 4x^2 + 36x + 80 - 120 = 0 4x^2 + 36x - 40 = 0 This is the quadratic equation. You could also divide the entire equation by 4 to simplify it to x^2 + 9x - 10 = 0.

Check: If x=1, then 4(1)^2 + 36(1) - 40 = 4 + 36 - 40 = 40 - 40 = 0. If x=1, the new dimensions are 10+2(1)=12 and 8+2(1)=10. The area is 12 * 10 = 120. This confirms the equation is correct.

Challenge

Alright, [Student Name], let's tackle a slightly more complex problem that often pops up in real life, like when a group of friends is trying to split a cost. It requires careful setup to avoid common errors.

Problem: A group of friends is pooling money to buy a $600 gaming console. If two more friends joined the group, each person would pay $50 less. Create a quadratic equation that can be used to find the original number of friends.

  1. Define Variables and Initial Contributions: Let n be the original number of friends. Each friend initially pays 600/n dollars. Common Error: Forgetting that 'each person would pay $50 less' means comparing the per-person cost, not the total cost. Avoid It: Clearly state what each expression represents.
  2. Define New Scenario and Contributions: If two more friends join, the new number is n + 2. Each person in the larger group would pay 600/(n + 2) dollars. Common Error: Confusing n+2 as the cost or the change in cost, instead of the new number of people. Avoid It: Assign variables to people, then use those to calculate costs.
  3. Formulate the Equation based on the difference: The problem states that the new cost per friend is $50 less than the original cost per friend. This translates to: (Original cost per friend) - (New cost per friend) = 50. 600/n - 600/(n + 2) = 50. Common Error: A common mistake is to subtract 50 from n or n+2, or to confuse which cost is larger. Avoid It: Clearly write down the individual cost expressions first, then apply the "less" relationship correctly. Since the new cost is less, the original cost must be larger.
  4. Rearrange into standard quadratic form: To eliminate the denominators, multiply the entire equation by the common denominator, n(n + 2). n(n + 2) [600/n - 600/(n + 2)] = 50 n(n + 2) 600(n + 2) - 600n = 50n^2 + 100n 600n + 1200 - 600n = 50n^2 + 100n 1200 = 50n^2 + 100n Move all terms to one side to get ax^2 + bx + c = 0: 50n^2 + 100n - 1200 = 0. You can simplify by dividing by 50: n^2 + 2n - 24 = 0.

Check: If n=4, 4^2 + 2(4) - 24 = 16 + 8 - 24 = 24 - 24 = 0. Original cost per friend: 600/4 = 150. New number of friends: 4+2 = 6. New cost per friend: 600/6 = 100. Difference: 150 - 100 = 50. The equation is correct.
