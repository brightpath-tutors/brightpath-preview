Adding Linear Expressions

Skill ID: 7.EE.001.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can combine like terms to add two or more linear expressions, simplifying the result into a single expression.
Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

Adding linear expressions involves combining two or more algebraic expressions that contain variables raised to the power of one, along with constants. A linear expression is like '4x + 7' or '10y - 2'. When you add them, you're essentially finding a simpler, equivalent expression. The core mechanism is identifying and combining 'like terms'. Like terms are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power, or are just constant numbers. For example, in the expression (5x + 3) + (2x - 1), '5x' and '2x' are like terms, and '3' and '-1' are like terms. To add them, you drop the parentheses (since you're adding, the signs inside don't change), then group the like terms, and finally add their coefficients and constants. So, (5x + 3) + (2x - 1) becomes (5x + 2x) + (3 - 1), which simplifies to 7x + 2. Think of it like combining ingredients for a recipe, [Student Name]. If you have 3 cups of flour and 2 cups of sugar in one bowl, and then you add another bowl with 1 cup of flour and 4 cups of sugar, you combine the flour amounts (3+1) and the sugar amounts (2+4) separately to get your total. This ensures you only add items that are alike, just as you combine 'x' terms only with other 'x' terms, and constants only with other constants. [Student Name], [they] is applying the distributive property implicitly and then using the commutative and associative properties to rearrange and group terms before performing the final arithmetic. This systematic approach ensures accurate simplification.

Stability — Clear & Structured

Let's calmly break down the process of adding linear expressions. A linear expression is an algebraic expression where the highest power of the variable is 1, like '9p - 4' or '12q + 6'. When we add two or more of these expressions, our goal is to combine them into one simpler expression by grouping and combining 'like terms'. The process is straightforward and predictable. First, remove any parentheses. Since you are adding, the signs of the terms inside the parentheses will not change. Next, identify all the 'like terms'. Remember, these are terms with the same variable raised to the same power, or terms that are just constant numbers. Finally, combine these like terms by adding their coefficients (the numbers in front of the variables) and adding the constant terms together. For example, let's add the linear expressions (9p - 4) and (3p + 10). Step 1: Remove parentheses: 9p - 4 + 3p + 10. Step 2: Identify like terms: (9p and 3p) are like terms, and (-4 and 10) are like terms. Step 3: Combine like terms: (9p + 3p) + (-4 + 10). This simplifies to 12p + 6. [Student Name], this systematic approach ensures that [they] can consistently arrive at the correct simplified expression. Think of it as organizing [their] math workspace; [they] groups similar items together before tidying up.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Welcome, [Student Name]! Let's walk through adding linear expressions step-by-step. Imagine you're calculating the total cost for two different school supplies. One expression represents the cost for 'x' notebooks and a fixed fee, and another for 'x' pens and another fixed fee. We want to find the total combined cost. Let's solve: (4x + 7) + (2x - 3)

  1. 💡 Hint: The first step is to identify and group 'like terms'. 'Like terms' are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power, or terms that are just constant numbers. First, rewrite the expression to group the 'x' terms together and the constant terms together. Remember that adding a negative number is the same as subtracting. So, (4x + 7) + (2x - 3) becomes: 4x + 2x + 7 - 3.
  1. 💡 Hint: Now that like terms are next to each other, you can combine them. Think of 'x' as a specific item, like a 'notebook'. If you have 4 notebooks and add 2 more notebooks, how many do you have? Combine the 'x' terms: 4x + 2x = 6x. Then, combine the constant terms: 7 - 3 = 4.
  1. 💡 Hint: Put your combined like terms back together to form the simplified expression. You've done the hardest part! Your final simplified expression is 6x + 4. This new expression represents the total combined cost of 'x' notebooks and 'x' pens, plus the combined fixed fees.

To check your work, pick a simple value for 'x', like x = 1. Original expression: (4(1) + 7) + (2(1) - 3) = (4 + 7) + (2 - 3) = 11 + (-1) = 10. Simplified expression: 6(1) + 4 = 6 + 4 = 10. Since both results are 10, our answer is correct!

Standard

[Student Name], let's practice adding linear expressions. This skill is super useful, for instance, if you're trying to figure out the total distance walked by a dog walker who takes different routes, where 'y' could be the unknown length of a park loop. We need to add (-5y + 10) + (8y - 4). Here's how we solve it:

  1. Identify like terms. In this expression, -5y and 8y are 'y' terms, and 10 and -4 are constant terms. It's helpful to visualize them as different categories of items you can combine.
  1. Group the like terms together. You can mentally (or physically) rearrange the terms so that the 'y' terms are next to each other, and the constant terms are next to each other. Remember the sign in front of each term stays with that term. So, we have: -5y + 8y + 10 - 4.
  1. Combine the like terms. First, add the 'y' terms: -5y + 8y. Think of it like gaining 8 dollars after owing 5 dollars; you end up with 3 dollars. So, -5y + 8y = 3y. Next, combine the constant terms: 10 - 4 = 6.
  1. Write the simplified expression. Put the combined 'y' term and the combined constant term together to get your final answer: 3y + 6. This expression now compactly represents the total distance.

To check your answer, substitute a value for 'y', such as y = 2. Original expression: (-5(2) + 10) + (8(2) - 4) = (-10 + 10) + (16 - 4) = 0 + 12 = 12. Simplified expression: 3(2) + 6 = 6 + 6 = 12. Since both equal 12, our addition is correct.

Challenge

Alright, [Student Name], let's tackle a slightly more complex linear expression addition. This one involves decimals and three different expressions. Pay close attention to the signs! We're adding: (3.5m - 6) + (-1.5m + 9) + (4m - 2.5)

  1. Identify and Group Like Terms: Look for all terms with 'm' and all constant terms. It’s crucial to include the sign in front of each term. A common error here is to forget a negative sign or incorrectly associate it with the wrong term. For example, '-6' is a negative constant, not a '-6m'. Grouping them looks like: 3.5m - 1.5m + 4m - 6 + 9 - 2.5.
  1. Combine 'm' Terms: Add and subtract the coefficients of the 'm' terms. (3.5m - 1.5m) is 2m. Then, add 4m to that result. So, 2m + 4m = 6m. A common error is miscalculating with decimals, especially with subtractions. Double-check your arithmetic: 3.5 - 1.5 = 2, and 2 + 4 = 6.
  1. Combine Constant Terms: Now, add and subtract the constant terms: -6 + 9 - 2.5. Start with -6 + 9, which equals 3. Then, subtract 2.5 from 3. So, 3 - 2.5 = 0.5. A common error here is sign mistakes, like treating '-2.5' as '+2.5'. Remember, -6 dollars + 9 dollars means you have 3 dollars, then spending 2.50 dollars leaves you with 50 cents.
  1. Write the Final Expression: Combine your simplified 'm' term and your simplified constant term. This gives us 6m + 0.5. This expression now represents the sum of all three original linear expressions.

To check, let m = 2: Original: (3.5(2) - 6) + (-1.5(2) + 9) + (4(2) - 2.5) = (7 - 6) + (-3 + 9) + (8 - 2.5) = 1 + 6 + 5.5 = 12.5. Simplified: 6(2) + 0.5 = 12 + 0.5 = 12.5. The check confirms our answer.
