Complex Unit Rates

Skill ID: 7.RP.001.01  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective
I can calculate and interpret complex unit rates involving fractions in both the numerator and denominator to solve real-world problems.
Core Explanations (5 teaching variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward
A complex unit rate is a specific type of ratio where at least one of the quantities being compared is expressed as a fraction. Unlike simple unit rates, which compare whole numbers to a single unit, complex unit rates involve fractions (like 3/4 mile or 1/10 hour) to determine 'how much per one whole unit.' For example, if an athlete runs 3/4 of a mile in 1/10 of an hour, their complex unit rate of speed is found by dividing the distance by the time. The mechanism involves dividing fractions: (3/4 mile) ÷ (1/10 hour). To divide fractions, [Student Name] will recall that we multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction. So, (3/4) × (10/1) = 30/4, which simplifies to 7 1/2 miles per hour. This is much like simplifying a complex fraction, where a fraction exists within the numerator or denominator of a larger fraction, down to a single, simpler fraction. [Student Name], mastering this skill means you can understand rates even when the measurements aren't whole numbers, helping [them] tackle more intricate real-world scenarios.

You know that if a baker uses 12 cups of flour for 3 cakes, you can easily find the unit rate: 12 cups / 3 cakes = 4 cups per cake. That’s the core idea: figuring out 'how much per one' of something. Now, we’re simply taking that very same concept and applying it to situations where the numbers might be fractions instead of just whole numbers. Think of it like this: if the baker uses 3/4 cup of flour for 1/2 of a cake, the goal is still to find out 'how much flour per one whole cake.' You're still performing division, just with fractions. There's no new, scary math involved – you already know how to divide fractions! [they] just needs to apply that skill to a rate problem. [Student Name], you've got this because the underlying logic is exactly what you've practiced. We’re just adding a fractional twist to a familiar process, making your math superpowers even stronger!

Stability — Clear & Structured
Let’s establish a clear and steady path to understanding complex unit rates, [Student Name]. Simply put, a complex unit rate is a ratio where either the numerator, the denominator, or both, are fractions. It measures 'how much' of one quantity there is 'per one whole unit' of another quantity, even when the initial measurements involve fractions. For example, consider a sprinkler system that waters 2/5 of an acre of a school's playing field in 1/2 of an hour. To find the complex unit rate (acres per hour), we divide the amount of land watered by the time taken: (2/5 acre) ÷ (1/2 hour). Following the rules for dividing fractions, [they] would calculate (2/5) × (2/1) = 4/5 acres per hour. This means that for every full hour the sprinkler runs, it waters 4/5 of an acre. [Student Name], this skill allows you to precisely analyze and compare rates in various real-world situations, like comparing watering speeds or production rates in factories.
Bridge — Connects Prior Knowledge
Remember in 6th grade, [Student Name], when you learned about unit rates (6.RP.005)? You discovered how to find how much of one thing there is 'per one' of another. For instance, if a car travels 120 miles on 4 gallons of gas, you easily calculated the unit rate as 120 miles / 4 gallons = 30 miles per gallon. This foundational skill taught you to simplify ratios to understand a consistent rate for a single unit. It's about breaking down a bigger comparison into its most basic, understandable form. Now, let's connect that solid understanding to complex unit rates. The core idea remains precisely the same: we still want to find 'how much per one unit.' The only difference is that the initial quantities might not be whole numbers; they might be fractions. So, instead of 120 miles and 4 gallons, you might encounter 1/2 mile in 1/4 hour. The method of division to find 'per one' is identical, just applied to fractions. So, the new skill we're building upon this foundation is calculating these complex unit rates. To do this, [they] multiplies (1/2) by the reciprocal of (1/10), which is (10/1). The calculation is (1/2) × (10/1) = 10/2 = 5 inches per minute. You're simply extending your existing division and ratio skills to fractions!
Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)
Alright, [Student Name], let's break down a problem involving complex unit rates step-by-step. Imagine you're helping out at a local animal shelter here in Kansas City. Last Saturday, you helped paint a new play area. You and your team painted 3/5 of a section of the wall in 2/3 of an hour. If you continue painting at the exact same steady pace, how much of a fence section can you paint in one full hour? 1. 💡 Hint: What is the question asking? The problem wants to know how much of the wall section you can paint in one whole hour. This means we need to find the unit rate: sections painted per 1 hour. We are given a fraction of a section painted in a fraction of an hour. Equation setup: (3/5 section) ÷ (2/3 hour) 2. 💡 Hint: How do you divide fractions? To divide fractions, you keep the first fraction, change the division sign to multiplication, and flip (find the reciprocal of) the second fraction. Calculation: (3/5) × (3/2) 3. 💡 Hint: How do you multiply fractions? Multiply the numerators together, and then multiply the denominators together. Calculation: (3 × 3) / (5 × 2) = 9/10 4. 💡 Hint: What does the answer mean? The result represents the amount of wall section painted per one hour. Answer: You can paint 9/10 of a wall section in one hour. Check: If you paint 9/10 of a section in 1 hour, then in 2/3 of an hour you'd paint (9/10) × (2/3) = 18/30 = 3/5 of a section. This matches the original problem, so our answer is correct!
Standard
[Student Name], let's tackle a common scenario you might find in a bakery! A popular donut shop in Portland, Oregon, uses 3/4 of a pound of their special blueberry filling to prepare 2/5 of a batch of their gourmet blueberry donuts. To plan their ingredient orders, they need to know how many pounds of blueberry filling they need to make one whole batch of these delicious donuts. Let's find the unit rate of filling per batch. 1. Identify the quantities and the rate: We are comparing the amount of blueberry filling (in pounds) to the amount of donut batches. The rate is 'pounds of filling per batch of donuts.' We have 3/4 pound of filling for 2/5 of a batch. 2. Write the rate as a fraction: We want to find the amount of filling per one batch. So, we set up the division as (filling) ÷ (batches). Rate: (3/4 pound) / (2/5 batch) 3. Divide the fractions: To divide fractions, multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction. (3/4) ÷ (2/5) = (3/4) × (5/2) Multiply the numerators: 3 × 5 = 15 Multiply the denominators: 4 × 2 = 8 * Result: 15/8 4. Interpret the result as a unit rate: The answer, 15/8, means 15/8 pounds of filling are needed for 1 batch of donuts. This can also be expressed as a mixed number: 1 and 7/8 pounds of filling per batch. Check: If 15/8 pounds are needed for 1 batch, then for 2/5 of a batch, you would need (15/8) × (2/5) = 30/40 = 3/4 pounds. This matches the original information, confirming our answer!
Challenge
[Student Name], get ready for a trickier problem! A sprinter from California ran 5/6 of a lap around the track in 2/3 of a minute during practice. If [they] kept that exact same pace for 2 and a half minutes, how many total laps would [they] complete, and what is [their] speed in laps per minute? This problem requires two steps! 1. Find the unit rate: laps per minute. First, we need to know how many laps the sprinter completes in one minute. This is our complex unit rate calculation. Rate: (5/6 lap) ÷ (2/3 minute) Common Error: Students often forget to invert the second fraction or invert the wrong one. Remember to 'Keep, Change, Flip!' Calculation: (5/6) × (3/2) = 15/12 = 5/4 laps per minute. (Simplified) 2. Use the unit rate to find total laps in 2 and a half minutes. Now that we know the sprinter's speed in laps per minute, we can multiply that rate by the total time. Remember that 2 and a half minutes is 2.5, or 5/2 minutes. Common Error: Forgetting to convert the mixed number time (2 and a half minutes) to an improper fraction (5/2) before multiplying, or making a calculation error with decimals. Calculation: (5/4 laps/minute) × (5/2 minutes) = 25/8 laps. 3. State the final answers clearly. Answer: The sprinter's speed is 5/4 laps per minute (or 1 and 1/4 laps per minute). In 2 and a half minutes, [they] would complete 25/8 laps (or 3 and 1/8 laps). Check: If [they] ran 3 and 1/8 laps in 2.5 minutes, then the rate is (25/8) / (5/2) = (25/8) * (2/5) = 50/40 = 5/4 laps per minute. This matches our unit rate, so the calculations are correct!
