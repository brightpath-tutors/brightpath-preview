One-Step Multiplication Equations

Skill ID: 6.EE.017.03  •  [Student Name] placeholders shown in blue

Learning Objective

Core Explanations (5 variants — expand to review)
Academic — Concept-Forward

A One-Step Multiplication Equation is a mathematical statement where an unknown number is multiplied by a known number, and the entire expression is equal to a known product. Our goal is to discover that unknown number. For example, consider the equation 6 * ? = 48. Here, 6 is a known factor, 48 is the product, and ? represents the unknown factor we need to find. To solve this, we use the inverse operation: division. Multiplication and division are like two sides of the same coin – they undo each other. If multiplying 6 by ? gives us 48, then dividing 48 by 6 will reveal what ? must be. So, 48 / 6 = ?, which means ? = 8. This is similar to how a key unlocks a lock; division is the key that unlocks the value of the unknown in a multiplication equation. [Student Name], once you understand this fundamental inverse relationship, solving these equations becomes much clearer. You're simply performing the opposite action to find the missing piece, [Student Name]. This process is precise and always works.

Worked Examples
Scaffolded (Step-by-Step)

Problem: Maya is selling handmade friendship bracelets. She sells each bracelet for $4. If she earned a total of $36 this weekend, how many bracelets did she sell?

[Student Name], let's break down how to solve this one-step multiplication equation step by step. We want to find out how many bracelets Maya sold, given her earnings and the price per bracelet. This type of problem is perfect for setting up an equation where a number (the price) times an unknown (the number of bracelets) equals a total.

  1. 💡 Hint: What information do you know, and what are you trying to find? How can you write this relationship using a variable? First, let's identify what we know and what we need to discover. We know Maya sells each bracelet for $4. This is the constant amount per item. We also know she earned a total of $36. This is our grand total. What we don't know is the number of bracelets she sold. Let's use the variable b to represent this unknown quantity – the number of bracelets. Since the cost per bracelet multiplied by the number of bracelets equals the total earnings, we can set up the equation: 4b = 36 This equation clearly shows that 4 multiplied by b (the number of bracelets) equals 36.
  1. 💡 Hint: How do you "undo" multiplication to get the variable by itself? Our goal is to isolate the variable b on one side of the equation. Currently, b is being multiplied by 4. To "undo" multiplication, we use its inverse operation, which is division. To keep the equation balanced and fair, whatever operation we perform on one side of the equation, we must perform on the other side. So, we will divide both sides of the equation by 4: 4b / 4 = 36 / 4
  1. 💡 Hint: Now perform the calculation to find the value of the variable. When we divide 4b by 4, the 4s cancel out, leaving us with just b. On the right side, we perform the division: 36 divided by 4 equals 9. b = 9 This tells us that Maya sold 9 bracelets.
  1. 💡 Hint: How can you make sure your answer is correct? It's always a good idea to check your solution! To do this, substitute the value you found for b back into the original equation: 4 * 9 = 36 36 = 36 Since both sides of the equation are equal, our answer is correct! Maya indeed sold 9 bracelets to earn $36. Great job, [Student Name]!
Standard

Problem: A local bakery uses 12 pounds of flour to make one batch of their popular apple pies. If they used a total of 180 pounds of flour yesterday, how many batches of apple pies did they make?

Solving one-step multiplication equations involves finding the value of an unknown variable. Here’s how to approach this problem:

  1. Identify the variable and write the equation. We know that 12 pounds of flour are used for one batch of pies. We also know the total flour used was 180 pounds. Let's use p to represent the number of batches of apple pies. The total flour used is the amount per batch multiplied by the number of batches, so our equation is: 12p = 180
  1. Isolate the variable using the inverse operation. The variable p is being multiplied by 12. To get p by itself (isolate it), we need to perform the inverse operation of multiplication, which is division. We must divide both sides of the equation by 12 to keep it balanced. 12p / 12 = 180 / 12
  1. Perform the calculation to solve for the variable. Dividing both sides: p = 15 So, the bakery made 15 batches of apple pies.
  1. Check your solution. To ensure our answer is correct, substitute the value of p back into the original equation: 12 * 15 = 180 180 = 180 Since both sides are equal, [Student Name], our solution is correct!

Reversing the Division Order
Mistake:
[Student Name] might incorrectly divide the coefficient by the constant term, instead of the constant term by the coefficient, leading to a fractional or incorrect whole number answer.
Why tempting:
It can feel natural to divide the smaller number by the larger number, or the number appearing first by the second, regardless of variable placement.
Correction:
Remember that to isolate the variable, you must divide the number on the side without the variable by the number multiplying the variable (the coefficient).
Subtracting Instead of Dividing
Mistake:
[Student Name] might mistakenly subtract the coefficient from both sides of the equation, confusing multiplication with addition/subtraction operations.
Why tempting:
Students are sometimes more comfortable with addition and subtraction, or might confuse the form ax = b with a + x = b.
Correction:
Always use the inverse operation to isolate the variable. The inverse of multiplication is division, not subtraction.
Ignoring Negative Coefficients
Mistake:
When a coefficient is negative, [Student Name] might forget to divide by the negative sign, resulting in an incorrect sign for the variable's solution.
Why tempting:
It's easy to overlook negative signs when focusing on the numerical value, or to misapply integer division rules.
Correction:
To isolate the variable, you must divide by the entire coefficient, including its negative sign. Remember that a positive divided by a negative results in a negative.
Incorrect Decimal Coefficient Handling
Mistake:
[Student Name] might incorrectly perform division when the coefficient is a decimal, sometimes multiplying instead of dividing, or misplacing the decimal point.
Why tempting:
Dividing by decimals can be perceived as more complex than dividing by whole numbers, leading to confusion with multiplication or standard division errors.
Correction:
To solve, divide both sides by the decimal coefficient. Remember that dividing by a decimal like 0.5 is the same as multiplying by 2.
Misinterpreting Implicit Multiplication
Mistake:
[Student Name] might not recognize that a number next to a variable (e.g., 6x) implies multiplication and attempts other operations like addition or subtraction.
Why tempting:
If the multiplication symbol * is not explicitly shown, some students may not immediately identify the operation as multiplication.
Correction:
Always remember that a number written directly next to a variable indicates multiplication. Use division as the inverse operation to solve.

Strategy
Isolate the Variable
When:
Use this strategy for any one-step multiplication equation where you need to find the value of an unknown variable.
How:

Identify the number multiplying the variable (the coefficient). Perform the inverse operation, which is division, by dividing both sides of the equation by that coefficient. This leaves the variable by itself on one side.

Example:
To solve 6x = 48: Identify 6 as the coefficient. Divide both sides by 6. 6x / 6 = 48 / 6, which simplifies to x = 8.
The 'Plug and Check' Method
When:
Apply this strategy after you've solved an equation, especially for high-stakes problems or when you have extra time to verify your work.
How:

Take the value you found for the variable and substitute it back into the original equation. Perform the calculation. If both sides of the equation are equal, your answer is correct. If not, re-evaluate your solution steps.

Example:
If you solved 7m = 63 and got m = 9, check it: 7 * 9 = 63. Since 63 = 63, your solution is correct.
Word Problem Translation
When:
This strategy is essential for solving real-world problems that require setting up an equation before solving.
How:

Read the word problem carefully. Identify the unknown quantity and represent it with a variable. Look for keywords that imply multiplication (e.g., 'times,' 'per,' 'each group of'). Formulate a one-step multiplication equation, then solve it using the 'Isolate the Variable' strategy.

Example:
Problem: 'A basketball team scored 5 points for each basket. If they scored 55 points total, how many baskets did they make?' Let b be the number of baskets. Equation: 5b = 55. Solve: b = 55 / 5 = 11 baskets.
Memory Hook
Divide to Untie!
When:
This hook helps [Student Name] remember that to 'untie' or undo multiplication in an equation, [they] must always use division.
Example:
Think of 12c = 60 as '12 things tied up together making 60.' To 'untie' c and find its value, you have to divide the total (60) by the number of 'ties' (12). So, c = 60 / 12 = 5.
The Equal Exchange Scale
When:
This helps [Student Name] visualize that whatever you do to one side of an equation, you must do to the other side to keep it balanced, just like a scale.
Example:
Imagine 4k = 28 is a perfectly balanced scale. If you divide the left side (4k) by 4 to get just k, you have to perform the exact same division on the right side (28) by 4 to keep the scale level. This results in k = 28 / 4 = 7.

Parent ELI5

Hello [Parent Name]! Let's break down One-Step Multiplication Equations so [they] can feel super confident. Imagine an equation is like a balanced scale. Whatever is on one side must be equal to what's on the other. In math, we use a letter, often 'x,' to represent a 'mystery number' that makes the equation true.

Here’s how it works: 1. Understand the Goal: When you see an equation like '3 times a number equals 15' (which in math looks like 3x = 15), your goal is to figure out what that 'x' (the mystery number) is. We need to get 'x' all by itself on one side of the scale. 2. Use the Inverse Operation: To 'undo' multiplication, we use its opposite, which is division. So, if 'x' is being multiplied by 3, we need to divide by 3 to get 'x' by itself. 3. Keep the Scale Balanced: This is crucial!
